Monolith vs Microservices: Food Delivery at Two Scales

The right architecture depends on your team size, not your tech ambitions. Two companies, two stages, two correct answers.

NALSD Walkthrough • ~15 min read

The Scenario: Two Companies, Same Product

Both companies are building a food delivery platform (think Uber Eats or DoorDash). They have the same core features but radically different contexts.

DimensionCompany A (Startup)Company B (Mature)
Engineers5200
StagePre-launch MVP5 years in production
Timeline3 months to launchContinuous delivery
Orders per monthTarget: 10,000 (first month)50,000,000 (50M)
Codebase0 lines (greenfield)2,000,000 lines
Teams1 team of 520 teams of 8–12

Core Features (Both Companies)

  1. User authentication — sign up, log in, manage profile
  2. Restaurant listings — search, browse, filter by cuisine/rating/distance
  3. Menu management — restaurants manage their menus and prices
  4. Cart and checkout — add items, apply promos, place order
  5. Payment processing — charge customer, pay restaurant and driver
  6. Order tracking — real-time status updates
  7. Driver matching — assign nearest available driver to order
  8. Notifications — push notifications, SMS, email

Back-of-Envelope: Company A (5 Engineers, MVP)

Before choosing an architecture, let us calculate the engineering cost of each approach. (Reference: BOE cheat sheet.)

Traffic and Capacity

Orders: 10,000/month = ~0.004 orders/sec (≈ 0)
  Peak (dinner rush): maybe 10 orders/minute = 0.17 orders/sec

This is effectively zero load. A Raspberry Pi could handle it.
Capacity is not the concern. Engineering velocity is.

Cost of Microservices for Company A

8 features → 8 microservices (auth, restaurant, menu, cart,
  payment, tracking, matching, notifications)

Per-service infrastructure overhead:
  - CI/CD pipeline setup:           2 days
  - Dockerfile + deployment config: 1 day
  - Health checks + monitoring:     1 day
  - API contract (OpenAPI spec):    1 day
  - Error handling (retries,
    timeouts, circuit breakers):    2 days
  - Service discovery setup:        0.5 days
  - Log aggregation config:         0.5 days
  Total per service:                ~8 engineer-days

8 services × 8 days = 64 engineer-days of infrastructure work
  = 64 / 5 engineers = 12.8 working days = ~2.5 weeks

Before writing a single line of business logic, the team
spends 2.5 weeks on infrastructure for 8 services.

Total Development Time with Microservices

Infrastructure:                          2.5 weeks
Inter-service communication plumbing:    2 weeks
  (API contracts, serialization, error handling,
   distributed tracing, request correlation IDs)
Business logic (8 features):             8 weeks
  (1 week per feature with 5 engineers parallelizing)
Integration testing:                     2 weeks
  (Testing across service boundaries is hard)
Debugging distributed issues:            1-2 weeks
  (Network failures, timeouts, cascading errors)

Total: 15.5-16.5 weeks = ~4 months

Budget: 3 months. They miss the deadline by a month.

Total Development Time with Monolith

Infrastructure:                          1 week
  (One repo, one CI/CD pipeline, one Dockerfile,
   one database, one deployment)
Business logic (8 features):             8 weeks
  (Same as above — the business logic work doesn't change)
Integration testing:                     1 week
  (Everything in one process — integration tests are just
   function calls, not cross-network calls)
Debugging:                               0.5 weeks
  (Stack traces work. Step-through debugging works.
   No network boundaries to debug across.)

Total: 10.5 weeks = ~2.5 months

Budget: 3 months. They ship with 2 weeks to spare.

The microservices approach costs Company A 60% more development time than the monolith, for zero capacity benefit. At 0.004 orders/sec, the "scalability" of microservices is solving a problem that does not exist.

Back-of-Envelope: Company B (200 Engineers, Mature)

Traffic and Capacity

Orders: 50M/month
  = 50M / (30 × 86,400) = ~19 orders/sec average
  Peak (Friday dinner rush): ~100 orders/sec

Each order triggers:
  - 1 payment charge
  - 1 driver matching query
  - 3-5 status update pushes
  - 2-3 notifications (customer + driver + restaurant)

Total backend operations: 100 orders/sec × ~10 ops/order
  = 1,000 operations/sec at peak

This is moderate load — a well-tuned monolith on a couple
of beefy servers COULD handle it. But capacity is not
the bottleneck for Company B. Organizational scaling is.

The Monolith Problem at 200 Engineers

Codebase: 2,000,000 lines of code in one repository.
Teams: 20 teams, all committing to the same repo.

Daily commits: 200 engineers × 2 commits/day = 400 commits/day
Merge conflicts: with 400 commits touching shared code,
  expect 20-40 non-trivial merge conflicts per day.

Deploy pipeline:
  1. Run all tests: 2M lines → 30,000 tests → 25 minutes
  2. Build container: 5 minutes
  3. Rolling deploy to production: 15 minutes
  Total: 45 minutes per deploy

Deploy frequency:
  Risk increases with batch size. With 45-min deploys,
  teams batch changes → bigger batches → more risk.
  Typical: 1-2 deploys per day.

  With 400 commits in 1-2 deploys, each deploy is a
  "big bang" release. If anything breaks, 20 teams are
  affected. Rolling back rolls back everyone's changes.

Blast Radius

Scenario: Team Payment pushes a bug in the payment module.

In a monolith:
  - The payment code runs in the same process as everything else.
  - A memory leak in payment code consumes all server RAM.
  - The ENTIRE application crashes — not just payments.
  - Restaurant listings page goes down.
  - Driver tracking goes down.
  - No one can even BROWSE restaurants.

  Impact: 100% of features unavailable.
  Time to detect: 2-5 minutes (alerting).
  Time to rollback: 15-45 minutes (full redeploy).
  Total outage: 17-50 minutes.

  At 100 orders/sec during dinner rush:
  = 50 min × 60 sec × 100 orders = 300,000 lost orders.

For Company B, the monolith is a scaling bottleneck — not for traffic, but for the organization. 20 teams cannot independently develop, test, and deploy their features. One team's bug brings down all teams' features. Deploy fear slows everyone down.

Naive Design: The Wrong Choice for Each Company

Company A with Microservices (Wrong)

8 services, each with its own repo, CI/CD pipeline, Docker image, database (polyglot persistence!), REST/gRPC APIs for inter-service communication, and Kubernetes deployment with health checks.

Company B with Monolith (Wrong)

One massive application. All 200 engineers commit to one repo. One PostgreSQL database with 200+ tables. Every deploy requires all 30,000 tests to pass.

What Breaks: Quantified

Company A with Microservices: Velocity Collapse

Engineer time allocation with microservices:
  Infrastructure maintenance:           25%
  Debugging distributed issues:         20%
  API contract management:              15%
  Actual feature development:           40%

  5 engineers × 40% on features = 2 effective engineers

Engineer time allocation with monolith:
  Infrastructure maintenance:           10%
  Debugging:                            10%
  Actual feature development:           80%

  5 engineers × 80% on features = 4 effective engineers

Company A with microservices ships at HALF the rate of
Company A with a monolith. For a startup racing to find
product-market fit, this is often fatal.

Company B with Monolith: Organizational Gridlock

Deploy frequency:
  Desired: each team deploys independently, multiple times/day
  Reality: 1-2 monolith deploys per day, coordinated

Feature velocity per team:
  Desired: ship a feature every 1-2 weeks
  Reality: 3-4 weeks (due to merge conflicts, shared testing,
           deploy coordination, rollback risk)

Incident rate:
  Each deploy includes 200-400 commits from 20 teams.
  P(at least one bug in 400 commits) is very high.
  Result: ~30-50% of deploys cause an incident.

  With 1-2 deploys/day:
  = 0.5 incidents/day × 30 min avg recovery
  = 15 min downtime/day = 91 hours/year
  = 98.96% availability

  Target: 99.95% = 4.4 hours/year
  Gap: 91 hours vs 4.4 hours → 20x too much downtime.

Iteration: The Right Architecture for Each Company

Company A: Monolith with Clean Boundaries

Build a monolith, but structure it as modules with clear interfaces. This gives you the speed of a monolith now, with the option to extract services later if needed.

# Project structure: modular monolith
food_delivery/
├── app.py                 # Main application entry point
├── config.py              # Single config file
├── models/                # All database models (one DB)
│   ├── user.py
│   ├── restaurant.py
│   ├── order.py
│   └── payment.py
├── modules/
│   ├── auth/
│   │   ├── service.py     # Business logic
│   │   └── routes.py      # HTTP endpoints
│   ├── restaurant/
│   │   ├── service.py
│   │   └── routes.py
│   ├── order/
│   │   ├── service.py     # Calls payment.service, matching.service
│   │   └── routes.py      #   via function calls, NOT HTTP
│   ├── payment/
│   │   ├── service.py
│   │   └── routes.py
│   ├── matching/
│   │   ├── service.py
│   │   └── routes.py
│   └── notification/
│       ├── service.py
│       └── routes.py
├── Dockerfile             # ONE container
├── docker-compose.yml     # App + PostgreSQL + Redis
└── tests/                 # ALL tests in one place

Key Rules for the Modular Monolith

RuleWhy
Modules communicate through defined service interfaces (function calls) When you eventually extract a service, you replace the function call with an HTTP/gRPC call. Same interface, different transport.
No module directly accesses another module's database tables Data encapsulation. Each module owns its tables. Others go through the service interface. This is the #1 prerequisite for future extraction.
One shared database, but with schema ownership The order module owns the orders table. The payment module cannot write to it directly. This makes future DB splitting straightforward.
Deploy as one unit One Docker container, one deploy pipeline, one test suite. Maximum simplicity for 5 engineers.

Company B: Decompose Along Team Boundaries

The principle: each team owns one or two services end-to-end. Service boundaries follow organizational boundaries (Conway's Law).

Communication Strategy

New Deploy and Blast Radius Numbers

Before (monolith):
  Deploy frequency: 1-2/day (entire application)
  Blast radius: 100% of features
  Changes per deploy: 200-400 commits from 20 teams
  Incident rate: 30-50% of deploys

After (microservices):
  Deploy frequency: each service deploys 3-5 times/day
  Blast radius: only that service's features
  Changes per deploy: 5-15 commits from 1 team
  Incident rate: 5-10% of deploys (smaller batches = safer)

  If payment-service has a bug:
    - Payment processing is affected ✗
    - Restaurant browsing works ✓
    - Order tracking works ✓
    - Driver matching works ✓

  Impact: 1 feature out of 8 affected (12.5%)
  vs monolith: 100% affected.

Availability improvement:
  Monolith: 98.96% (91 hours downtime/year)
  Microservices: assuming similar incident rate per service,
    but each incident only affects 1/8 of features:
    Per-feature availability: ~99.87%
    Overall user-perceived availability: ~99.5-99.9%
    (Users can still browse even if payment is down)

The Threshold: When to Switch

There is no magic number, but there are clear signals. The transition typically happens between 20 and 50 engineers.

SignalWhat It MeansTypical Team Size
Merge conflicts are daily and painful Too many engineers in one codebase 15–30 engineers
Deploy takes >30 minutes Test suite and build are too large for one pipeline 20–40 engineers
One team's bug regularly breaks another team's feature Insufficient isolation between modules 20–50 engineers
Teams cannot deploy independently Deploy coordination overhead exceeds benefit 25–50 engineers
On-call engineers must understand the entire system Codebase too large for one person to know 30–60 engineers

The Strangler Fig Pattern: Company A does not need to rewrite their monolith when they reach 30 engineers. Instead, they extract one module at a time into a service, routing traffic to the new service while the old code remains. Over 6–12 months, the monolith shrinks as services grow around it — like a strangler fig tree growing around a host tree.

Extraction Order

Extraction order (start with the service that benefits most):

1. notification-service (month 1-2)
   - Purely asynchronous, no sync dependencies
   - Easy to extract: replace function call with Kafka event
   - Low risk: if notifications are delayed, no one loses money

2. matching-service (month 3-4)
   - CPU-intensive (geospatial algorithms)
   - Benefits from independent scaling
   - Team Matching (now 8 engineers) owns it fully

3. payment-service (month 5-6)
   - Strict compliance requirements (PCI DSS)
   - Isolation reduces audit scope
   - Security benefit: fewer engineers have access to payment code

4. Leave the rest in the monolith until there's a reason to extract.
   "Don't extract a service until the pain of keeping it in
    the monolith exceeds the pain of operating it as a service."

Key Takeaways

  • Monoliths are not bad; premature microservices are. A monolith is the correct architecture for most teams under 20 engineers. It maximizes development velocity when you need it most — finding product-market fit.
  • Microservices solve organizational problems, not technical ones. The primary benefit is independent deployment and isolated blast radius. This matters when 20+ teams are stepping on each other in a shared codebase. It does not matter for 5 engineers.
  • The threshold is 20–50 engineers. Below 20, the overhead of distributed systems exceeds the benefit. Above 50, the coordination cost of a monolith becomes the bottleneck. Between 20 and 50 is the transition zone — extract services incrementally using the Strangler Fig pattern.
  • Decompose along team boundaries (Conway's Law). Service boundaries should match organizational boundaries. If one team owns a service end-to-end, they can deploy independently without coordinating with other teams.
  • Async communication (Kafka) over sync (HTTP/gRPC) wherever possible. Async decouples services in time and failure. If notification-service is down, orders still get placed and events buffer in Kafka. Only use sync calls when the user is waiting for an immediate answer.
  • Design the monolith for future extraction. Clean module boundaries, service interfaces between modules, and data ownership make it straightforward to extract services later. You get the best of both worlds: monolith speed now, microservices flexibility later.
