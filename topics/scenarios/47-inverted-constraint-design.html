De-engineering: a HIPAA Clinic SaaS, One Small Team, Postgres-Only

Design a healthcare appointment + patient-record service for ~500 small clinics (~5 M patients, ~50 k appointments/day, a few hundred RPS peak) — HIPAA-compliant from day one, built and operated by a team of ≤ 3 engineers, with Postgres as the only datastore. The textbook NALSD answer has ~25 moving parts (15+ microservices, Kafka, Redis, Elasticsearch, an analytics warehouse, multi-region active-active, a service mesh, ~15–30 engineers, ~$50–150k/mo); under these three constraints we de-engineer it down to a 4-part shape — a modular monolith, one Postgres, a load balancer, and backups — that 3 people can run on-call, for ~$2–5k/mo.

S0. Scenario & the inverting constraints

A small-clinic SaaS: each clinic manages its provider schedules, books patient appointments, stores clinical notes and demographics, sends appointment reminders, and runs day-to-day billing. The product is back-office software, not a consumer app — usage is bursty around business hours, timezone-spread across the US, ~a few hundred RPS at peak. The interesting part is not the feature set; it's the three constraints that, taken together, forbid most of the moves the other scenario pages make. We will not build the over-engineered version (sketched in §Reference); we will start from it and de-engineer.

The three inverting constraints

  • Regulatory — HIPAA from day one. Protected health information (PHI) means: an audit log of every PHI access (who, what, when, why); encryption at rest and in transit; role-based access controls; per-tenant isolation strong enough that clinic A can never see clinic B's data; Business Associate Agreements (BAAs) with every subprocessor (the cloud provider, the email vendor); a breach-notification runbook; a documented contingency plan with backups + tested restore. HIPAA's minimum necessary standard and its addressable vs. required implementation-specification mechanism are themselves a de-engineering discipline: implement the required floor; for addressable items, either implement them or document a reasoned risk decision — you do not build a maximal security apparatus, you build the documented minimum.
  • Staffing — ≤ 3 engineers, building and operating. Every component must be something 3 people can deploy, monitor, patch, and be paged for. A 3-way on-call rotation. No component whose failure modes the team cannot reason about at 3 a.m. This forbids: a 15-service mesh, a streaming platform, a separate search cluster, an analytics warehouse, multi-region active-active, a dedicated data team, six environments.
  • Tech minimalism — Postgres is the only datastore. No Kafka, no Redis, no Elasticsearch, no separate cache, no document store. Postgres 16 absorbs: the relational data (obviously), the job queue (a table + SELECT … FOR UPDATE SKIP LOCKED), the cache (its own buffer pool + a small in-process LRU), full-text search (tsvector + a GIN index), the audit log (an append-only, monthly-partitioned table), and per-tenant isolation (Row-Level Security policies). PgBouncer is allowed — it is part of “running Postgres,” not a new system.

System class

Class: product service — user-facing CRUD over relational entities with a regulatory overlay — see functional-requirements#class-product. The FR vocabulary is user-facing capabilities; the load-bearing NFRs are not throughput or tail latency (the workload is tiny) but operational complexity (number of distinct systems to run), compliance (the HIPAA floor), and a realistic availability/durability target for a back-office clinic SaaS — not the seconds-RTO target a consumer product would pick.

Clarified requirements (cite functional-requirements.html)

  • Appointments — per clinic: list/create/reschedule/cancel appointments against a provider's availability; prevent double-booking a provider slot; send a reminder some hours before.
  • Patient records — per clinic: create/view/update patient demographics and clinical notes; search patients by name / DOB / phone within the clinic.
  • Access & audit — role-based access (front-desk vs. clinician vs. clinic admin vs. our support staff); every PHI read/write recorded in an append-only audit log with actor, target, timestamp, and purpose; a “break-glass” emergency-access override that is itself audited.
  • Billing (lightweight) — mark appointments billable; export claim-ready summaries; payment processing itself is delegated to a BAA-covered processor (out of scope).
  • Reminders / notifications — enqueue an email/SMS reminder per upcoming appointment; delivered via a BAA-covered vendor; at-least-once is acceptable (a duplicate reminder is benign).

Non-goals (named, not silently dropped): a patient-facing portal / mobile app (clinic staff are the users; patient self-service is a later product, not infra); e-prescribing / lab integrations / HL7-FHIR interop (a separate integration product); a clinical decision-support / ML layer; an analytics/BI warehouse for cross-clinic reporting (we keep the records; we do not build a dimensional model); video telehealth; payments processing (delegated to a processor under a BAA); multi-region service (single region, in a US AZ pair); sub-second SLOs of any kind; building our own KMS/HSM (we use the cloud provider's KMS under a BAA); a SIEM / dedicated security operations function (we have an audit log and runbooks, not a 24/7 SOC).

Flagged assumptions (would confirm with the customer / interviewer): (1) the BAA with the largest clinic agrees an RTO of ~1 hour and an RPO of ~minutes — back-office software, an hour of downtime during a workday is painful but not catastrophic, and there is no contract today demanding the seconds-RTO a hot active-active gives; (2) a clinic's hot working set — the patients it touches in a day plus their recent notes — is small (a clinic sees maybe a few hundred patients/day); (3) cross-clinic queries are not a product requirement (so we never need to fan a query across all clinics); (4) PHI access volume is ~a few touches per patient per day on average across the fleet; (5) the team is exactly 3 engineers and that is not changing in the next year.

The fundamental tension: the “scale it like everyone scales” reflex says microservices + Kafka + Redis + Elasticsearch + multi-region + a data team. That design is correct at Epic/Cerner scale. At ~500 clinics, ~5 M patients, a few hundred RPS, and a 3-person team, every one of those moves costs more operational complexity than the bottleneck it solves costs at our scale — and HIPAA's minimum-necessary discipline says implement the documented minimum, not the maximum. Per-call SLO & the deliberate absences

  • p99 page-load API call ≤ ~300 ms; p99 patient search ≤ ~200 ms (a tsvector GIN lookup over 5 M rows, filtered by clinic_id — a clinic has thousands of patients, not millions, so the index range is tiny).
  • No internal RPC SLOs — there is no internal RPC. No async event-bus contract — the only async work is the reminder job, polled from a Postgres table. No separate search API — search is a query against the same database the rest of the app uses.
  • Reminder delivery: enqueued at booking time with run_at = appointment_start − lead_time; the poller picks it up within ~one poll interval (a few seconds); at-least-once (a retried job may send a duplicate reminder — benign and acceptable).

Reference design — the over-engineered version we are not building

This is the “scale it like everyone scales” design — the thing the other scenario pages build toward. It is sketched here only so each de-engineering iteration has a concrete “before”. Every move in it is correct at Epic/Cerner scale; every move in it is wrong here, and the iterations say why.

Architecture (the thing we will shrink)

Why each move is correct — at a different scale — and the iteration that removes it here. Solid slate = sync request path; dashed gray = async / event / index-feed path.

  • Microservices + mesh — correct when many teams must ship independently and a service's blast radius must be contained. With a 3-person team there are no independent teams; the mesh is pure operational tax. Removed in Iter 0.
  • Per-service databases — correct when services genuinely have independent scaling/ownership. Here all the data is small, relational, and queried together; per-service DBs just multiply the things to patch. Removed in Iter 0 (collapsed) / Iter 1 (the rest).
  • Kafka event stream — correct at high event volume with many independent consumers and replay needs. ~50 k appointments/day = ~0.6 events/s; a Postgres table + a poller handles it with ~1000× headroom. Removed in Iter 1.
  • Redis (sessions + hot cache) — correct when the working set or QPS exceeds what the DB's buffer pool absorbs. Here the ~50 GB hot set fits Postgres's 64 GB buffer pool and reads are ~1 ms anyway. Removed in Iter 1.
  • Elasticsearch — correct for Google-grade relevance, fuzzy matching, aggregations over huge corpora. Here it's “find this patient by name/DOB/phone within one clinic”: tsvector + GIN over 5 M rows, clinic-scoped, is sub-100 ms. Removed in Iter 1.
  • Analytics warehouse + data team — correct when cross-tenant analytics is a product. It is a non-goal here; we keep the records (and the audit log) but build no dimensional model. Removed (never built) — not a Core iteration; it's a non-goal in S0.
  • Multi-region active-active + hot standby + 24/7 SRE — correct when seconds of downtime is a real revenue hit or a contract demand. Here a back-office clinic SaaS with no such contract is fine with a warm standby and a ~1-hour RTO. Reduced in Iter 3 (to a warm standby + runbook).

De-engineering Iteration 0 — collapse the microservices into a modular monolith

Bottleneck analysis of the reference design (what's the dominant problem at our scale)

The reference design's dominant problem is not a performance bottleneck — it's the operational-complexity NFR. A 3-person team cannot run ~12+ services + their sidecars + the mesh control plane + a per-service deploy pipeline + a per-service on-call surface. The mesh exists to give independent teams independent blast radii and independent release cadence; there are no independent teams. The microservice split exists to let services scale independently; the whole app at this scale fits a couple of mid-size instances, so there is nothing to scale independently. Evidence: #systems_to_operate ≈ 25 against a team of 3; the implied headcount (~15–30) is 5–10× the constraint. This is the constraint that forces the first removal — cite the “Majestic Monolith” (Basecamp/37signals: a small team should ship a well-modularized monolith, not a distributed system) and “You Are Not Google” (don't adopt FAANG-scale architecture for non-FAANG-scale problems).

Which constraint forced the removal & what we did instead

  • Constraint: ≤ 3 engineers can't operate ~12+ services + a mesh + per-service deploys + per-service on-call. The microservice split bought nothing here (no independent teams, nothing to scale independently) and cost everything (the operational-complexity NFR).
  • What we did instead: one deployable — a well-modularized monolith (clear module boundaries in the codebase: appointments/, records/, billing/, notifications/, auth/, search/; no module reaches into another's tables; cross-module work is a typed function call), one process, one Postgres, one deploy pipeline. Cite #04 monolith vs. microservices for the tradeoff; this is the deliberate monolith side, picked for staffing.

Bottleneck we now accept (and when it bites)

  • Accepted bottleneck: you can't scale modules independently — the whole app scales as a unit, and a runaway in one module (a search query storm, a billing-export loop) can starve the others' threads/connections in the same process. Classified loosely as lock-contention / shared-resource contention within one process.
  • When it bites: when one module's load is ~10× the rest's and growing — e.g., if patient search becomes 10× the OLTP load and is hammering the buffer pool. Revisit trigger: a sustained per-module CPU/connection imbalance → extract that one module into a service (it's already a clean package boundary, so the extraction is mechanical) — not a rewrite, a carve-out. That's a “good problem,” years away at the current trajectory.

De-engineering Iteration 1 — Postgres absorbs the queue, the cache, and search

Bottleneck analysis of the post-Iter-0 design

After Iter 0 the design is a monolith + Postgres + LB — plus Kafka, Redis, and Elasticsearch still hanging off the side. Those three are now the dominant operational-complexity bottleneck: each is a distributed system with its own failure modes (Kafka: ISR shrink, log retention, consumer-lag storms; Redis: bgsave fork OOM, eviction surprises, cluster resharding; Elasticsearch: shard rebalancing, mapping explosions, JVM heap pressure) that a 3-person team must run, patch, and be paged for — and the tech-minimalism constraint flatly forbids them. Evidence: #systems_to_operate is stuck at ~6 because of these three; each adds ~1 system that contributes ~0 product value at our scale (the volumes are 2–3 orders of magnitude below where any of them earns its keep — see the headroom facts in Essential Formulas). This is the constraint that forces the next removal — cite the “just use Postgres for everything” genre and “Choose Boring Technology” (you get ~3 innovation tokens; running Kafka + Redis + ES at this scale spends all of them on plumbing).

Which constraint forced the removal & what each replacement is — with the rate where it stops

  • Queue → Postgres table + SELECT … FOR UPDATE SKIP LOCKED. A job_queue table with a partial index on (run_at) WHERE status='ready'; a transactional outbox (the booking transaction inserts the reminder job in the same commit, so a job is never lost or duplicated relative to its trigger); a poller loop that grabs a batch with SKIP LOCKED (so multiple pollers don't fight over the same rows), processes, marks done. Why not SQS/Kafka here: SQS is another managed service (another BAA, another thing to monitor) and Kafka is a distributed log to operate — both buy ordering/replay/throughput we don't need. A single Postgres + SKIP LOCKED handles ~thousands of jobs/min comfortably; our rate is ~35/min (~50 k reminders/day). Where it stops: roughly when sustained job rate approaches ~tens of thousands/min, or when the queue table's churn (insert+update+delete per job) starts producing autovacuum lag that hurts OLTP — then you'd move the queue to its own Postgres, or finally adopt SQS. Cite #23 job scheduler for the sophisticated version (leader election, Redis sorted set) — deliberately not built here.
  • Cache → Postgres buffer pool + a tiny in-process LRU. Postgres caches its own hot pages; the ~50 GB hot set fits the 64 GB instance, so point reads are ~1 ms RAM hits anyway. The in-process LRU is just for the handful of tiny, ultra-hot, slow-changing lookups (a clinic's provider list, role definitions, feature flags) — bounded, in the app heap, no network. Why not Redis here: at a few hundred RPS over a RAM-resident working set, a Redis cluster adds a network hop, a cache-coherence problem (invalidation), and a thing to operate, for ~0 latency win. Where it stops: when the hot working set exceeds the buffer pool (read miss rate climbs, p99 reads go to disk-IO territory) or when QPS is high enough that the per-query parse/plan overhead matters — then add a read replica (still Postgres) for read scaling, and only then a cache. Cite caching.html for the read-through pattern — and the corollary: don't add a cache until the buffer pool isn't enough. Classified accepted bottleneck: a single node's memory-capacity ceiling for the hot set.
  • Search → tsvector + GIN index. A generated search_tsv tsvector column on patient (name, DOB-as-text, phone), a GIN index on it, queries filtered by clinic_id first (so the scan is over a clinic's thousands of patients, not 5 M). Why not Elasticsearch here: ES gives Google-grade relevance, fuzzy/typo tolerance, faceting, aggregations — none of which “find this patient” needs; it costs a 3-node cluster, an index-feed pipeline, mapping management, and JVM heap babysitting. tsvector over 5 M small rows, clinic-scoped, is sub-100 ms. Where it stops: when search queries get genuinely complex (cross-clinic, fuzzy, ranked-by-relevance, faceted) or when search load is ~10× OLTP and competing for the buffer pool — then add a read replica dedicated to search, or finally pull search into ES. Classified accepted bottleneck: a heavy search query competes with OLTP for the buffer pool / disk IO on the one node.

Bottleneck we now accept (and when it bites)

  • Accepted bottleneck: a single Postgres node carries everything — OLTP writes (capped at ~thousands/s with synchronous_commit=on; we use ~tens/s), the connection limit (~hundreds; PgBouncer transaction-pooling keeps us well under it), the queue churn, the search load, and the audit-log writes (Iter 2). A heavy search or a queue-churn spike competes with OLTP for the buffer pool and IO. Cite connection-saturation (mitigated by PgBouncer, accepted as a watch item), memory-capacity-bound, disk-io-bound — we are explicitly not fixing these, we are noting when they'll bite.
  • When it bites: ~10–50× the current write rate, or if search queries get complex, or if the hot set outgrows RAM. Revisit trigger (in order): (1) read p99 climbing / buffer-cache hit ratio dropping → add a Postgres read replica, route reads + search there; (2) write rate approaching ~1000/s sustained → pull the queue and/or audit log into their own Postgres; (3) search complexity exceeds tsvector → ES. Each is a single, well-understood step — the inverse of de-engineering, taken one move at a time.

De-engineering Iteration 2 — HIPAA, the minimum-necessary way (add only what's required, document what's merely addressable)

Bottleneck analysis — the regulatory floor we haven't met yet

This is the one iteration that adds — but the discipline is still de-engineering: HIPAA's Security Rule splits implementation specifications into required (you must) and addressable (you must either implement or document a reasoned risk decision and an equivalent alternative), and the minimum necessary standard says limit PHI use/disclosure to the minimum needed. So the move is “implement the required floor; for each addressable item, decide and document; build nothing maximal.” The gap after Iter 1: the design has no audit log of PHI access, no explicit per-tenant isolation enforced below the app layer, no documented encryption/BAAs/breach plan. The constraint that forces this iteration is regulatory; the constraint that shapes it (no new systems, no security team, no HSM) is the staffing + tech-minimalism pair — so everything HIPAA needs is built inside Postgres (an audit table, RLS policies) or inside the cloud platform we already use under a BAA (TDE/encrypted volumes, KMS, TLS, backups) — no SIEM, no DLP suite, no dedicated SOC. Cite cross-cutting#privacy for the standing definitions of audit / residency / retention / deletion; only the scenario-specific choices are inline.

De-engineering Iteration 3 — DR and operations, the runbook way (not the hot-standby way)

Bottleneck analysis — the contingency-plan gap

After Iter 2 the design is a monolith + one Postgres + an LB + the HIPAA data floor — but there is one Postgres in one AZ, no standby, and no tested restore. HIPAA's contingency-plan requirement (data backup + disaster recovery + emergency mode) is unmet, and a single-AZ Postgres is a single point of failure. The reference design's answer — multi-region active-active + a hot standby + a 24/7 SRE rotation — is exactly the operational complexity the staffing constraint forbids: 3 people cannot reason about cross-region replication lag, split-brain, and active-active conflict resolution at 3 a.m. So the move is the minimum contingency plan that satisfies HIPAA and the agreed BAA RTO/RPO: a warm standby (built-in Postgres streaming replication) + automated backups + a drilled restore runbook + a small on-call rotation. Cite cross-cutting#dr for the standing RPO/RTO/drill definitions; bottleneck-to-decisions#cross-region in the anti-direction — this is the decision the page declines to revisit (multi-region) because the fix costs more operational complexity than the bottleneck (an AZ loss) costs at our scale.

Deliberately not automatic: a 3-person team prefers a known, drilled, manual promote over an automatic failover that can flap or split-brain in ways they can't debug at 3 a.m.

Which constraint forced the removal & what we did instead

  • Constraint: ≤ 3 engineers + a modest budget — but HIPAA requires a contingency plan. A multi-region active-active design (the reference) means the team reasons about cross-region replication lag, conflict resolution, and split-brain — failure modes 3 people can't debug under pressure; a hot standby with automatic failover adds flapping/split-brain risk for a sub-second-RTO benefit no contract demands.
  • What we did instead: a warm standby via built-in Postgres streaming replication (one extra node, one well-understood mechanism); continuous WAL archiving + daily base backups to object storage (RPO ~minutes via PITR); a documented and drilled restore runbook (RTO ~1 hour — the agreed BAA target for a back-office clinic SaaS); and a small on-call rotation among the 3 engineers with a clear escalation path. Cite cross-cutting#dr; cite replication-decisions#single-async (single-leader async replica — the chosen topology) over #single-sync (rejected: per-write latency for durability we don't need) and #multi-leader-async (rejected: conflict resolution the team can't operate). Why streaming replication over Aurora-style multi-AZ or a hot active-active: streaming replication is in the box, one mechanism, one mental model; Aurora multi-AZ is more automatic but more magic to debug and more $; a hot active-active is the reference design's complexity. At our scale and team size, “the boring built-in thing with a drilled runbook” wins.

Bottleneck we now accept (and when it bites)

  • Accepted bottleneck: an AZ loss = ~minutes-to-~1-hour of downtime (detect + promote the standby + DNS propagation), not the seconds a hot active-active gives; and the async standby means RPO ~seconds (the replication lag) rather than zero. Classified as accepting the consequences of failure-modes#leader-loss on a manual-failover topology, and declining the cross-region fix.
  • When it bites: when a clinic's contract demands a tighter RTO (e.g., 5 minutes), or when you're at enough clinics that an hour of downtime in a workday is a material revenue/reputation hit. Revisit trigger: a contract clause or a revenue-impact threshold → promote the warm standby to a hot standby with automatic failover + multi-AZ load balancing (cuts RTO to ~minutes) — cost: +effectively the same Postgres node (it's already there, just now serving) + meaningful operational complexity (now you reason about replication lag affecting reads and split-brain detection). Impact: the HIPAA contingency-plan requirement is met and the single-AZ SPOF is removed — with one extra Postgres node (a warm standby via the database's built-in streaming replication), backups to object storage, and a runbook the team drills quarterly. The accepted cost — an AZ loss is a ~1-hour, human-in-the-loop event, not a seconds-long automatic one — is the right trade when the alternative is 3 people running a multi-region system whose failure modes they can't reason about at 3 a.m.; and the revisit trigger (a contract demanding a tighter RTO) is named, so the day someone needs hot-standby, the path is clear. This is the “runbook beats robot” bet at small team size: a drilled manual failover you understand beats an automatic one you don't.

🔀 If a top-3 clinic's contract demanded a 5-minute RTO and zero RPO
  • Iter 3 reverses one step: promote the warm standby to a hot standby with automatic failover (cuts RTO to ~minutes) and switch the primary's commit to synchronous replication to the standby (RPO → 0, at +a few ms per write). Cost: ~the same node count, +operational complexity (replication-lag-aware reads, split-brain detection). That's a re-engineering step, motivated by a contract that changed — not a day-one default.
  • If many contracts demand it, or downtime becomes a real revenue hit: go multi-region active-active — the reference design, introduced one measured bottleneck at a time. See #46 for that journey; this page is its prequel.

Probe iterations (the interviewer pulls the thread — beyond the 4 de-engineering iterations)

The de-engineering budget is spent (Reference + Iter 0–3). These probes are the threads an interviewer pulls — mostly about re-engineering (the inverse of this page), plus the regulatory and failure stress tests.

“What if you grow to 10× clinics — 5,000 clinics, 50 M patients?” — the de-engineering in reverse

You re-engineer, one measured bottleneck at a time — the exact inverse of the order this page de-engineered: (1) read replica — when the buffer-cache hit ratio drops and read p99 climbs, add a Postgres read replica (still Postgres) and route reads + patient search to it; the primary now does writes + the queue + audit (this also relieves the “heavy search competes with OLTP” bottleneck Iter 1 accepted). (2) extract search to Elasticsearch — when search queries get genuinely complex (fuzzy, ranked, cross-clinic) or search load is ~10× OLTP, feed an ES cluster from the patient table's change stream; the tsvector column stays as a fallback. (3) split out the queue/audit — when the queue's churn (or the audit log's write rate) starts producing autovacuum lag that hurts OLTP, move the job_queue table (and/or the audit_log) to its own Postgres — they're already self-contained. (4) extract the highest-load module into a service — when one module's CPU/connection share is ~10× the rest and growing, carve it out (it's a clean package boundary); now you have a 2-service system, not a monolith — and you adopt the minimal mesh-ish glue (service discovery, mTLS) for those two. (5) multi-region — when a contract or a revenue threshold demands it, the warm standby becomes a hot standby, then a second region — the reference design, reached the right way. Each step is one move with a measured trigger — this page's iterations, run backward.

“A regulator audits you tomorrow — what do you show them?”

You point at three things, all of which already exist: (1) the audit_log table — append-only, monthly-partitioned, write-once-by-privilege, with cold partitions archived to object storage; you run a query and show every PHI access (actor, clinic, resource, purpose, break-glass flag, timestamp) for any patient over any window. (2) the documented risk decisions for every addressable safeguard — encryption-at-rest (implemented), automatic logoff (implemented), the SIEM/SOC/HSM/DLP suite (not implemented; here is the reasoned decision and the equivalent alternative), per the addressable-implementation-specification mechanism. (3) the drilled restore runbook + the last quarterly drill's timing — the contingency plan, tested. Plus: the BAAs (with dates) with the cloud provider, KMS, object storage, and the email/SMS vendor; the RBAC role definitions; the encryption config (TDE, TLS, KMS envelope). The audit log, the documented decisions, and the drilled restore are the answer — this is exactly why Iter 2 built the floor and Iter 3 drilled the restore. Cite cross-cutting#privacy.

“Your one Postgres primary dies right now — walk me through it.”

On-call gets paged (the LB health checks and the app's DB-error rate both fire). Runbook step 1: confirm it's the primary, not a network blip (check the standby's replication state — is it still receiving WAL?). Step 2: pg_ctl promote the warm standby in AZ B (it's caught up to within ~seconds — that's the RPO). Step 3: repoint the app's single DB DNS/config entry to the new primary (the app re-resolves on connection error); the app comes back. Step 4: optionally spin a fresh standby in a third AZ. End to end: ~minutes-to-~1-hour (mostly detection + the human steps + DNS propagation) — the agreed RTO. The data loss is bounded by the replication lag (~seconds) plus, if the standby were also lost, point-in-time recovery from WAL archive (RPO ~minutes). This is deliberately a human-in-the-loop procedure: a 3-person team prefers a drilled manual promote over an automatic failover that can flap. Cite failure-modes#leader-loss.

“You hire 20 more engineers — now what?”

Now the reference design's moves start to pay off — but you introduce them one at a time, each justified by a measured bottleneck, exactly like a normal NALSD page (this scenario is the prequel to the others). With teams that need independent release cadence, split the monolith into services along the module boundaries you kept honest from day one. With higher event volume and consumers that need replay, the Postgres queue becomes Kafka. With a working set that outgrows the buffer pool, a read replica then Redis. With complex search, Elasticsearch. With a cross-tenant analytics product, a warehouse + a data team. With a contract demanding a tighter RTO, a hot standby then multi-region. The discipline doesn't change — you just have the headcount to operate the sophistication now, so the operational-complexity NFR loosens and the bottlenecks that were “accepted, revisit later” become “fix now.” The mistake to avoid: doing all of it at once because you can afford the people — you still introduce each move only when a number forces it.

The boring-technology budget — how many risky choices do you get?

McKinley's “Choose Boring Technology” framing: you get ~3 “innovation tokens” — new/risky technologies whose failure modes you don't yet know in your bones. Running Kafka + Redis + Elasticsearch at this scale spends all 3 tokens on plumbing — infrastructure that adds zero product value here. Spend the tokens on the product instead: maybe a clever appointment-overlap data structure, maybe a slick clinical-note editor, maybe a smart reminder-timing model — things customers see. Postgres, a modular monolith, a load balancer, and backups are boring on purpose: their failure modes are documented, the team knows them, the operational surface is small. The de-engineering iterations on this page are, in aggregate, “don't spend your innovation tokens on infrastructure you don't need at this scale” — which is the same thing as “you are not Google,” the same thing as “the majestic monolith,” the same thing as HIPAA's “minimum necessary.”

Degradation contract

When the Postgres primary fails → on-call runs the runbook: promote the warm standby (caught up to ~seconds = the RPO), repoint the app's DB DNS entry, verify; ~minutes-to-~1-hour (= the RTO); during that window the app returns 5xx (it's a back-office tool — staff retry shortly). When the warm standby fails → the primary keeps serving (no replica = degraded DR posture, alerted); spin a fresh standby. When an app instance fails → the LB health-checks it out; the other 1–2 instances absorb the load (each was <10% CPU); the queue worker on the surviving instances keeps polling. When a bad deploy ships → standard rollback (one artifact, one pipeline — redeploy the previous version); a small staging environment + a smoke test gate it (cite failure-modes#bad-deploy). When Postgres is slow (a heavy query, autovacuum, a missing index) → pg_stat_statements + pg_stat_activity name the offender; kill it / add the index; the queue worker backs off (cite failure-modes#slow-dependency). When the email/SMS vendor is down → reminder jobs retry (the job_queue row stays, attempts++, exponential backoff) — at-least-once delivery; a delayed reminder is acceptable. When a row is corrupted / a clinic offboards → point-in-time recovery to before the corruption (WAL archive), or the documented purge for offboarding (cite failure-modes#data-corruption). The invariant: the service degrades to “down for ~minutes-to-an-hour” in the worst case (an AZ loss), never to “data lost” (RPO ~minutes) and never to “PHI accessed without an audit trail” (the audit insert is in the same transaction as the access).

Operations (C6) — deliberately minimal

  • Metrics (SLIs → NFRs). A small dashboard: app request p99/p999 + 5xx rate (→ the latency NFR); Postgres — buffer-cache hit ratio (→ the “hot set fits RAM” assumption; if it drops, the read-replica trigger is near), connection count vs max_connections (→ the connection-saturation watch item), replication lag to the warm standby (→ the RPO), longest-running query, autovacuum lag, table sizes (esp. audit_log and job_queue — the split-out triggers); queue depth + oldest-ready-job age (→ reminder timeliness); audit-log write rate + monthly-partition size (→ the audit-growth trigger + an A/B-style integrity sanity check — PHI-reads should produce ~1 audit row each); break-glass-events count (should be ~0; each one alerts); backup age + last-restore-drill timestamp (→ the DR NFR). That's it — ~a dozen graphs a 3-person team actually looks at, not a wall of dashboards nobody reads.
  • Rollout. One artifact, one pipeline: build → a small staging env (one app instance + a small Postgres with anonymized data) → a smoke test → deploy to production (rolling over the 2–3 instances; the LB drains each). DB migrations: backward-compatible, applied before the code that needs them, with a one-command rollback for the code; expand-then-contract for any breaking change. A bad deploy rolls back by redeploying the previous artifact — minutes.
  • On-call. A 3-way rotation among the 3 engineers; a short, drilled runbook for the handful of real incidents (primary failover, slow Postgres, bad deploy, vendor outage); a clear escalation path (everyone can do everything, so escalation is “wake up a second person”). The drilled-restore is quarterly. There is no 24/7 SOC and no separate SRE team — deliberately; the architecture was chosen so that 3 people can run it.
  • Headroom. ~10–50× write headroom on the one Postgres node; the hot set (~50 GB) fits the 64 GB buffer pool with margin; app instances run <10% CPU (they exist for redundancy + rolling deploys). Reasons for the margin: traffic growth, a noisy day, a heavy ad-hoc query, a rolling deploy that briefly runs N−1 instances. The margin is the planning device — when it's eaten, the revisit ladder (read replica → split out queue/audit → ES) kicks in.

Real-system comparison

This is the architecture a lot of successful small SaaS companies actually run, and a few have written about it: Basecamp/37signals popularized “The Majestic Monolith” (a small team should ship a well-modularized monolith, not a microservice constellation) and the “One Person Framework” idea (the framework should let one person build and run a real product); Stack Overflow famously served enormous traffic for years on a handful of servers and a couple of SQL Server boxes — the canonical “you don't need FAANG architecture for non-FAANG scale” case; Pinboard (Maciej Cegłowski) ran a profitable bookmarking business as one person on one boring box for over a decade and wrote about why; Dan McKinley's “Choose Boring Technology” is the “innovation tokens” argument verbatim; the “You Are Not Google” / “scalability! but at what cost?” essays are the direct push-back on premature distributed-systems adoption; and the “just use Postgres” / “Postgres is enough” genre documents exactly the Iter-1 move (Postgres as queue via SKIP LOCKED, as cache via the buffer pool, as search via tsvector). On the regulatory side, HIPAA's minimum necessary standard and its addressable vs. required implementation-specification mechanism (in the Security Rule) are the source of the Iter-2 discipline — you implement the required floor and document a reasoned risk decision for everything addressable; AWS's HIPAA reference materials and the “HIPAA on a small team” write-ups show the audit-log + RLS + KMS + BAA pattern. Where this page diverges from any one of those: it fuses all three constraints (small team + Postgres-only + HIPAA) onto one premise so every de-engineering lever is exercised; in the wild a given company hits one or two of them, not all three at once — but the moves transfer unchanged. Anticipated pushbacks

Stress tests against the derivation above — depth rehearsal, not interview rehearsal. The flavor is “a senior engineer defending restraint, with receipts.”

What if you grow to 10× clinics tomorrow — doesn't this whole design fall over? (scale flip)

It doesn't fall over — it grows along a named ladder, and each rung is one move. 10× clinics = ~5,000 clinics, ~50 M patients, ~500 k appointments/day ≈ ~6/s avg, a few thousand RPS peak, ~30 GB/day of audit. First thing that bites: the buffer-cache hit ratio (the hot set may now exceed 64 GB) → bump the Postgres instance class, then add a read replica and route reads + search to it (still Postgres). Next: the job_queue churn or the audit_log write rate starts producing autovacuum lag on the OLTP node → move those self-contained tables to their own Postgres. Next: search complexity outgrows tsvector → Elasticsearch. Next: one module's load dominates → carve it out into a service. Next: a contract demands a tighter RTO → hot standby, then multi-region. That's the reference design, reached one measured bottleneck at a time — which is exactly what every other scenario page does. The minimal design isn't “the design forever”; it's “the design now, with the growth path explicit.”

Why not just use Aurora / DynamoDB / a managed everything — isn't self-managed Postgres the risky choice for a 3-person team? (alt-engine)

Use managed Postgres — the page does (the constraints table says “managed”): the cloud provider runs the OS, the patching, the backups, the failover plumbing; the team configures it and runs the app. The choice that matters isn't “self-managed vs. managed”; it's “one boring relational engine the team knows vs. five specialized systems.” Aurora is a fine managed-Postgres variant — more automatic, somewhat more expensive, and somewhat more magic to debug when something weird happens (the storage layer is opaque); for a 3-person team that values being able to reason about the system, vanilla managed Postgres is the slightly more boring choice. DynamoDB is the wrong shape: the data is highly relational (joins across patient/appointment/provider/note, RLS-style isolation, full-text search, a transactional outbox) — modeling that on a KV store is fighting the tool, and you'd still need something for search. The deeper point: every “why not X” here has the same answer — X is another thing to operate, and at this scale Postgres already does X's job with headroom; revisit when a number says otherwise. Cite storage-engines#btree (the relational/B-tree engine is the right fit for this data) and replication-decisions#single-async (the chosen DR topology).

Isn't a monolith a single point of failure — one bug takes down everything? (failure-mode probe)

A monolith deployed as 2–3 instances behind a load balancer is no more a single point of failure than 12 microservices — less, actually, because there are fewer moving parts and fewer network boundaries to fail at. A bug that crashes the process crashes one instance; the LB routes around it; you roll back the deploy (one artifact, one pipeline — minutes). A bug that doesn't crash but corrupts data is just as dangerous in microservices — arguably worse, because the corruption can leak across service boundaries via events you can't easily undo; in a monolith it's one transaction in one database with point-in-time recovery. The real SPOF in this design is the Postgres primary — which is why Iter 3 added a warm standby and a drilled failover runbook. Microservices trade “one process can fail” for “the mesh can fail, service discovery can fail, the event bus can fail, N× the deploy surface can fail” — a worse trade for a 3-person team. The module boundaries kept honest in the codebase give you the future option to split out a hot module without giving you the operational tax today. Cite failure-modes#bad-deploy.

HIPAA from day one with a 3-person team — aren't you under-investing in compliance? What if you get breached? (regulatory)

You're investing exactly what HIPAA's own framework says to: the required floor (audit log of every PHI access, encryption at rest + in transit, access controls + per-tenant isolation, BAAs, a contingency plan with tested restore, a breach-notification process) is fully implemented; for every addressable safeguard you've made and documented a reasoned risk decision (encryption-at-rest: implemented, cheap; automatic logoff: implemented, cheap; a SIEM/SOC/HSM/DLP suite: not implemented, here's the equivalent alternative and why it's proportionate at our scale) — which is what the addressable-implementation-specification mechanism explicitly permits. If you're breached: the audit log tells you exactly what PHI was accessed (so the breach-notification scope is precise, not “assume everything”); the encryption-at-rest may qualify for safe-harbor on encrypted-at-rest data; the documented risk decisions show you reasoned, not ignored; the breach runbook drives the notification. Under-investing would be not having the audit log, or having backups you never tested, or a SIEM you can't actually monitor (a checkbox, not a control). The minimum-necessary discipline isn't “do less compliance”; it's “do the controls that are real for a team your size, document the rest, and revisit when scale or an audit demands more.” Cite cross-cutting#privacy + nfr-checklist#compliance.

Where does the bill come from, and what's the one knob that moves it? (cost flip)

~$2k–5k/mo all-in, and ~half of it is the two Postgres nodes (~$720/mo: primary + warm standby) — which is the right place for the money, because the database is the durable, can't-lose-it part. The app instances (~$300–500/mo) exist for redundancy + rolling deploys, not throughput; the LB, KMS, and backups are rounding errors; the email/SMS vendor is usage-priced and small. The one knob: the warm standby. Make it a hot standby (serving reads, with automatic failover) + add multi-AZ load balancing → RTO drops from ~1 hour to ~minutes (and RPO toward 0 if you also switch to synchronous replication, at +a few ms per write) — cost: ~the same node (already provisioned, now it earns its keep) + meaningful operational complexity (replication-lag-aware reads, split-brain detection — new failure modes to drill). Worth it only past a named threshold: a clinic contract demanding a tighter RTO, or a clinic count where an hour of workday downtime is a material revenue hit. Below that, warm-standby-plus-runbook is cheaper and simpler — and simpler is a real cost a 3-person team pays in sleep and in mean-time-to-understand. Cite cost-shapes#database-heavy.

How do you roll this out from a typical “we already built three microservices and a Redis” starting point without an incident? (migration)

De-engineering is a migration like any other — strangler-fig in reverse. Step 1: stand up the modular monolith alongside the existing services, with the new modules calling into the same databases the old services own (read-mostly first); route a slice of traffic to the monolith behind a flag, watch error rates. Step 2: move write paths into the monolith module-by-module, dual-writing briefly where needed, then cut over and decommission the corresponding microservice. Step 3: collapse the per-service databases — one at a time, migrate each service's tables into the one Postgres (with a logical-replication or dump-and-load window, keeping the old DB read-only as a fallback until the monolith is proven). Step 4: fold the queue — double-publish to both the old queue and the new job_queue table, switch consumers to the table, then stop publishing to the old queue and tear it down. Step 5: fold the cache — turn off the Redis read-through path, confirm the buffer-pool hit ratio holds, decommission Redis. Step 6: fold search — build the tsvector column + GIN index, shadow-query both ES and Postgres comparing results, switch to Postgres, decommission ES. Rollback at any step: flip the flag / re-enable the old path (which you kept until the new one was proven). The whole thing is “one component at a time, old path stays until new path is proven” — no big-bang. (If you're starting fresh, you just build the minimal design directly — which is the point.)

Production is slow right now — where do you look first? (monitoring/debug)

The dashboard is small, so the decision tree is short: (1) app p99 elevated + 5xx climbing? → almost always Postgres — check pg_stat_activity for a long-running query (a missing index, a clinic with an unusually big patient set, an ad-hoc report) and pg_stat_statements for the cumulative offender; kill it / add the index. (2) buffer-cache hit ratio dropped? → the hot set may have outgrown RAM (the read-replica trigger is near) or a big scan polluted the cache; check what ran. (3) reminders late? → the queue worker — oldest-ready-job age + queue depth; is a poison job stuck (high attempts)? is the email vendor down (jobs retrying)? (4) connection count near max_connections? → PgBouncer pool exhaustion or a connection leak in the app; recycle pools, find the leak. (5) replication lag to the warm standby climbing? → the standby is struggling or the network is — alert, because the RPO is degrading; not user-visible yet. (6) everything fine but one clinic complains? → check the audit log for that clinic (was there a break-glass event? an RLS-denied storm from a misconfigured role?). Each branch maps to a degradation-contract entry and a runbook step — and there are few enough branches that a 3-person team can hold the whole tree in their heads, which is the point of keeping the design to ~4 components.

S7. Key Takeaways

  • De-engineering is a skill: match the design to the constraint, not to the conference talk. The sophisticated design (microservices + Kafka + Redis + ES + multi-region + a data team) is the right answer at Epic/Cerner scale and the wrong answer at 500 clinics with 3 engineers. The senior move is to start from the sophisticated design and remove a component per binding constraint, naming the bottleneck you accept and the trigger that would justify adding it back. Reflexive sophistication is the junior move.
  • Every removal pairs with a named accepted bottleneck and a revisit trigger — that's what makes it restraint, not laziness. Collapse to a monolith → accept “can't scale modules independently” → revisit when one module's load is ~10× the rest. Postgres absorbs the queue/cache/search → accept “one node carries everything” → revisit ladder: read replica → split out queue/audit → ES. Warm standby instead of hot → accept “AZ loss = ~1-hour outage” → revisit when a contract demands a tighter RTO. If you can't name the accepted bottleneck and the trigger, you haven't done the analysis — you've just cut corners.
  • A modern Postgres is a queue, a cache, a search engine, and a relational store — and at small scale, all four jobs together fit one node's headroom. SELECT … FOR UPDATE SKIP LOCKED + a poller is a perfectly good job queue up to ~thousands/min; the buffer pool + a tiny in-process LRU is a perfectly good cache when the hot set fits RAM; tsvector + GIN is a perfectly good search engine for “find this record” queries. Don't run Kafka + Redis + Elasticsearch when one boring database does the work — that's three more systems to patch, monitor, and be paged for, in exchange for capabilities you don't need yet.
  • HIPAA's own framework is a de-engineering discipline: implement the required floor, document a reasoned risk decision for everything addressable, build nothing maximal. The required floor — an append-only audit log of every PHI access, encryption at rest + in transit, RBAC + per-tenant isolation, BAAs, a contingency plan with tested restore — is built inside Postgres (an audit table, RLS policies) and inside the cloud platform you already use under a BAA (TDE, KMS, backups). The maximal apparatus (SIEM, SOC, HSM, DLP) is documented as addressable-with-a-risk-decision and revisited if scale or an audit demands it. Compliance done right adds zero new systems.
  • For a small team, a drilled runbook beats an automatic robot. A warm standby (built-in streaming replication) + WAL archiving + a quarterly-drilled restore runbook gives RPO ~minutes and RTO ~1 hour with one extra node and one well-understood mechanism. A hot standby with automatic failover gives seconds-RTO — and split-brain/flapping failure modes a 3-person team can't debug at 3 a.m. Pick the automatic version only past a named threshold (a contract clause, a revenue-impact line); below it, the boring manual procedure you understand is both cheaper and safer.
  • You get ~3 innovation tokens — spend them on the product, not the plumbing. Postgres, a modular monolith, a load balancer, and backups are boring on purpose: documented failure modes, a small operational surface, a team that knows them in its bones. Running a streaming platform, a cache cluster, and a search cluster at this scale spends all three tokens on infrastructure that adds zero customer-visible value. The de-engineering iterations on this page are, in aggregate, “you are not Google” + “the majestic monolith” + “choose boring technology” + “minimum necessary” — the same idea, four ways.
  • This page is the prequel to the other scenario pages. Their sophisticated designs are what this system grows into if its constraints lift — and when they do, you introduce each move one at a time, justified by a measured bottleneck, which is exactly what those pages do. The de-engineering arc here, run backward, is the re-engineering arc there. Knowing both directions — when to add a component and when to refuse one — is the actual skill.
