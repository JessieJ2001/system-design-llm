Backpressure in an IoT Telemetry Pipeline

When sensors flood your pipeline at 5x its processing capacity, how do you keep critical alerts flowing while gracefully shedding non-critical data?

NALSD Walkthrough • ~15 min read

The Scenario

You are designing a telemetry ingestion pipeline for an industrial IoT platform. The system monitors environmental conditions across a large geographic area — think weather stations, factory floor sensors, or agricultural monitoring devices.

The Fleet

  • 100,000 sensors deployed across the field.
  • Each sensor sends a reading once per second during normal operation.
  • Each reading is 200 bytes: device_id (16B), timestamp (8B), temperature (8B), humidity (8B), pressure (8B), gps_lat (8B), gps_lon (8B), plus metadata and framing overhead (~136B).

The Spike: Storm Events

When a sensor detects anomalous conditions (rapid temperature change, pressure drop, etc.), it switches to high-frequency mode: 10 readings per second. During regional weather events, tens of thousands of sensors trigger simultaneously.

  • Worst case: all 100K sensors enter storm mode.
  • Duration: 10 to 60 minutes per storm event.
  • Result: traffic jumps from 100K msgs/sec to 1M msgs/sec — a 10x spike.

The Downstream Constraint

The analytics pipeline (aggregation, anomaly detection, dashboarding) processes at a maximum of 200K msgs/sec. This is limited by database write throughput, ML inference latency, and aggregation window computation.

The Critical Requirement

Not all messages are equal. Readings where temperature exceeds a threshold (e.g., factory overheating, wildfire detection) are critical alerts that must be delivered within seconds. Non-critical readings (routine telemetry) can be delayed or even dropped during overload — but trends must still be preserved.

Before designing anything, let us calculate what happens if we simply queue the excess messages during a storm event.

Excess (queued):                800,000 msgs/sec

Queue growth in bytes:
  800,000 msgs/sec x 200 bytes = 160 MB/sec

Drain Time After Storm Ends

Once the storm ends and producers drop back to 100K msgs/sec, the consumer has spare capacity to drain the backlog:

Consumer capacity:       200,000 msgs/sec
Steady-state production: 100,000 msgs/sec
Spare capacity for drain: 100,000 msgs/sec

Drain rate:  100,000 msgs/sec x 200 bytes = 20 MB/sec

Drain time after 10-min storm:  96 GB / 20 MB/sec = 4,800 sec = 80 minutes
Drain time after 60-min storm: 576 GB / 20 MB/sec = 28,800 sec = 8 hours
The core problem: After a 60-minute storm, the queue holds 576 GB of data. It takes 8 hours to drain. Any critical alert that entered the queue during the storm will be delivered hours late. A factory overheating alert delivered 4 hours late is useless — the building already burned down.

Alert Latency Under Naive Queueing

Let us compute worst-case alert latency. Suppose a critical alert is produced at minute 59 of a 60-minute storm. It enters a queue that already has ~566 GB of data ahead of it (59 minutes of 160 MB/sec accumulation). The consumer processes at 40 MB/sec (its full capacity of 200K msgs/sec × 200 bytes).

Data ahead of alert: 59 min x 60 sec x 160 MB/sec = 566,400 MB = 566 GB
Consumer rate: 40 MB/sec (200K msgs/sec x 200 bytes, full capacity)

Time until this alert is processed (FIFO ordering):
  566,400 MB / 40 MB/sec = 14,160 sec = 3.93 hours

Total latency from production to processing: ~4 hours.

This is entirely unacceptable for time-sensitive alerts.

Naive Design

The simplest pipeline: sensors publish to a single Kafka topic, consumers read from it sequentially.

How It Works

  1. Each sensor sends readings to a Kafka producer (or an MQTT broker that forwards to Kafka).
  2. Kafka writes all messages to the telemetry-raw topic, partitioned by device_id.
  3. A consumer group reads from all partitions, processes each reading (store in time-series DB, check alert thresholds, update dashboards).

During steady state (100K msgs/sec), the consumer keeps up easily — it has 2x the required capacity. The system works fine.

What Breaks

When a storm event hits and traffic jumps to 1M msgs/sec, four things go wrong simultaneously:

1. Unbounded Queue Growth

The consumer processes at 200K msgs/sec. The remaining 800K msgs/sec pile up in Kafka. The queue grows at 160 MB/sec. Over a 60-minute storm, this accumulates 576 GB of data. Kafka can store this (it is designed for durable storage), but the growing backlog creates cascading problems.

2. Critical Alerts Buried in the Queue

Kafka topics are strictly ordered within each partition. Consumers read in order. A critical alert (factory overheating) enters the queue behind millions of routine humidity readings. The consumer has no way to skip ahead — it processes messages in the order they were written. The alert arrives hours late.

3. No Message Differentiation

All messages — critical alerts and routine telemetry — sit in the same topic. The consumer cannot distinguish between "temperature exceeded 200°C — fire risk" and "humidity is 45% — normal." It processes them with equal priority in arrival order.

4. Disk Exhaustion Risk

If the storm lasts longer than expected, or multiple storms overlap, Kafka broker disk usage grows continuously. If a broker runs out of disk space, it starts rejecting all writes — including critical alerts. Now you have lost both non-critical telemetry and the alerts that actually matter.

The fundamental issue: This design treats all data as equally important and has no mechanism to push back on producers when consumers cannot keep up. This is the absence of backpressure.

Iteration 1: Priority-Based Topic Separation

The most impactful change: stop treating all messages the same. Separate critical alerts from routine telemetry at the source.

The Idea

Instead of one Kafka topic, use two:

Producer-Side Classification

Each sensor evaluates its own reading before sending. This is a simple threshold check that costs nearly zero computation:

def send_reading(reading):
    if reading.temperature > CRITICAL_THRESHOLD:
        kafka_producer.send(topic='telemetry-critical', value=reading)
    else:
        kafka_producer.send(topic='telemetry-normal', value=reading)

Sizing the Critical Path

How much traffic does the critical topic carry? Critical alerts are rare — typically less than 1% of all readings, even during a storm. Let us be conservative and assume 1%:

During storm: 1,000,000 msgs/sec total
Critical (1%):    10,000 msgs/sec = 2 MB/sec
Normal (99%):    990,000 msgs/sec = 198 MB/sec

At 10K msgs/sec, the critical consumer has enormous headroom. Even a single consumer instance can handle this easily. We assign a dedicated consumer pool of 3 instances for redundancy.

Alert Latency Now

Critical consumer capacity: 200K msgs/sec (dedicated pool)
Critical traffic:            10K msgs/sec

Queue depth for critical topic: effectively zero (consumer is 20x faster)
Alert latency: sub-second (network + Kafka commit + processing)
Result: Critical alerts are now delivered in under a second, regardless of how backed up the normal telemetry queue is. This is the single most important improvement.

What Still Breaks

The normal telemetry topic still grows at 790K msgs/sec excess (990K in minus 200K consumer capacity). The 576 GB backlog problem remains for non-critical data. If the analytics team needs recent data for dashboards, they are still looking at hours-old information during storms.

Iteration 2: Load Shedding for Non-Critical Telemetry

With critical alerts on a fast path, we now address the growing backlog of normal telemetry. The key insight: during a storm, we do not need every single routine reading. We need trends. Three strategies, each with different tradeoffs:

Strategy A: Sampling

During overload, keep only every Nth reading. This preserves the shape of trends while reducing volume.

class AdaptiveSampler:
    def __init__(self, consumer_capacity=200_000):
        self.capacity = consumer_capacity
        self.sample_rate = 1.0  # keep everything by default

    def update_rate(self, current_ingress_rate):
        """Called periodically (e.g., every 5 seconds)."""
        if current_ingress_rate > self.capacity:
            # Sample down to match consumer capacity
            self.sample_rate = self.capacity / current_ingress_rate
        else:
            self.sample_rate = 1.0

    def should_keep(self, reading):
        # Deterministic sampling: hash device_id so the same
        # devices are always sampled (preserves per-device trends)
        return (hash(reading.device_id) % 1000) < (self.sample_rate * 1000)

Sampling Math

With a storm rate of 990K normal msgs/sec and consumer capacity of 200K msgs/sec:

Sample rate = 200,000 / 990,000 = ~0.20 (keep 20%)

Effective throughput after sampling:
  990,000 x 0.20 = 198,000 msgs/sec

Consumer can handle 200,000 msgs/sec. Queue stays flat.

Data loss: 80% of non-critical readings during storm.
Trend preservation: Yes (20% sample captures shape of curves).
Per-device coverage: ~20,000 of 100,000 devices have full data.

Strategy B: Edge Aggregation

Instead of the pipeline sampling (discarding individual readings), push aggregation to the sensors themselves. During overload, sensors batch 10 readings into a single summary:

Normal mode: send 1 reading/sec (raw values)
Storm mode:  send 1 summary/sec (min, max, avg of last 10 readings)

Each summary is slightly larger (~300 bytes) but replaces 10 x 200B = 2000B.
Compression ratio: ~6.7x

Storm traffic with aggregation:
  100,000 sensors x 1 summary/sec = 100,000 msgs/sec x 300B = 30 MB/sec

Consumer capacity: 200,000 msgs/sec. Massive headroom.
Edge aggregation advantage: No data loss. Every reading contributes to the min/max/avg summary. The consumer gets 100% coverage of all devices with reduced granularity (10-second resolution instead of per-second).

Strategy C: Bounded Queue with Drop-Oldest

Configure the normal telemetry queue with a maximum size. When full, drop the oldest non-critical messages. This ensures the consumer always processes the most recent data.

Queue max size: 10,000,000 messages = 2 GB
At 790K excess msgs/sec, the queue fills in:
  10,000,000 / 790,000 = ~12.7 seconds

After that, every new message evicts the oldest message.
Consumer always sees data at most ~50 seconds old:
  10,000,000 / 200,000 msgs/sec = 50 seconds of data in queue

In practice, you combine strategies. Use edge aggregation as the primary mechanism (requires sensor firmware support). Use sampling as a fallback for sensors that cannot aggregate. Use a bounded queue as a safety net to prevent disk exhaustion regardless.

Iteration 3: Reactive Backpressure Signaling

So far, our load shedding is based on the producer knowing the current ingress rate or the pipeline making local decisions. But the most effective backpressure is end-to-end: the consumer tells the producer to slow down.

What Is Backpressure?

Backpressure is a mechanism where a slow component in a pipeline signals upstream components to reduce the rate of incoming work. The concept comes directly from fluid dynamics — when a pipe narrows, pressure builds up behind the constriction.

The most familiar example is TCP flow control. The receiver advertises a "window size" telling the sender how much data it can accept. If the receiver is slow, the window shrinks, and the sender transmits less. This is the same idea, applied to our telemetry pipeline.

The Control Loop

1. Consumer monitors its own lag:
   lag = (newest offset in Kafka) - (last committed offset)
   lag_seconds = lag / consumer_throughput

2. If lag_seconds > 5 minutes:
   Publish message to "control" topic:
   { "action": "SLOW_DOWN", "target_rate": 1, "reason": "consumer_lag" }

3. Sensors subscribe to the control topic.
   On receiving SLOW_DOWN:
   - Switch to aggregation mode (1 summary/sec instead of 10 raw/sec)
   - Or reduce sending rate to 1 reading every 5 seconds

4. When lag drops below 1 minute:
   Publish: { "action": "RESUME_NORMAL" }
   Sensors return to normal reporting rates.

The Backpressure Signal Flow

Why Not Just Use TCP Flow Control?

TCP flow control operates at the connection level between the sensor and the Kafka broker. It can slow down the sensor's TCP writes, but this creates problems:

  • The sensor's send buffer fills up, and the sensor itself must decide what to do (block? drop?). TCP does not understand message priority.
  • Kafka brokers accept writes quickly (they are fast at appending to the log). The bottleneck is the consumer, which is downstream of Kafka. TCP backpressure between sensor and broker does not help with a slow consumer.

Application-level backpressure is necessary because the system needs to make semantic decisions: which messages to keep, which to aggregate, which to drop.

Stability: Avoiding Oscillation

A naive control loop can oscillate: lag is high → tell sensors to slow down → lag drops → tell sensors to resume → lag spikes again → repeat. This is the classic control theory problem.

class BackpressureController:
    def __init__(self):
        self.high_watermark = 300   # 5 minutes of lag
        self.low_watermark = 60     # 1 minute of lag
        self.state = "NORMAL"
        self.cooldown_until = 0     # prevent rapid toggling

    def evaluate(self, lag_seconds, current_time):
        if current_time < self.cooldown_until:
            return None  # still in cooldown, no action

        if self.state == "NORMAL" and lag_seconds > self.high_watermark:
            self.state = "THROTTLED"
            self.cooldown_until = current_time + 60  # 60-sec cooldown
            return "SLOW_DOWN"

        if self.state == "THROTTLED" and lag_seconds < self.low_watermark:
            self.state = "NORMAL"
            self.cooldown_until = current_time + 60
            return "RESUME_NORMAL"

        return None  # no state change

Key stability mechanisms:

  • Hysteresis (high/low watermarks): Trigger SLOW_DOWN at 5 minutes of lag, but only resume at 1 minute. The gap between thresholds prevents rapid toggling.
  • Cooldown period: After any state change, wait at least 60 seconds before allowing another change. This gives the system time to respond.
  • Gradual recovery: Instead of an instant RESUME_NORMAL, gradually increase the allowed rate (e.g., +20% every 30 seconds) to avoid re-spiking.

Storm Event Behavior

     Ingress jumps to 1M msgs/sec.

T=0 to T+12s: Queue fills rapidly. Consumer lag grows.

T+12s: Backpressure controller detects lag > 5 min threshold.
       Publishes SLOW_DOWN to control topic.

T+15s: Sensors receive signal, switch to aggregation mode.
       Ingress drops to ~100K msgs/sec (aggregated summaries).
       Plus ~10K critical msgs/sec on fast path.

T+15s onward: Consumer drains the 12-second backlog (~9.6M messages).
       Drain time: 9.6M / (200K - 100K) = 96 seconds.
       Steady-state reached at ~T+2min.

T+60min: Storm ends. Controller publishes RESUME_NORMAL.
         Sensors return to 1 reading/sec.

Critical alert latency throughout: <1 second.
Normal telemetry latency: ~2 minutes during initial spike,
  then near real-time (aggregated) for remainder of storm.

What If Backpressure Signal Is Delayed?

The bounded queue acts as a safety net. If sensors take 30 seconds to respond to the SLOW_DOWN signal:

30 seconds at 790K excess msgs/sec = 23.7M messages = 4.7 GB
Bounded queue cap: 10M messages = 2 GB

Queue fills at ~12.7 seconds. After that, drop-oldest kicks in.
Consumer always processes recent data (at most ~50 seconds old).
Some non-critical data is lost (acceptable by design).
Critical data is unaffected (separate topic).

Key Takeaways

  1. Separate by priority first. The single highest-impact change is putting critical messages on a dedicated fast path. No amount of queue management helps if alerts are mixed in with bulk data.
  2. Backpressure must be end-to-end. Local buffers only delay the problem. True backpressure signals flow from the slowest consumer back to the original producers, just like TCP flow control.
  3. Load shedding is a feature, not a failure. Deliberately dropping or aggregating non-critical data during overload is a design choice that preserves system stability. Keeping everything and drowning is worse.
  4. Defense in depth. No single mechanism is sufficient. Priority separation handles differentiation. Edge aggregation handles volume reduction. Bounded queues handle safety. Backpressure handles sustained overload. Each layer catches what the previous layer misses.
  5. Do the math first. The BOE calculation showed that naive queueing leads to 8-hour drain times and 4-hour alert latency. This immediately rules out the "just add more Kafka storage" approach and forces a design that addresses the root cause.
  6. Hysteresis prevents oscillation. Any control loop needs separate thresholds for triggering and releasing (high/low watermarks), plus a cooldown period. Without this, the system flaps between throttled and normal states.
