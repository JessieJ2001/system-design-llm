Designing a GPU Cluster Scheduler for ML Training

An internal ML platform's scheduler: assign training jobs — each requesting K GPUs for T hours, with a priority and a gang constraint — to a fleet of 50 k H100 GPUs across 12 zones, minimizing fragmentation, with up to 5 k jobs queued at peak, a 95% effective-utilization target, and a placement decision in <2 s p99. We start from the LC #253 "Meeting Rooms II" structure lifted directly — a min-heap of GPU free-times; on each queued job, pop K earliest-free GPUs, first-fit, ignore topology, no gang semantics — which scatters a 1024-GPU gang across 30 racks and 5 zones (so it trains at a fraction of packed throughput), strands GPUs in half-allocated jobs (and deadlocks first-fit under contention), and re-scans 50 k GPUs × 5 k jobs in one process — and iterate to a topology-aware, gang-atomic, preemption-capable, cell-based hierarchical scheduler with speculative cross-cell placement, background defragmentation, and admission backpressure. The twist that makes this page different from the generic resource scheduler (#29): rooms are not interchangeable — placement quality is decided by which GPUs (same NVLink domain? same rail? same rack? same zone?), and the bill being optimized is the managed resource's utilization, not the scheduler's own infra cost.

NALSD Walkthrough • ~55 min read • LC #253 (Meeting Rooms II)

LC kernel. Meeting Rooms II (LC #253 / #252). Textbook structure: sort meetings by start time; keep a min-heap of end-times of rooms currently in use; for each meeting, if the heap's smallest end-time ≤ this meeting's start, pop it (that room frees up and is reused), otherwise allocate a new room; push this meeting's end-time. The heap size at any moment is the number of concurrent rooms. O(n log n). Lift it to GPUs: rooms → GPUs, end-times → "GPU free at time t", a meeting → a job wanting K GPUs for T hours — pop the K GPUs with the earliest free-times, assign, push back with free-time now+T. Two properties that break at 50 k GPUs: (1) rooms are interchangeable — in the LC problem any free room is as good as any other; on a GPU fleet a placement is a subtree cover over a NVLink-domain / rail / rack / zone topology, and a 1024-GPU gang scattered across 30 racks in 5 zones trains at maybe 30–50% of the throughput of one packed into adjacent racks on the same rails — the heap's "earliest free K" is the worst placement by locality; (2) requests are singletons — one meeting wants one room; a gang job wants all K GPUs to start together or none, partial allocation is wasted (idle GPUs you're paying for), and naive first-fit deadlocks under contention (job A holds 600, job B holds 400, both want 1024, neither yields). The C8 production cousin replaces the bare min-heap with a topology-aware best-fit placer (a tree search that picks the tightest enclosing locality tier that fits the gang, packing to leave big contiguous holes), wrapped in all-or-nothing gang reservations with priority preemption, run by a cell-based hierarchical scheduler (so no single process re-scans the whole fleet), with speculative cross-cell placement for the long pole of a big gang. (LC numbers it #253; some sources #252 for the count-only "Meeting Rooms" — same min-heap.)

S0. Scenario & Requirements

An internal ML platform owns a fleet of 50 k H100 GPUs — 8 GPUs per server, NVLink/NVSwitch full-mesh inside a server — spread across ~12 availability zones, with servers grouped into racks and racks wired into a rail-optimized InfiniBand fabric (the i-th GPU of every server in a pod connects to rail-leaf-switch i, so the i-th GPUs across a pod share a rail). Researchers and production pipelines submit training jobs: "give me K GPUs for ~T hours, at priority p, as a gang (all K together or none)." K ranges from 8 (one server) to 1024 (a 128-server gang), in powers of two; T from minutes to weeks; up to 5 k jobs queued at peak. The scheduler decides which GPUs each job gets, when, and (if the fleet is full) who gets preempted — jobs checkpoint, so a preemption costs a checkpoint+restart from the last checkpoint, not the whole run. The headline tension: the GPU fleet costs roughly $876 M/year of capacity, so fragmentation is the cost — running at 95% effective utilization vs. 80% is ~$130 M/year — but "effective" is not "allocated": a 1024-GPU gang scattered across the fabric burns the GPUs and produces a fraction of the training progress, so the scheduler must place gangs topology-tight, atomically, fast (<2 s p99), and at scale (one process can't re-evaluate 50 k GPUs × 5 k jobs every cycle).

System class

Class: resource scheduler / placement service — see functional-requirements.html. It takes a stream of resource requests against a fixed pool, decides placements (and preemptions) honoring constraints and priority, maintains an allocation map, and emits start/stop commands; it is not a workload manager (it doesn't run the training itself — the node agents and the training framework do that), and it is not a generic VM scheduler (#29) — the resources are not interchangeable, so every placement formula is topology-weighted. The scheduler framing puts placement quality (fragmentation), decision latency, gang atomicity, and the hierarchical-fleet scaling problem at the centre; the algorithmic-kernel framing brings the C8 min-heap→topology-best-fit swap.

Functional requirements (cite functional-requirements.html)

  • Submit a job. Accept (gpus_requested K, est_hours T, priority, gang=true, topology_constraint?, team); return a job_id and a queue position; the job is queued until placed.
  • Place a gang. Assign the job a set of exactly K GPUs — all at once (gang semantics) — choosing GPUs to minimize fragmentation and maximize the job's interconnect locality (NVLink domain > rack > rail/pod > zone > cross-zone). Once placed, emit a start command to the node agents holding those GPUs.
  • Honor priority & preempt. When the fleet can't host a higher-priority queued job but a lower-priority running job's GPUs would let it fit (in an acceptable topology), preempt the lower-priority job: signal it to checkpoint, reclaim its GPUs, place the high-priority job, re-queue the preempted job at the head of its priority tier.
  • Free on completion / cancel. When a job finishes (or is cancelled, or its node agents report it dead), reclaim its GPUs into the free pool.
  • Report state. A job's status (queued / running / preempted / done / failed) and, for queued jobs, an estimated wait; a fleet view (per-zone/-rack free GPUs, fragmentation, utilization). Operators can drain a cell/rack for maintenance, set per-team priority quotas.

Out of scope (named, not silently dropped): running / monitoring the training job itself (the node agents + the training framework own that — we only place and preempt); the checkpoint storage system (we trigger a checkpoint and trust the framework's checkpoint to object storage; sizing that is its own scenario); job autoscaling / elastic training (a job's K is fixed at submit time; elastic jobs that grow/shrink are a probe); cross-org capacity markets / chargeback billing (the priority tiers are an internal proxy for it); the inference fleet (a separate, different-shaped scheduler — #34 is the serving side). Flagged assumptions (would confirm with the interviewer): (1) jobs checkpoint — a preempted job loses at most one checkpoint-interval of progress, not the whole run; (2) K ≤ 1024 < one zone's GPU count (~4,160), so no single gang spans a zone — multi-zone gangs are a probe; (3) the fleet is homogeneous H100 — a mixed H100/A100 fleet (placement constraints) is a probe; (4) "95% utilization" means effective (throughput-weighted) GPU-hours, not just allocated GPU-hours — a scattered gang counts toward allocation but discounts toward effective; (5) job runtimes T are estimates, not contracts — a job can overrun (and gets preempted by a higher-priority one if it does), and the scheduler doesn't rely on T for correctness, only for queue-wait estimates and packing hints.

  • 34-llm-inference-serving.html — the serving side of the same GPU fleet: many small, latency-bound, short-lived requests, batched on a handful of GPUs — the opposite shape (this page: few large, throughput-bound, hours-long gangs). The fundamental tension: the GPU fleet is the cost (~$876 M/year of capacity), so fragmentation is money — but the scheduler can't just "allocate 95% of GPUs," because a gang scattered across the fabric burns GPUs and trains at a fraction of packed throughput. So it must place gangs topology-tight (or queue them — never half-allocated), atomically (gang semantics, or first-fit deadlocks under contention), fast (<2 s p99, incl. the preemption search), and at scale (one process can't re-evaluate 50 k GPUs × 5 k jobs every cycle — partition into cells). Every iteration closes one of those, and the cost story is unusual: the thing being optimized is the managed resource's utilization, and the scheduler itself is a rounding error on the bill.

ID scheme (cite identifier-schemes.html): job_id = a KSUID — time-sortable (jobs are listed newest-first; the queue's tie-break is submit-time, which the id encodes), forgery-resistant, generated client-side without a coordinator (the submit API doesn't need a sequence service on the hot path). gpu_addr = a hierarchical natural address (zone, rack, server, gpu_idx) — not a random id, because the address must encode the topology so the placer can compute locality from the id alone (same prefix → same enclosing tier) with zero lookups; it's fixed at hardware-install time and never changes. cell_id = a small stable string aligned to a topology boundary. Rejected: a flat opaque gpu_id (a UUID) — then every locality check needs a topology-lookup table, and the placer's inner loop is the hottest path there is; a monotonic job_seq from a sequence service — a coordinator on the submit path for no benefit (the queue order is priority-then-time, derivable from the KSUID). preemption_id = KSUID, time-sortable (the preemption ledger is read chronologically).

Per-endpoint SLO

  • POST /v1/jobs — p99 ≤ 100 ms to 202 (just "appended to the queue"; the placement happens asynchronously).
  • Placement decision (scheduler picks up a job → decision: assign GPUs, or queue-with-reason, including the preemption search) — p99 ≤ 2 s (the headline SLO; drives the cell-size cap and the per-cell scheduler headroom).
  • Production-tier queue wait (submitted → running, preempting if needed) — p99 ≤ 5 min; research-tier — best-effort, hours; the queue depth stays bounded under sustained load (admission control, Iter 3).
  • GET /v1/jobs/{id}, GET /v1/fleet — p99 ≤ 200 ms (a read of the job store / a cached fleet snapshot).
  • POST /v1/cells/{id}:drain — LRO; the 202 is immediate; the drain itself completes within the requested timeout or reports which jobs it couldn't move.

S3. Back-of-Envelope — the ratios that govern every iteration

Four numbers shape the whole arc. No optimization technique appears here — topology-aware best-fit, gang atomicity, preemption, cells, and hedging are named as future moves but are not used in any calculation below.

The fleet IS the cost

capacity_$/yr = 50,000 GPUs x $2/GPU-hr x 8,760 hr/yr ~= $876 M/yr  (~$73 M/mo)  (internal amortized; ~$5B/yr at cloud on-demand)
=> each percentage point of EFFECTIVE utilization on this fleet is worth ~$8.76 M/yr.
=> running at 80% effective vs 95% effective = 15 pts = ~$130 M/yr  -- or, equivalently: at 80% you'd need
   50,000 x (95/80) ~= 59,400 GPUs to do the same useful work as a 95%-utilized 50,000-GPU fleet -> ~9,400 extra GPUs
   you didn't have to buy ~= ~$165 M/yr. EITHER framing: fragmentation is tens of $M/year. (cost-shapes#ml-training:
   the bill is GPU-hours; fragmentation IS the cost.)

The topology penalty (why "allocated" ≠ "effective")

A training step on a K-GPU gang = compute + an all-reduce of the gradients over the K GPUs.
For a comm-heavy model the all-reduce time is gated by the SLOWEST link in the gang's communication path.
  8-GPU gang, one server (NVLink ~900 GB/s)        -> all-reduce ~free                  -> throughput ~1.0x
  1024-GPU gang, packed in ~16 adjacent racks on
     shared rails (intra-zone fabric ~25-50 GB/s)  -> all-reduce noticeable but ~tier-3  -> throughput ~0.7-0.85x
  1024-GPU gang, SCATTERED across 30 racks in 5
     zones (cross-zone DCI ~few GB/s)              -> all-reduce dominates the step       -> throughput ~0.3-0.5x
=> a scattered 1024-GPU gang BURNS 1024 GPUs and produces ~1/2 to 1/3 the training progress of a packed one.
   It counts as 1024 "allocated" GPUs but only ~400-500 "effective". If half the fleet's GPU-hours go to large gangs
   and they're all scattered: avg_topology_eff ~= 0.5x1.0 + 0.5x0.4 ~= 0.7  -> effective_util tanks even at 95% allocation.
   The fix (Iteration 0) is topology-aware placement: pack gangs into the tightest tier that fits.

The scan — and why the scheduler is a rounding error

A single scheduler that, each cycle, ranks 5,000 queued jobs by priority and, for each, evaluates placement quality
(even topology-blind: which K GPUs?) against the 50,000-GPU fleet -> ~5,000 x 50,000 = ~2.5 x 10^8 placement-quality
evals per pass; at ~10 ns each -> ~2.5 s per full pass, and it grows ~linearly with both the fleet and the queue.
Add the preemption search (for a job that doesn't fit: which lower-priority running jobs to evict? -> consider ~hundreds
of candidates, recompute placement after each candidate set) and the per-decision cost for a big job balloons -> blows
the 2-s p99 decisively (Iteration 1's regression -> Iteration 2's fix). Partition into N cells: per-cell scan
~= (50,000/N) x (5,000/N) = ~2.5 x 10^8 / N^2 -> at N=12, ~1.7 x 10^6 evals/pass ~= ~17 ms. p99 < 2 s with 100x margin.

Meanwhile the SCHEDULER FLEET itself: ~12 cell schedulers x 2 (primary + warm standby) + ~2-3 routers + 3-5 etcd
~= ~30 c7i.4xlarge x $0.71/hr x 720 ~= ~$15 k/mo ~= ~$0.18 M/yr ~= 0.02% of the $876 M/yr it manages.

S4. Naive Architecture — the simplest thing that meets the FRs

One scheduler process. A min-heap of GPU free-times — the LC #253 "Meeting Rooms II" structure, exactly: a heap of 50 k entries, each (gpu_addr, free_at) keyed by free_at (= now if the GPU is idle, or the estimated finish time of the job using it). On each scheduling cycle: sort the queued jobs by priority (then submit time); for each job in order, requesting K GPUs — pop the K entries with the earliest free_at (if the K-th smallest free_at ≤ now, the gang can start now; if not, the job "is scheduled to start at" that time and we move on); assign those K GPUs to the job; push them back with free_at = start + T; emit a start command. First-fit, by free-time only — no topology, no gang atomicity (a job that gets only 600 of its 1024 just... holds 600 and waits, GPUs idle). Rules: priority-order the queue, that's it. One machine; the allocation map is a hash in its RAM, with a periodic dump to disk. It satisfies every FR — submit, place, priority, free, report. The numbers tell us how far short of the NFRs it falls.

Numbers (the gap)

Effective utilization (the headline failure):
  - Topology-blind first-fit: the heap's "K earliest-free" is, by construction, whatever GPUs happen to free up first --
    scattered across racks, rails, and zones. A 1024-GPU gang routinely lands on ~20-40 racks across 3-6 zones.
  - For a comm-heavy model, all-reduce over a cross-zone-scattered gang -> ~0.3-0.5x throughput (BOE: topology penalty).

Why it's broken (per failing NFR)

  • Effective utilization — FAILS, hard. ~56% effective vs 95% target — the LC heap's "K earliest-free GPUs" is, by topology, the worst placement: a scattered 1024-gang trains at ~0.4×, and partial holds strand another ~15% idle. This is the shape-determining failure: the whole premise is "fragmentation is the cost," and a topology-blind, gang-blind scheduler is maximally fragmenting. Classified hot-partition-adjacent in spirit (the resource has internal structure the naive design ignores), but the precise framing is "bin-packing without the locality dimension" — the first move (Iteration 0) is topology-aware best-fit. Cite cost-shapes#ml-training (the bill is GPU-hours; fragmentation IS the cost).
  • Priority SLA / throughput — FAILS. No gang atomicity → first-fit deadlocks under contention (A holds 600, B holds 400, both want 1024) → the queue grows, production jobs starve behind stuck big jobs. At 5 k queued jobs this is the steady state.
  • Availability / safety — FAILS. One process, one machine: its loss stops all placements/preemptions/reclaims; restart is a stale-dump-plus-rebuild taking minutes; no fence → possible double-allocation; orphaned reservations accumulate.
  • Placement decision latency — borderline, becomes binding soon. The heap ops are fast, but a job stuck unable to fill its gang has unbounded time-to-placed under contention, and the per-cycle compute grows linearly with the fleet — and the moment Iteration 0 adds a topology tree-walk per placement and Iteration 1 adds a preemption-candidate search, it goes over 2 s p99 decisively. Classified coordinator-bottleneck — one process evaluating the whole fleet against the whole queue.

Cost of naive: the scheduler itself is one box (~$0.01 M/yr) — trivially cheap. The catastrophe is the wasted GPU capacity: at ~56% effective utilization, ~$340–380 M/year of the $876 M/year fleet is producing nothing — scattered gangs crawling through their all-reduce, and idle GPUs held by half-allocated jobs. This is the inverse of a normal NALSD arc: there is almost nothing to spend on the system, and an enormous amount to save on the resource it manages. A staff designer names that up front — "the bill is the fleet, not the scheduler; spend on scheduler nodes freely, because each one that buys 0.1 pt of utilization (~$0.9 M/yr) pays for ~150 of itself" — and then optimizes accordingly.

Iteration 0 — Topology-aware best-fit placement (a tree search over locality tiers)

System after this iteration

Connections and line-style legend. Solid slate = sync hot path; dashed gray = async / persistence; dashed red = (none this iteration).

  • ① Users → Priority job queue — unchanged: POST /v1/jobs, appended, re-sorted by (priority, submitted_at) each cycle.
  • ② Queue → Rule eval — iterate the queue in priority order; for each job, ask the topology tree for a placement.

Delta vs Naive: the flat min-heap of GPU free-times is replaced by a topology tree — the same set of GPUs, but now indexed by their (zone, rack, server) structure, with free-GPU counts and largest-contiguous-block sizes rolled up at every level. Placement changes from "pop the K earliest-free" to "walk the tree to the tightest tier that fits K, best-fit there." Everything else — the queue, the priority ordering, the allocation map, the node-agent path, the single-process structure — is unchanged.

Bottleneck

Naive's shape-determining failure is effective utilization: ~56% vs the 95% target, because the LC heap's "K earliest-free GPUs" is, by topology, the worst placement — a scattered 1024-gang trains at ~0.4×, dragging the GPU-hour-weighted average topology efficiency to ~0.7. The whole premise is "fragmentation is the cost," so this is the dominant failing NFR. It is the bin-packing bottleneck with a dimension the naive design ignored: not "is there a free slot?" but "is there a contiguous, low-latency-connected block of K slots?" Per bottleneck-to-decisions#hot-partition and the bin-packing entry in #29: when the resource has internal structure (here, a fabric hierarchy) the placement must score candidates on that structure, not first-fit. Iteration 0 takes that lever: model the fleet as a tree, walk it to the tightest enclosing tier that fits the gang, and pack to leave big contiguous holes.

Alternatives considered (C4)

  • Topology tree + tightest-enclosing-tier best-fit — chosen. The tree makes "which tier can host this gang?" an O(depth) walk and "which candidate leaves the biggest hole?" an O(candidates) compare; it directly attacks the topology-efficiency term (gangs land packed) and the contiguous-hole term (best-fit preserves room for the next big gang). It's the standard answer for "bin-pack a resource with a locality hierarchy" — Borg's machine-scoring, Kubernetes' topology-aware scheduling, Slurm's --topology plugin all do a version of it.
  • Keep the flat heap, just sort candidates by a locality score — rejected. Re-scoring 50 k GPUs by "how close are you to the others I've picked?" per job is the O(N×jobs) scan that already worries the latency NFR — and it still doesn't give you "the tightest tier that fits," only "the k least-bad individual GPUs," which isn't the same (you want a block, not k nearby points). The tree's roll-ups are what make "is there a rack with 64 free?" O(1).
  • Solve placement as an ILP every cycle (optimal packing) — rejected here, deferred. An integer program over "which GPUs to which jobs, minimize fragmentation + maximize locality" gives the optimal answer — but at 50 k GPUs × 5 k jobs it doesn't solve in 2 s, and you don't get to re-solve from scratch every cycle (placements are sticky). The greedy tree-walk gets ~90–95% of the way; ILP is the right tool for periodic rebalancing (a background job over a small window), which is exactly where #29 uses it — we'd lift it here as a probe, not the hot path. (Constraint-flip: 🔀 if jobs were short — minutes, not hours — and turnover were high, a per-cycle ILP over the small set of currently-pending jobs against the currently-free blocks would be both feasible and worth it.)
  • Reserve fixed "pools" per gang size (a 1024-pool, a 512-pool, ...) — rejected. Eliminates the fit search, but statically partitioning a 50 k fleet by job size is itself a fragmentation source — the 1024-pool sits half-empty while 512-jobs queue. Dynamic best-fit on a tree adapts; static pools don't. (We do keep a soft notion of "reserve enough big contiguous blocks for the production-tier 1024-jobs" — that's the defrag job's target in Iter 3.)

The chosen move

Model the 50 k GPUs as a topology tree: zone (12) → rack (~780) → server (6,250, each an 8-GPU NVLink full-mesh domain) → GPU (50,000); plus a rail-group cross-link layer between racks (the pod). Each interior node carries free_gpus and largest_contiguous_block — the size of the biggest gang it could host topology-tight right now — maintained incrementally: an alloc/free at a GPU bubbles a delta up the tree (O(depth) ≈ 4 levels). Placement for a job wanting K GPUs: (1) determine the target tier — the smallest tier whose kind can hold K at all (K≤8: a server / NVLink domain; K≤64: a rack; K≤~512: a pod/rail-group; K≤~4,160: a zone; larger would be cross-zone but K≤1024 so it never gets there); (2) among nodes at that tier with largest_contiguous_block ≥ K, score by leave-the-biggest-hole — prefer the node whose remaining contiguous block after this allocation is largest (best-fit on the block, so a future 1024-gang still has somewhere to go); ties broken toward the node that's already partly used (consolidate, don't smear); (3) if no node at the target tier fits, walk up a tier and try again, recording the locality cost; (4) if even the zone tier can't fit it topology-tight, place it on the best scattered set available now (still no gang atomicity here — a partial-or-scattered placement is what Iter 1 replaces with "queue instead"). Record the chosen topology_tier in the allocation (it's what the utilization metric weights by, and what defrag targets). Cite the bin-packing / filter-and-score machinery in #29 Iter 1 — this is that, with the score function dominated by the locality-tier term.

What breaks (C5; cite failure-modes.html)

  • Locality hot-spot — the "good racks" get all the small jobs. Best-fit-by-leave-biggest-hole, applied naively, can pile small jobs into a few racks (they "leave the biggest hole" elsewhere by definition) — concentrating churn and heat on a corner of the fleet. Defence: a secondary score term that spreads independent small jobs across racks (anti-affinity for unrelated tenants) while still consolidating fragmentation; cap per-rack job count. Blast radius: a few hot racks (thermal throttling, faster wear); detection: per-rack temperature + churn metrics.
  • Topology-tree drift. The tree's roll-up counts are derived state; a missed update (a crash mid-bubble, a node-agent report lost) leaves the tree thinking a rack has 64 free when it has 0 → it places a gang there → double-allocation or a failed start. Defence: the allocation map (the gpu_addr→job_id records) is the source of truth; the tree is a cache rebuilt from it on startup and periodically reconciled in the background (scan the map, recompute roll-ups, diff, alarm on drift). Blast radius: a few bad placements until reconcile; detection: the drift alarm + start-command failures.
  • Big-gang squeeze. If the fleet fills with small jobs (each leaving a small hole), largest_contiguous_block at the zone tier can drop below 1024 even when total free GPUs >> 1024 — a production 1024-job can't be placed despite "plenty of free GPUs." Defence: keep a soft reservation of N big contiguous blocks (don't fill the last rack of a free pod with a 16-job if a 1024 is queued); this becomes the defrag job's explicit target in Iteration 3. Blast radius: a queued production big-job; detection: largest_contiguous_block vs queued_max_K gauge.

Iteration 1 — Gang scheduling: all-or-nothing reservations + priority preemption with checkpointing

System after this iteration

Connections and line-style legend. Solid slate = sync hot path; dashed gray = async / persistence / lease; dashed red = preemption.

  • ① Users → Priority queue — unchanged; the queue is now explicitly tiered (production / research / best-effort) and within a tier ordered by submit time.
  • ② Queue → Gang-atomic placement — for each job in priority order, ask the topology tree for a placement of all K GPUs at an acceptable tier for that job's size class (e.g., a 1024-job's "acceptable" is "≤ one zone"; an 8-job's is "one server"). If found → reserve all K atomically (commit the allocation, update the tree, emit StartJob). If not → the job stays queued (no partial reservation, ever — we never assign GPUs we can't immediately use as a full gang).
  • ③ Placement → Preemption — if a higher-priority queued job can't fit and there are lower-priority running jobs whose GPUs would let it fit topology-tight: find the smallest-total-GPU set of lower-priority victims whose reclaimed GPUs (plus current free GPUs) form an acceptable block, preferring victims that checkpointed recently (cheap to preempt — the checkpoint hint says how much progress they'd lose). Signal each victim's node agents Preempt(checkpoint, deadline); at the deadline reclaim the GPUs whether or not the checkpoint finished (the job restarts from its last checkpoint); place the beneficiary; re-queue the victims at the head of their priority tier with a back-off so we don't immediately re-preempt them.
  • ④ Placement ↔ Alloc map + tree — atomic commit/rollback of a gang reservation: either all K entries flip to allocated→job_id and the tree's counts update, or none do (no torn state). Disk dump for durability. HeartbeatAlloc from node agents renews each allocation's lease; a missed lease → the orphan reaper reclaims those GPUs (the job died without telling us).
  • ⑤ Preemption → Preemption ledger — every preemption appends (preemption_id, victim_job_id, beneficiary_job_id, gpus_reclaimed, checkpoint_requested_at, reclaimed_at, reason); this is the audit trail (who was preempted by whom, when, how much they lost) and the input to a per-team "you've been preempted N times this week" report and to anti-thrash logic (don't preempt the same job twice in a window).
  • ⑥ Scheduler → Node agents (StartJob / Preempt) — StartJob(job_id, gpu_addrs) as before; Preempt(victim_job_id, checkpoint_deadline) tells the agents holding the victim's GPUs to signal the training process to checkpoint and exit by the deadline.
  • ⑦ Node agents → Scheduler (Heartbeat / completion) — periodic HeartbeatAlloc(job_id, gpu_addrs) renews the lease; a completion (or a preemption-exit) reports the GPUs free; the scheduler reclaims them and re-runs placement (the freed block may now satisfy a queued gang).

Delta vs Iteration 0: placement becomes gang-atomic — reserve all K at an acceptable topology tier or leave the job queued; no partial holds, ever. A priority preemption path is added: a high-priority queued job that won't fit can evict the smallest set of cheap-to-preempt lower-priority running jobs. A preemption ledger records every preemption (audit + anti-thrash). The topology tree, the allocation map, the node-agent path are reused unchanged; still one process, still no cells/failover.

Bottleneck

Iteration 0's residual: ~75% effective utilization vs the 95% target, and the gap is now entirely the idle_held_frac term — partial holds (a job with 600 of its 1024 GPUs, the 600 sitting idle) and contention deadlock (A holds 600, B holds 400, both want 1024, neither yields). At 5 k queued jobs this is the steady state, not an edge case, and it also blows the priority-SLA NFR — a production job starves behind stuck big jobs forever. The classification is two failure modes feeding one bottleneck: pool-starvation (resources held but unusable) and a deadlock cycle; the bottleneck is "the scheduler hands out resources it can't make productive." Per the resource-scheduler playbook (#29) and the gang-scheduling literature (Borg's "alloc sets," kube-batch / Volcano's PodGroup): the fix is all-or-nothing — never reserve a partial gang — plus a preemption escape valve so the queue can't deadlock on big jobs. Iteration 1 does both: gang atomicity kills partial holds and the deadlock; priority preemption gives a high-priority job a path forward when the fleet is full; checkpointing bounds the preemption cost to one checkpoint interval, not the whole run.

Alternatives considered (C4)

  • All-or-nothing gang reservation + priority preemption with checkpointing — chosen. Atomicity is the only thing that structurally prevents partial holds and the deadlock cycle (you can't deadlock holding resources you never grabbed). Preemption is the only escape valve when the fleet is full and a production job needs in — and checkpointing is what makes preemption acceptable (a bounded cost, not a catastrophe). This is the Borg / Volcano model. Two related techniques, one bottleneck ("resources handed out but not productive").
  • Backfill instead of preemption (let small jobs use the gaps while a big job waits for its block) — chosen as a complement, not a substitute. Backfill (Slurm's classic move) does help — while a 1024-job waits for a contiguous block, run short small jobs in the holes (with a deadline so they vacate in time). We keep it: it raises allocated_frac without preemption churn. But backfill alone doesn't bound the big-job wait (no small job ever has to leave if its T is long), so preemption is still needed for the priority SLA. (Constraint-flip: 🔀 if all jobs were short — minutes — backfill alone would suffice and preemption could be dropped; the queue would drain fast enough that a big job never waits long.)
  • Reservation-with-timeout (hold a partial gang for N minutes hoping the rest frees up) — rejected. This is the naive partial-hold with a band-aid; it still idles the held GPUs for up to N minutes × (every contended big job) — at 5 k queued jobs that's exactly the ~15% idle we're trying to kill. Atomic-or-queue idles nothing.
  • No preemption, just a longer queue and bigger fleet — rejected. "Buy 10% more GPUs so production never has to wait" is ~$88 M/year — vastly more than the scheduler-side cost of a preemption path, and it doesn't even work (a single research job claiming 4096 GPUs for two weeks still blocks production for two weeks without preemption). Preemption is ~free and decisive.
  • Preempt by killing (no checkpoint) — rejected. Costs the victim its entire run-since-start — for a job that's been running 10 days, that's catastrophic and makes preemption politically impossible. Checkpoint-then-preempt costs one interval (~5–30 min). The cost knob is the checkpoint interval: tighter = cheaper preemption but more checkpoint IO; we let the framework set it per job and use the hint.

The chosen move

Gang atomicity. A job's placement is all K GPUs at an acceptable topology tier, committed atomically, or nothing. "Acceptable tier" is a per-size policy: K≤8 → must be one server (one NVLink domain); K≤64 → one rack; K≤512 → one pod/rail-group; K≤1024 → ≤ one zone (and we'd prefer tighter — a 256-job goes in one rack-cluster if it can, a pod if it can't, a zone only as a last resort). If the tree can't satisfy that, the job stays queued — we never reserve a partial gang, so no GPU is ever held idle waiting for siblings, and there's no cycle to deadlock on. The reservation commit is a two-phase flip over the K GPU records (all or none) so a crash mid-commit leaves no torn state. Priority preemption. When the head-of-queue is a higher-priority job that won't fit: among lower-priority running jobs, find the minimum-total-GPU subset whose GPUs, freed, would let the beneficiary fit topology-tight — an instance of the "minimum hitting set" shape, solved greedily (sort lower-priority jobs by "GPUs freed per unit of progress-lost" using the checkpoint hint; take the cheapest until the block is satisfiable). Signal those victims to checkpoint with a deadline; at the deadline reclaim regardless; commit the beneficiary's gang; re-queue victims at the head of their tier with an exponential back-off (so a thrash-prone job isn't re-preempted immediately) and a per-job preemption-rate cap from the ledger. Backfill. While a big job waits for its block to assemble (e.g., it's queued for a 1024-block that's currently 900 free + a 128-job finishing in 20 min), let short jobs (declared T < the wait) run in the holes, with a hard vacate-deadline; this keeps allocated_frac high without churn. Cite the gang-scheduling + preemption machinery in #29 (and Borg's alloc-sets / Volcano's PodGroup as the production cousins).

What breaks (C5; cite failure-modes.html)

  • Preemption thrash. A burst of high-priority jobs preempts a wave of research jobs; they re-queue at the head of their tier and immediately get scheduled into freed GPUs — only to be preempted again by the next high-priority arrival. Net effect: research jobs make no progress, checkpoint IO storms, the preemption ledger floods. Defence: exponential back-off on re-queue after a preemption; a per-job preemption-rate cap (after N preemptions in a window, the job becomes "do-not-preempt" for a cooldown — it now blocks higher-priority jobs, which is the lesser evil); preempt the fewest, cheapest victims (the min-set search), not the most. Blast radius: a slice of research jobs stalling; detection: preemption-rate-per-job and checkpoint-IO metrics.
  • Priority inversion / starvation. A production job needs a 1024-block; the only lower-priority jobs occupying a candidate block are themselves large, recently-checkpointed-long-ago (expensive to preempt) — the min-cost search picks "preempt nothing, wait" — and the production job waits past its 5-min SLA. Or: a do-not-preempt-cooldown'd research job sits on the only viable block. Defence: an SLA-breach escalation — if a production job's queue wait exceeds the SLA, the cost cap is lifted (preempt even expensive victims); a hard "production always wins eventually" invariant. Blast radius: one production job, briefly; detection: per-job queue-wait vs SLA gauge.
  • Stuck preemption (victim won't die). A victim's training process ignores the checkpoint signal (hung, in a CUDA kernel, OOM-looping); the deadline passes; the scheduler reclaims the GPUs at the agent level (kill the process, reset the GPUs) — but if the agent itself is wedged, the GPUs are neither freed nor productive. Defence: the deadline-reclaim escalates from "signal" → "SIGKILL" → "agent reboots the GPU" → "fence the server out of the fleet (mark draining) and page"; the orphan-reaper lease catches a server that goes silent. Blast radius: up to one server's GPUs, briefly; detection: preemption-deadline-exceeded metric + the agent heartbeat.

Iteration 2 — Cell-based hierarchical scheduler (Borg-cell / Omega style)

System after this iteration

Connections and line-style legend. Solid slate = sync hot path; dashed gray = async / checkpoint / config / lease; dashed red = failover promotion.

  • ① Users → Global router — POST /v1/jobs now hits the router (stateless, ~2–3 replicas behind an LB). The router writes the job to the job store and picks a target cell.
  • ② Router → Cell scheduler — the router routes the job to a cell whose current free contiguous block can host the gang at an acceptable tier — for small jobs, any cell with room; for a 1024-job, a cell with a free 1024-block (or one likely to free one soon; the router queries each cell's largest_contiguous_block gauge). If no single cell can host a big gang right now, the job queues against the cell most likely to free a block (or, in Iter 3, the router fires speculative attempts at several). The cell scheduler then runs the exact Iteration-0/1 logic over its own ~4,160-GPU subtree: topology-tree best-fit, gang-atomic reserve-or-queue, priority preemption within the cell.
  • ③ Cell scheduler → etcd (checkpoint) — each cell scheduler holds a short-TTL lease in etcd (it's the primary for its cell) and periodically writes its allocation map + queue snapshot to etcd (the cell's map is < ~2 MB — tiny). The warm standby (in another AZ) reads the lease and the map; if the primary's lease lapses (crash, partition, GC > TTL), etcd's fencing lets the standby take over: it already has the (near-current) map, reconciles a few seconds of node-agent heartbeats, and continues. Running jobs in the cell never noticed.
  • ④ Cell scheduler ↔ Job/quota/ledger store — the cell scheduler reads its queued jobs (filtered to its cell) and writes state transitions + preemption ledger entries to the shared Postgres (unchanged from Iter 1; now partitioned by cell logically).
  • ⑤ Cell schedulers → Router (free-block gauges) — each cell periodically reports (free_gpus, largest_contiguous_block, queue_depth_by_priority) to the router so it can route the next job to a cell that fits. This is the only cross-cell coupling, and it's coarse and stale-tolerant (a slightly-out-of-date gauge just costs a routing retry).
  • ⑥ Cell scheduler → Node agents — StartJob / Preempt / StopJob to the agents in its cell (a server belongs to exactly one cell); unchanged otherwise.
  • ⑦ Node agents → Cell scheduler (heartbeat) — HeartbeatAlloc renews the allocation lease at the owning cell scheduler; on a primary→standby promotion the agents re-register with the new primary (they discover it via etcd / a service-discovery hop).

Delta vs Iteration 1: the one scheduler process is split into N≈12 cell schedulers (one per zone, ~4,160 GPUs each) each running the Iter-0/1 logic over its own subtree, plus a thin stateless global router that assigns each job to a cell. Each cell scheduler gets a warm standby (other AZ) and checkpoints its (tiny) allocation map to etcd, which also holds the cell-scheduler leases (fence + promote). Cells are independent failure domains. The placement algorithm inside a cell, the gang/preemption semantics, the node-agent protocol, the job/ledger stores are all unchanged — only the partitioning + the redundancy are new.

Bottleneck

Iteration 1's residual: the single scheduler process is the SPOF (its loss stops all placement — running jobs survive, but the fleet drifts useless within minutes; restart is a stale-dump rebuild taking minutes; > the ~30-s availability target) and the latency cliff (the preemption search for a big high-priority beneficiary — scan ~thousands of running jobs, evaluate which subset frees an acceptable block, re-fit after each — runs ~1–3 s on one box, blowing the 2-s p99). Both are the same root cause: one process evaluating the whole fleet against the whole queue. Classified coordinator-bottleneck — the canonical signal: a single component's per-cycle work grows with the product of the fleet size and the request volume, and its loss is a total outage. Per bottleneck-to-decisions#coordinator-bottleneck and cross-cutting#cells: partition the work into independent cells (each scheduler owns a slice of the fleet, so per-cycle work shrinks ~N× — or N²× for the fleet×queue product), route requests to cells with a thin stateless layer, and replicate each cell scheduler (warm standby + state checkpoint, fenced by a lease) so a cell-scheduler loss is a sub-second handoff, not a fleet outage. This is Borg's cell model (a Borgmaster per cell of ~10k machines), Omega's shared-state partitioning, Kubernetes' multi-cluster federation — the same move. Iteration 2 does all of it: ~12 cells aligned to zones, a stateless router, warm-standby cell schedulers with etcd leases.

Alternatives considered (C4)

  • Cell-based hierarchical scheduler — build it Borg-style — chosen. ~12 cells aligned to zones (a cell = a topology-coherent slice of the fleet, ~4,160 GPUs); each cell scheduler runs the full Iter-0/1 logic over its subtree (per-cell scan ~4,160 GPUs × ~420 jobs ≈ ~1.7 M evals ≈ ~17 ms — the 2-s p99 holds with ~100× margin even with topology tree-walk + preemption search); a thin stateless router assigns jobs; each cell scheduler has a warm standby fenced by an etcd lease (failover ~seconds; running jobs unaffected). It's the standard at this scale; the question is build-vs-adopt — see the next three.
  • Kubernetes + Volcano/kube-batch for gang scheduling — a real option, rejected at this scale. Volcano gives PodGroup gang scheduling, queues with priorities, preemption, and (with the topology plugin / node-feature-discovery) some topology awareness; you'd run a K8s cluster per cell and federate. The trade: K8s' scheduler is generic and slow for this — at ~4,160 GPUs × pods, the default scheduler's filter-and-score over node objects is heavier than our purpose-built topology tree, and Volcano's gang preemption is less surgical than the min-cost-victim-set search; OpenAI's "Kubernetes to 7,500 nodes" post documents the patches needed (custom schedulers, removing default plugins, gang scheduling, topology-aware placement) precisely because vanilla K8s doesn't scale here. We'd take K8s if the platform already ran on it (operational leverage); greenfield at 50k GPUs, a Borg-style purpose-built cell scheduler is tighter. (Meta's MAST and Microsoft's Singularity are both bespoke for the same reason.)
  • Slurm (one controller per cell) — rejected. Slurm is the HPC default — gang scheduling (job arrays / heterogeneous jobs), backfill, topology plugin, preemption — and a single slurmctld comfortably handles thousands of nodes. But: its preemption model is coarser (suspend/requeue, not min-cost-victim-set), its topology model is a fixed switch-tree (less expressive than our rail-aware tree), and integrating per-team priority quotas + a programmatic job API + checkpoint-hint-aware preemption means heavy customization on a C codebase. Slurm is the right answer for a classic HPC center; a cloud-native ML platform with a programmatic API and dynamic priority leans bespoke or K8s+Volcano.
  • Ray (or a Ray-based platform) for the whole thing — rejected for placement, kept for the layer above. Ray's PlacementGroup with STRICT_PACK/STRICT_SPREAD is a gang+topology primitive, and a lot of training tooling runs on Ray — but Ray's GCS is itself a centralized scheduler with the same coordinator-bottleneck shape at 50k GPUs, and it's optimized for fine-grained task scheduling, not hours-long gang reservations. The platform may well expose Ray to users (as the training runtime); underneath, the GPU placement is this cell scheduler. Different layers.
  • One cell = one rack-cluster (~64 GPUs), so ~780 tiny cells — rejected. Per-cell scan becomes trivial, but a 1024-GPU gang can't fit in any cell — you'd need cross-cell "super-cells" for everything bigger than a rack, recreating the coordinator problem one level up. The cell must be at least as big as the largest gang it should host topology-tight — one zone (~4,160) comfortably exceeds 1024, so 12 zone-cells is the sweet spot; you could subdivide a zone into 2–3 cells (~25–36 cells) if a cell scheduler ever got hot, as long as each still >> 1024. (And: if the max gang grew past one zone — an 8192-GPU job — you'd genuinely need a super-cell spanning multiple zones, or accept cross-zone for those; that's a scale-flip probe.)

The chosen move

Partition the 50 k GPUs into N≈12 cells, one per zone — a cell is a topology-coherent slice (~520 servers ≈ ~4,160 GPUs ≈ ~65 racks) so a job placed in a cell can be placed topology-tight within that cell (a 1024-gang fits comfortably in 4,160). Each cell runs a cell scheduler: it owns its cell's topology tree + allocation map + queue, and runs the identical Iteration-0/1 logic over its subtree — topology-tree best-fit, gang-atomic reserve-or-queue, min-cost-victim-set preemption, backfill. Per-cell per-cycle work: ~4,160 GPUs × ~(5,000/12)≈420 queued jobs ≈ ~1.7 M placement-quality evals ≈ ~17 ms; the preemption search is over ~(running jobs in one cell) ≈ hundreds, not thousands — so a big-beneficiary preemption decision is ~tens of ms, not seconds. The global router is stateless (~2–3 replicas): on submit, it writes the job to the job store and picks a target cell — for a small job, any cell with room (least-loaded, or sticky to the submitter's team's "home" cell); for a 1024-job, a cell whose largest_contiguous_block ≥ 1024 (or, if none, the cell most likely to free one — in Iteration 3 it fires speculative attempts at several). The router learns each cell's (free_gpus, largest_contiguous_block, queue_depth_by_priority) from coarse periodic reports — the only cross-cell state, and stale-tolerant. Failover: each cell scheduler holds a short-TTL etcd lease (it's the cell's primary) and checkpoints its (~<2 MB) allocation map + queue to etcd every few seconds; a warm standby in another AZ reads the lease and the map; on lease lapse, etcd fences the old primary and the standby takes over — it has the near-current map, reconciles a few seconds of node-agent heartbeats, and continues (the #37 warm-standby pattern, made cheap because the per-cell state is tiny). Running jobs survive a cell-scheduler outage (node agents keep them alive); only new placements / preemptions / reclaims in that cell pause, for ~seconds. Cite cross-cutting#cells (cell-based sharding — Borg cells / Omega) and sharding-strategies#range (the partition is topology-range-based: a cell owns a contiguous slice of the address space, so a gang's GPUs are all in one cell by construction). The #29 generic resource scheduler's Iteration 3 ("cell-based hierarchical scheduling") is this move; here the cells must also respect the topology hierarchy.

What breaks (C5; cite failure-modes.html)

  • Cell-scheduler loss / split-brain. A cell scheduler crashes or partitions; its standby promotes. Risk: both think they're primary (the partitioned old one didn't notice it lost the lease). Defence: etcd-leased fencing — committing an allocation or issuing a StartJob requires holding the lease; etcd can't grant two leases; the fenced side's commands are rejected by the node agents (which check the lease epoch). Blast radius: ~1/12 of the fleet's placements pause for < the lease TTL; running jobs untouched; detection: etcd lease-change events + a placement-pause metric per cell; recovery: automatic.
  • Lease-expiry storm under an etcd blip. etcd hiccups → many cell schedulers lose their leases at once → a wave of promotions → brief fleet-wide placement pause. Defence: lease TTLs longer than a typical etcd blip; promotions are cheap (warm standbys with near-current ~2 MB maps) and idempotent (the new primary reconciles and continues); the router rate-limits how many cells it'll let flip at once; node agents keep running jobs alive throughout. Blast radius: a transient placement pause across cells; detection: a fleet-wide promotion-rate spike.
  • A hot cell (one zone gets all the big jobs). The router, routing 1024-jobs to "whichever cell has a free 1024-block," can pile them all on one zone → that cell's queue grows, its scheduler is busiest, its GPUs cycle hardest. Defence: the router spreads independent big jobs across cells (weighted by free capacity + inverse queue depth), not just "first cell that fits"; per-cell queue-depth and scheduler-CPU alarms; if a cell scheduler is consistently hot, subdivide that zone into 2 cells. Blast radius: one zone's queue-wait degrades; detection: per-cell queue-depth + scheduler-CPU.
  • Router routes to a cell that just filled up. The router's per-cell free-block gauges are stale by the report interval; it routes a 1024-job to a cell whose block just got taken → the cell can't place it → the job bounces back to the router. Defence: the cell scheduler returns "no fit" promptly; the router retries with a fresh gauge or, in Iteration 3, has already fired speculative attempts at several cells; gauges reported frequently (sub-second) for the contended big-block resource. Blast radius: a routing retry (~10s of ms); detection: routing-retry rate.

Two paths: (1) super-cells — the router can, for the rare giant gang, temporarily compose a super-cell spanning 2–3 zones, with a transient super-cell scheduler that coordinates the member cells (re-introducing a mini-coordinator, but only for a handful of jobs — acceptable); the gang accepts cross-zone interconnect for the inter-zone segments, which for an 8192-GPU run is unavoidable anyway (no DC has 8192 GPUs on one rail) and is engineered around (hierarchical all-reduce: NVLink within server, IB within zone, DCI between zones, with the cross-zone hop done sparsely). (2) bigger cells — if giant gangs are common, make a cell a whole zone-pair and live with fewer, larger cells (the per-cell scan grows but is still << the single-scheduler scan). Iterations 0–1 are unchanged; Iteration 2's cell sizing and Iteration 3's hedging both re-derive; this is a probe.

Iteration 3 — Tail-tolerance (speculative cross-cell placement + background defragmentation) + admission backpressure

This is #29's Iteration 4 (background defragmentation), GPU-flavored: the migration cost is a checkpoint+restart, not a live VM migration.

  • ⑦ Cell schedulers → Node agents — StartJob / Preempt / StopJob as before, plus Migrate(job_id, new_gpu_addrs, checkpoint_deadline) for defrag (semantically: a preempt-from-here + start-there pair, with the same checkpoint mechanics).

Delta vs Iteration 2: three additions, all targeting the tail and the backlog. Speculative cross-cell placement — the router fires a big gang at several cells in parallel and takes the first commit (hedged requests; collapses the big-gang queue-wait tail and de-coordinator-izes the router). A background defragmenter — per cell, migrate (checkpoint+restart) small jobs to coalesce big contiguous holes, keeping big-block inventory healthy. An admission controller — when the queue grows faster than completions, delay/shed best-effort jobs and signal capacity pressure, keeping the queue bounded. The cell schedulers, etcd, node agents are otherwise unchanged.

Bottleneck

Iteration 2's residual is the tail, in three guises: (1) a big production gang queued for a 1024-block can wait for some cell to free one — and "whichever cell happens to free a block first" is exactly a long-pole / straggler problem — blowing the 5-min production queue-wait SLA at p99; (2) the slow accretion of small fragmented holes as jobs come and go (and the cell-boundary tax from Iter 2) gradually erodes big-block inventory, so the long pole gets longer over weeks; (3) under a sustained burst (a quarter-end model-release crunch), the queue can grow faster than jobs complete — an unbounded backlog. The first two are tail-latency (the system's p99 is gated by the slowest-to-resolve member — here, the slowest-to-place gang member is the gang itself waiting for a block) — per the standard playbook, you attack it with hedging / speculation (try several, take the first) and by reducing the variance at the source (defrag keeps blocks available). The third is backlog-growth — per bottleneck-to-decisions#backlog, the fix is admission backpressure: shed/delay the work that can be shed (best-effort), prioritize the work that can't (production), and signal the source so it can back off. Iteration 3 does all three; they're the same bottleneck shape ("the tail / the queue grows without bound") and the technique budget (Naive + 4 iterations) is now spent.

Alternatives considered (C4)

  • Speculative cross-cell placement (hedged PlaceGang) + background defrag + admission control — chosen. Hedging is the textbook tail-killer when you have m independent providers (here, cells) and want the min latency — the cost is the wasted tentative reservations on the losing cells (short-lived, cheap). Defrag attacks the variance at the source (keep big blocks available, so the long pole is shorter). Admission control is the only thing that bounds a backlog (you can't out-schedule a queue that grows faster than it drains). Three techniques, one bottleneck shape.
  • No speculation — just route the big gang to one cell and wait — rejected. Then the big-gang queue wait is the wait of that one cell's next free block — high variance; one unlucky routing decision (the cell whose only big-job-in-progress just got extended) and the production job blows its SLA. Hedging across 3 cells turns "one unlucky cell" into "all 3 unlucky," which is ~cubed-rarer. The wasted tentative holds cost a few seconds of a few cells' "I'm tentatively reserving" state — trivial.
  • No defrag — just preempt to make room for a queued big gang — partially rejected. Preemption (Iter 1) can assemble a block by evicting jobs — but evicting a 1024-job's worth of small running jobs to make a block is expensive (many checkpoints, many restarts) and disruptive (lots of victims), whereas defrag migrates a few small jobs to a different spot in the same cell (one checkpoint each, no loss of allocation) to coalesce a hole — same outcome, much cheaper. We use preemption for urgent production placement and defrag for steady-state block-inventory maintenance. (Constraint-flip: 🔀 if migration were impossible — jobs can't be moved — preemption is the only block-assembly tool and defrag drops out; the production SLA gets more expensive.)
  • No admission control — let the queue grow, it's just numbers in a table — rejected. A 50 k-job backlog isn't free: queue-wait estimates become meaningless, the cell schedulers' queue scans grow, users can't tell "queued for 10 min" from "queued for 3 days," and there's no signal to capacity planning that more GPUs are needed. Bounding the queue (shed best-effort, signal pressure) keeps the system legible and gives the org the data to buy more hardware (the real fix for sustained over-demand). Best-effort jobs are explicitly the shed-able tier — that's the deal.
  • Defrag via "drain a whole rack periodically" — rejected. Coarser, more disruptive (drains everything on the rack, including jobs that didn't need to move), and ill-targeted (it might drain a rack that wasn't a fragmentation problem). Targeted small-job migration moves the minimum.

The chosen move

Speculative cross-cell placement. For a big gang (threshold, e.g. K≥256), the router computes the m≈3 most promising cells (highest largest_contiguous_block, lowest big-gang queue depth, spread across AZs) and sends PlaceGang(job) to all m in parallel; each cell, on receipt, tentatively reserves a K-block if it can (a short-TTL hold, recorded so it doesn't double-promise) and replies; the router commits the first OK (turns the tentative hold into a real allocation, emits StartJob) and sends Cancel to the rest (they drop their holds). If all m say "no fit now," the job queues against the cell with the best gauge, and the router re-hedges on the next cycle. This is hedged requests, and it makes the big-gang placement latency the min over m independent cells, not the latency of one chosen-then-stuck cell — the production queue-wait p99 drops from "wait for one specific cell's next block" to "wait for the soonest of 3 cells' next block." Background defragmenter. Per cell, a low-priority background loop: when there's a queued big production gang that can't be placed and could be if a few small jobs moved, pick the migration set that creates the needed big block at minimum progress-cost (the small jobs whose checkpoints are recent; the fewest of them), and migrate each — Migrate(job_id, new_block, deadline): checkpoint, restart on the new block, free the old — coalescing the hole. Throttled to a migrations/hr budget; logged to a migration ledger; never run speculatively (only to satisfy a concrete queued big gang). This keeps big-block inventory healthy against the slow accretion of small holes. Admission control. An admission controller (could live in the router) watches per-priority queue depth and the completion rate over a sliding window; when the backlog is growing (queue-in > queue-out), it: returns longer wait estimates / soft 503s to new best-effort submissions, hard-rejects best-effort submissions if the queue exceeds a cap (with a "capacity-constrained, retry after T" reason — never a silent drop), leaves production/research flowing (production has the preemption escape valve; research waits longer by design), and publishes a capacity-pressure gauge for submitters and ops. Cite bottlenecks#tail-latency (hedging), the defrag machinery in #29 Iter 4, and bottlenecks#backlog-growth (admission backpressure).

What breaks (C5; cite failure-modes.html)

  • Speculation amplification. If many big gangs are queued and the router hedges each across 3 cells, the cells see 3× the PlaceGang traffic, mostly tentative-and-cancelled — and if a cell is slow to respond, the router might re-hedge before the cancels land, piling up stale tentative holds. Defence: cap concurrent hedges per cell; tentative holds have a short TTL (auto-released); the router de-dupes (one in-flight hedge per job); back off the hedge fan-out when cells report high tentative-hold churn. Blast radius: a cell's placement throughput degrades; detection: tentative-hold-churn + PlaceGang-rate metrics.
  • Defrag thrash / migration loss. Defrag migrates a small job to a "better" spot; then a big gang arrives that needs that spot, so defrag migrates it again; meanwhile each migration costs the job a checkpoint interval — a job could lose hours to repeated moves. Defence: a per-job migration-rate cap (from the migration ledger — after N migrations in a window, the job is "do-not-migrate"); defrag only runs when a concrete queued big production gang benefits (no speculative shuffling); a migration is only chosen if its progress-cost < the utilization-gain it unlocks. Blast radius: a few small jobs losing extra progress; detection: per-job migration count + the migration-ledger.
  • Admission shedding the wrong thing / never recovering. Under a long crunch the admission controller sheds best-effort indefinitely — fine, that's the deal — but if the queue-in/queue-out estimate is noisy it could oscillate (shed, un-shed, shed) or, worse, mis-classify a research job as best-effort and shed it. Defence: hysteresis on the shed threshold (shed above X, un-shed below Y < X); priority is an immutable property of the job, never inferred; a hard invariant that production and research are never shed (only delayed); the capacity-pressure signal is the human escalation path (buy more GPUs / reprioritize). Blast radius: best-effort jobs delayed/rejected (acceptable by tier); detection: shed-rate + shed-flap metrics + the pressure gauge.
  • Speculative-placement deadlock-lite. Two big gangs hedge across overlapping cell sets; each tentatively reserves a block in a different cell; the router commits gang A in cell X (cancelling its hold in cell Y), but the cancel races a commit in cell Y for gang B that depended on A's hold being released — transient inconsistency. Defence: tentative holds are first-class allocations with their own short lease; "commit" and "cancel" are idempotent and ordered by an epoch; a cell never double-commits a block; on any ambiguity the cell rejects and the router re-hedges. Blast radius: a hedge retry (~10s of ms); detection: tentative-hold-conflict metric.

Probe iterations (the interviewer pulls the thread — beyond the 5-Core budget)

Each is a real follow-up that would shift the design; each names the constraint that drives it.

Probe A — Multi-zone gangs: a 1024-GPU job that must span 2 zones (no single zone has a free 1024-block) — when is it worth it vs. queueing for a single-zone hole?

Move. Add a cross-zone placement mode the router can offer a queued big gang: "I can give you 512 GPUs in zone 3 and 512 in zone 7 now (cross-zone DCI between the halves, intra-zone fabric within each half), or you wait ~20 min for a single-zone 1024-block." The decision is a throughput-vs-wait trade: cross-zone-split throughput ≈ ~0.5–0.7× (the all-reduce now has a slow inter-zone hop, mitigated by hierarchical all-reduce — reduce within each zone-half first, then one cross-zone exchange); waiting costs wait_min × 1024 × $/GPU-min of idle-vs-running. Worth it if the job is long (a 2-week run amortizes a 20-min wait trivially — wait) and not worth it if it's short or the user is iterating (a 2-hour debugging run — take the cross-zone). The scheduler exposes both and the user (or a policy) picks; the router needs cross-zone block accounting. Cite bottlenecks#cross-region (the cross-zone-latency penalty, milder than true multi-region). Constraint that drives it: occasional big-block scarcity + heterogeneous job durations — a one-size policy is wrong.

Probe B — A spot / preemptible tier: a cheaper tier that's always preempted first — how does the scheduler model it?

Move. Make "best-effort" an explicit preemptible tier with a price discount (charged to teams at, say, 30% of the production rate — the internal-chargeback analog of spot). The scheduler already preempts lowest-priority-first (Iter 1); the addition is: (1) preemptible jobs are placed opportunistically in any free GPUs without a topology guarantee (they take what's there — they're tolerant by definition); (2) they're the first victims of any higher-tier placement, no min-cost search needed; (3) admission never shed them by rejection — it just doesn't promise them; they run when there's slack and stop when there isn't (so the fleet's "idle" GPUs are never truly idle — preemptible jobs soak them, lifting allocated_frac toward 1.0 without risking the SLA tiers). This is the single biggest realized-utilization lever after topology-aware placement: a fleet with a healthy preemptible tier runs at ~98–99% allocated because slack instantly fills with preemptible work. Constraint that drives it: there's always some fault-tolerant batch work (HP sweeps, eval runs, data jobs) willing to be preempted for a discount — not using it leaves utilization on the table.

Probe C — Fair-share across teams (DRF): when 20 teams contend, "priority" isn't enough — how do you share fairly across GPUs and GPU-memory and fabric bandwidth?

Move. Layer dominant-resource fairness (DRF — Mesos's allocator) on top of the priority tiers: within a tier, instead of strict FIFO, the scheduler picks the next job from the team whose dominant-resource share (max over {GPUs, total GPU-memory, total fabric bandwidth requested} as a fraction of the team's quota) is currently lowest — so a team that's hogging GPUs gets deprioritized even if another team is hogging memory-heavy small jobs. With H100s, GPU and GPU-memory are nearly collinear (every job gets whole GPUs, each with 80 GB), so DRF here mostly reduces to GPU-fair-share with a fabric-bandwidth tiebreaker for jobs declaring rail-bandwidth needs — but the framework matters for the heterogeneous-fleet case (Probe E) where A100s have less memory. The cell schedulers each run DRF over their cell's queue; the global router can rebalance team allocations across cells. Constraint that drives it: multi-team contention where priority alone causes one team to monopolize a tier — needs a fairness layer, and DRF is the multi-resource generalization of weighted-fair-share.

Probe D — Heterogeneous fleet: the platform also has 2 zones of older A100s — how does a job get placed across mixed hardware?

Move. A job declares a hardware constraint: "H100 only" (it needs FP8 / the memory / the NVLink bandwidth), "A100 OK" (it's small / not bandwidth-bound), or "any" (cheapest available). The topology tree gains a gpu_type attribute per leaf; the placer filters candidates by the job's constraint before the locality scoring; a job marked "any" prefers A100s when its size class doesn't need H100 (so the scarce H100s go to jobs that need them — a coarse but effective allocation rule). Cells stay homogeneous (an A100-zone cell, an H100-zone cell) so a gang is never split across GPU types (mixed-type gangs train at the slower type's rate — never worth it). The chargeback rate differs by type; the preemptible tier is mostly A100s (cheap, fault-tolerant work). Constraint that drives it: capex reality — you don't replace 50k GPUs overnight; older hardware lingers and the scheduler must route around it sensibly. (Borg's machine-attribute constraints, Slurm's feature flags, K8s node selectors — all the same idea.)

Probe E — Checkpoint/restart cost amortization: preemption and defrag both cost a checkpoint interval — how do you keep that small without drowning in checkpoint IO?

Move. The scheduler doesn't own checkpointing, but it can shape it: (1) hint a checkpoint before a likely preemption — when the scheduler sees a high-priority job queued that might preempt a low-priority running job soon, it can ask that job's agents to checkpoint now (proactively), so if the preemption fires, the loss is ~0, not a full interval; (2) tier the checkpoint cadence by preemption risk — jobs in the preemptible tier checkpoint more often (they expect to be bumped); jobs at high priority checkpoint less often (they rarely get bumped) — trading IO for resilience where it's needed; (3) credit checkpoint IO to the job's chargeback so teams that want cheap preemptible GPUs internalize the IO cost of frequent checkpoints. The framework picks the mechanism; the scheduler picks the policy. Constraint that drives it: at 50k GPUs, "everyone checkpoints every 5 min just in case" is a huge, mostly-wasted IO load on the checkpoint-storage system — cadence should track actual preemption risk.

The whole design is "spend on the scheduler, save on the fleet," and every iteration's ROI is enormous (the most expensive iteration, the ~30-node cell fleet, cost ~$0.18 M/yr and bought a working 2-s p99 + 99.9% availability — ~$165 M/yr of reclaimed waste was already on the table from Iter 0's algorithm change alone).

Degradation contract

When a cell scheduler dies → its warm standby promotes in ~seconds (etcd-fenced); only new placements/preemptions/migrations in that cell pause for that window; running jobs in the cell keep running (node agents don't need the scheduler to keep a job alive); if the standby is also gone → cold rebuild from etcd + heartbeat reconcile (~seconds). When etcd is degraded (below quorum) → cell schedulers can't renew leases or checkpoint → they keep serving with their in-RAM state (placements continue, just not durably checkpointed) and stop committing risky operations until quorum returns — a "freeze, don't break" posture; running jobs untouched throughout. When the global router is down → new submissions are rejected (the user retries; the queue isn't lost — it's in Postgres + the cells); already-routed jobs keep getting scheduled by their cells. When the queue grows faster than completions → admission sheds/delays best-effort (with a reason — never silent), keeps production/research flowing, signals capacity pressure. When a preempted/migrated job won't checkpoint-and-exit → the deadline-reclaim escalates (signal → SIGKILL → agent resets the GPU → fence the server, page); the orphan-reaper lease catches a silent server. When no cell can host a queued big gang → defrag tries to coalesce a block; if it still can't, the gang waits (visible queue-wait estimate) or the user is offered a cross-zone split (Probe A). The system never double-allocates a GPU, never silently drops a job, never lets a production job starve indefinitely (SLA-breach escalation lifts the preemption cost cap), and never exceeds the 2-s placement-decision SLO (cell-bounded search). Cite failure-modes#leader-loss + #backlog-overflow + #pool-starvation.

Operations (C6)

  • Metrics (SLIs → NFRs). Effective utilization — the headline: GPU-hours weighted by topology_eff(K, tier) over running jobs / total GPU-hours; broken down by tier (how much is running at zone-tier vs server-tier) and by team; plus raw allocated_frac and idle_held_frac (the latter should be ~0 — non-zero means a gang-atomicity bug). Placement decision latency p99 per cell (and the preemption-search latency separately — the thing that regressed in Iter 1). Production queue-wait p99 vs the 5-min SLA; queue depth by priority + the queue-in/queue-out rates (the backlog signal). Fragmentation — largest_contiguous_block per cell vs queued_max_K (the "can we place the biggest queued gang?" gauge); defrag migrations/hr + progress-cost. Preemption rate, per-job preemption count (anti-thrash), checkpoint-IO from preemptions. Availability — cell-scheduler promotion rate, placement-pause-per-cell, orphan-reap rate, lease-expiry events. Capacity pressure + shed rate + shed-flap. Router — routing-retry rate, tentative-hold churn, hedge fan-out.
  • Rollout. A new cell-scheduler binary canaries on one cell's standby first (it shadows the primary — reads the lease and map, computes placements, but doesn't act — and its decisions are diffed against the live primary for 24 h); then promote it (it becomes the primary; the old binary becomes the standby — instant rollback); roll cell-by-cell. The router and admission controller are stateless — standard blue-green behind the LB. Rollback triggers: a divergence in the canary's placement decisions, any regression in placement-decision p99 or production queue-wait, any rise in idle_held_frac or the shed-flap rate.
  • Migration (single-scheduler → cellular). Bootstrap: stand up the cell schedulers in read-only shadow — each watches its zone's node-agent heartbeats and the job store, builds its topology tree + allocation map from current state, computes what it would place, but the old single scheduler still owns the fleet. Diff the shadow decisions against the old scheduler's for a week; chase down divergences (usually a tie-break or a topology-tree edge case). Then cut over zone-by-zone: hand zone 1's servers to cell 1's scheduler (the old scheduler stops managing them; the cell scheduler takes the etcd lease), watch for a day, proceed; the global router goes live once all zones are cellular. The old scheduler is kept as a fallback (it can re-take a zone) for a week, then decommissioned.
  • Headroom. +50% on cell-scheduler nodes (a deploy + an AZ loss promoting many standbys); etcd sized for the lease + checkpoint write rate × 3; the router sized for ~3× submission peak. The GPU fleet runs at 0% headroom by design — the 95%-utilization target is the headroom (the 5% slack absorbs scheduling churn, preemption restarts, and the cell-boundary tax); the preemptible tier (Probe B) soaks any slack above that. Reason for the scheduler headroom: a binary roll happening during an AZ loss is a Tuesday; the scheduler must not be the thing that falls over when the fleet most needs placing.

Real-system comparison

The closest production cousins: Google Borg (the "Large-scale cluster management at Google with Borg" paper) — the cell model (a Borgmaster per cell of ~10k machines, with a primary + Paxos-replicated state, and a stateless link to machine-resident Borglets), priority tiers, preemption, alloc-sets (Borg's gang primitive), and the "scheduler is cheap, the fleet is expensive" economics — this page is essentially Borg specialized to a GPU fleet with a fabric hierarchy; Omega (shared-state, optimistically-concurrent schedulers over a common cell state — the alternative to Borg's monolithic-per-cell that some of the per-cell-scheduler design here leans toward); Kubernetes + Volcano/kube-batch (the open-source gang-scheduling answer: PodGroup all-or-nothing, queues, preemption, topology plugins) — what you'd reach for if the platform already ran on K8s, with OpenAI's "Kubernetes to 7,500 nodes" post documenting exactly the patches the 50k-GPU regime forces (custom schedulers, removed default plugins, gang scheduling, topology-aware placement); Meta's MAST and Microsoft's Singularity (bespoke planet-scale ML schedulers with preemption + checkpointing + topology awareness — both built rather than adopted, for the same scale reasons); and on the topology side, NVIDIA's NVLink/NVSwitch + rail-optimized fabric docs (the source of the "8-GPU NVLink domain > rack > rail > zone" hierarchy and why a scattered gang's all-reduce stalls). Anticipated pushbacks

Stress tests against the derivation above — depth rehearsal, not interview rehearsal. If a question can't be answered in 3–6 sentences from the text above, the page is missing a probe.

What if the fleet were 10× tomorrow — 500 k GPUs, and jobs up to 8192 GPUs? (scale flip)

The cell model scales by adding cells: 500 k GPUs at ~4,160 GPUs/cell ≈ ~120 cells, ~240 cell-scheduler nodes — still ~$0.5 M/yr, still rounding error. The per-cell scan is unchanged (each cell is still ~4,160 GPUs × ~(50,000/120)≈420 jobs). The new pressure is two-fold: (1) the global router now picks among 120 cells — still cheap (a gauge lookup), but the speculative fan-out for big gangs should stay small (~3) or it amplifies; (2) the 8192-GPU gang exceeds one zone (~4,160), breaking the "a gang fits in one cell" invariant — that's Probe A's super-cell move (transiently compose a cross-zone super-cell with a thin coordinator for the handful of giant jobs, and engineer hierarchical all-reduce around the cross-zone hop). The bottleneck shifts from "coordinator" (long since cellular) to "the rare giant-gang placement," which is a coordinator again but for <1% of jobs — acceptable. The +50% scheduler headroom is unaffected; the GPU fleet's headroom is still "the 5%."

Why build a Borg-style cell scheduler instead of just running Kubernetes + Volcano (or Slurm, or Ray)? (alt-engine)

If the platform already ran on K8s, you'd extend it — the page says so (Iter 2's C4). K8s + Volcano gives gang scheduling (PodGroup), queues, preemption, and topology plugins; you'd federate a K8s cluster per cell. The cost: K8s' scheduler is generic — at ~4,160 GPUs × pods, its filter-and-score over node objects is heavier than a purpose-built rail-aware topology tree, and Volcano's gang preemption is coarser than the min-cost-victim-set search — which is precisely why OpenAI's "Kubernetes to 7,500 nodes" post is a catalog of patches (custom schedulers, removed default plugins, gang scheduling, topology placement), and why Meta (MAST) and Microsoft (Singularity) built bespoke. Slurm is the HPC default and handles the node count fine, but its preemption (suspend/requeue) and topology model (a fixed switch tree) are less expressive, and bolting on a programmatic job API + dynamic per-team priority + checkpoint-hint-aware preemption means heavy customization on a C codebase. Ray's PlacementGroup is a gang+topology primitive but the GCS is itself a centralized scheduler with the same coordinator-bottleneck shape at 50k GPUs — great as the training runtime users see, not the GPU placer underneath. Greenfield at 50k GPUs, bespoke-Borg-style wins on tightness; everywhere else, "extend what you have."

What changes if this is a regulated / multi-tenant-isolation environment — e.g., teams whose jobs must not share a server, or data-residency per zone? (regulatory)

The placer already handles "must not co-locate" — a job carries an anti-affinity constraint (topology_constraint extended: "no other tenant on my servers"); the topology tree's candidate filter excludes servers running another tenant; the cost is some lost packing efficiency (a few pts of utilization — the price of isolation, named). Data-residency-per-zone becomes a hard placement constraint: a job tagged "EU-only" can only be routed to EU-zone cells — the router's cell-selection respects it; cross-zone splits (Probe A) are forbidden across jurisdiction boundaries. What gains: an audit log of every placement and preemption (already have the ledgers — extend with the constraint that was enforced and why); RBAC on the operator API tightened (drain/quota changes need approval); encryption-at-rest on the control-plane DB and etcd (job metadata is the roadmap-leak risk — SSE + KMS, key rotation). Naming what stays the same: the topology-aware placement, gang atomicity, preemption, cells, hedging — all untouched; regulation adds constraints and governance, not new bottlenecks.

An entire zone goes dark right now — what's the blast radius and the recovery sequence? (failure)

Two things happen, separately. (1) The zone's GPUs are gone — ~4,160 GPUs (~8% of the fleet) and every running job in them; those jobs restart from their last checkpoint elsewhere when the scheduler re-queues them (the scheduler sees the node-agent heartbeats stop, marks those allocations dead, re-queues the jobs at the head of their tier with a "zone failure" reason — they don't lose more than a checkpoint interval). (2) The zone's cell scheduler primary is gone — its warm standby is in a different AZ (we placed it there for exactly this), so it promotes in ~seconds (etcd-fenced) and... has nothing to schedule, because the cell's GPUs are dark; it sits idle until the zone returns, then rebuilds its topology tree from the recovering node-agents. The router stops routing new jobs to the dead cell (its gauges show 0 free) and spreads them across the remaining 11 cells — their queues grow ~10%, which the admission controller's pressure signal flags. etcd (5 nodes, spread across AZs) keeps quorum on the survivors. The on-call sees: a re-queue spike (the zone's jobs), an elevated queue depth across surviving cells, and the zone's GPU count drop to 0; runbook: confirm it's a zone-down (not a partition), let auto-recovery proceed, escalate to the DC team. Cite failure-modes#leader-loss + #network-partition.

Where does the bill come from, and what's the one knob that moves it most? (cost)

~99.98% of the bill is the GPU fleet (~$876 M/yr at internal-amortized ~$2/GPU-hr); the scheduler is ~$0.2 M/yr. So the knob that moves the bill is effective utilization, and the single biggest lever on it — by a wide margin — is topology-aware placement (Iter 0): it took effective utilization from ~56% to ~75% (~$165 M/yr reclaimed) with one algorithm change and zero new hardware. After that, gang atomicity + preemption (Iter 1, ~+$105 M/yr) and the tail/defrag work (Iter 3, ~+$53 M/yr) are the next levers; the cell partition (Iter 2) costs a small utilization tax but is non-negotiable for operability. On the system side the only knob worth naming is the warm standby (~$8.6 k/mo for ~second failover vs ~minute) — trivially worth it. The "cost-vs-latency knob" framing barely applies here: the latency-sensitive thing (placement decision) costs nothing to make fast (it's a cell-bounded tree walk on a cheap node); the expensive thing (GPUs) isn't a latency knob, it's the entire point. Cite cost-shapes#ml-training.

How do you roll this out from a single existing scheduler without stranding jobs or double-allocating GPUs? (migration)

Phase 1: stand up the cell schedulers in read-only shadow — each watches its zone's node-agent heartbeats and the job store, builds its topology tree + allocation map from the live state, and computes what it would place — but the old single scheduler still owns the fleet and is the only thing issuing commands. Diff the shadow placements against the old scheduler's for a week; reconcile divergences (tie-breaks, topology edge cases). Phase 2: cut over zone-by-zone — for zone 1, atomically (via the etcd lease) transfer ownership of its servers from the old scheduler to cell 1's scheduler: the old one stops managing them, the cell scheduler takes the lease and starts issuing commands; node agents check the lease epoch on every command, so there's no window where both could act on the same GPU. Watch zone 1 for a day, then proceed zone-by-zone; the global router goes live once all 12 zones are cellular. Phase 3: keep the old scheduler as a fallback (it can re-take a zone's lease) for a week, then decommission. Rollback at any phase: a shadow-divergence beyond tolerance, an idle-held-GPU spike, or a placement-latency regression → transfer the zone's lease back. The risk is entirely Phase 1's diff; if the shadow matches for a week, the cutover is boring.

Utilization just dropped 8 points overnight — where do you look first? (monitoring/debug)

Decompose effective_util = allocated_frac × avg_topology_eff × (1 − idle_held_frac) × (1 − preempt_waste) on the dashboard and see which term moved. (1) allocated_frac dropped → fewer GPUs are assigned — check the queue: is it empty (genuine low demand — fine) or full but not draining (a cell scheduler stuck? a routing bug sending jobs to a dead cell? check per-cell placement-pause and routing-retry rates)? (2) avg_topology_eff dropped → gangs are landing scattered — check largest_contiguous_block per cell vs queued_max_K: did fragmentation accrete (defrag throttled too low? a wave of awkward small jobs?), or did a topology-tree drift bug make the placer think there's no tight block when there is (check the tree-reconcile drift alarm)? (3) idle_held_frac went non-zero (it should be ~0) → a gang-atomicity bug — some job is holding GPUs it can't use; find it in the allocation map (an allocation with fewer running processes than gpu_addrs), page; this is a correctness regression. (4) preempt_waste spiked → preemption thrash — check per-job preemption counts and the preemption ledger: a burst of high-priority jobs churning research jobs (back-off not kicking in?), or a do-not-preempt-cooldown bug. Each term maps to a specific failure mode from the What-Breaks blocks and a runbook entry; the "decompose the utilization formula" discipline is exactly so this is a dashboard read, not a guess.

S7. Key Takeaways

  • When the resource has a fabric, "any free slot" is the wrong answer. The LC #253 min-heap of free-times treats rooms as interchangeable; a GPU fleet's slots are not — a 1024-gang scattered across the fabric trains at ~0.4× a packed one. The first move is always: model the resource as a topology tree, place to the tightest enclosing tier that fits, pack to leave big contiguous holes. This one change took effective utilization ~56%→~75% with zero new hardware.
  • Gang requests need all-or-nothing semantics, or first-fit deadlocks. A singleton scheduler that hands out partial gangs strands GPUs (idle, held) and deadlocks under contention (A holds 600, B holds 400, both want 1024). Reserve all K atomically or leave the job queued — never a partial hold. Pair it with priority preemption (with checkpointing, so the cost is one interval not the whole run) as the escape valve when the fleet is full.
  • One scheduler can't re-scan 50 k GPUs × 5 k jobs every cycle — partition into cells. The coordinator-bottleneck is structural: per-cycle work grows with fleet×queue, and the process is a total-outage SPOF. Cells (one scheduler per ~4,160-GPU topology-coherent slice + a thin stateless router + warm standbys fenced by leases) shrink the work ~N²× and turn a scheduler loss into a ~second handoff. This is the Borg cell model; the cell must be at least as big as the largest gang it should host topology-tight.
  • The long pole of a big gang is the tail — hedge it. A 1024-job queued for "whichever cell frees a 1024-block first" is a straggler problem; fire speculative placement attempts at several cells in parallel and commit the first — the wait becomes the min over m cells, not the latency of one chosen-then-stuck cell. Complement with a background defragmenter (migrate small jobs to coalesce big blocks) so the long pole is shorter at the source, and admission backpressure so the backlog stays bounded.
  • Sometimes the thing you're optimizing isn't your system — it's the resource your system manages. This scheduler costs ~$0.2 M/yr; the fleet it places costs ~$876 M/yr. So the cost discipline inverts: spend freely on the scheduler (warm standbys, defrag workers, hedged placement, an extra cell), because each $1 that buys ~0.001 pt of effective utilization (~$8.8 k/yr) pays for itself ~50×. The whole arc reclaimed ~$310 M/yr of GPU capacity for a ~$0.2 M/yr scheduler — name that ROI before drawing the first box, and let it justify every "expensive" scheduler-side choice.
  • "Effective" utilization ≠ "allocated" utilization. A scheduler can hit 95% allocated and still be at ~56% effective if the gangs are scattered (each burns its GPUs producing a fraction of the training progress) or half-allocated (idle held GPUs). Weight your utilization metric by topology efficiency and subtract idle held GPUs — that's the number that maps to dollars, and it's the one the design must move.
  • Checkpointing is what makes preemption (and migration) cheap — mandate it. A preemption that costs the victim its whole 10-day run is politically impossible; one that costs a checkpoint interval (~5–30 min) is routine. The "jobs checkpoint" assumption is load-bearing for the priority SLA, for defrag, and for the preemptible/spot tier — which is why platforms make checkpointing the price of admission to the cheap tiers. Tier the cadence by preemption risk; don't make everyone checkpoint every 5 min "just in case."
