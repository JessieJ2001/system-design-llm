Designing a Real-Time Anomaly-Window Detector over Metric Streams

A cloud provider's host-metrics anomaly detector: for every (host, metric) pair, fire when max over the last 5 min crosses a static threshold or diverges from the rolling baseline by Nσ — 5 M hosts × 40 metrics = 200 M independent windows, 20 M samples/sec, an alert decision within 30 s, tolerate at most one missed sample, 30-day retention. We start from one process holding a monotonic deque per series in a giant in-RAM hash-map (the LC #239 "sliding-window maximum" structure, lifted directly) — which doesn't fit on one node, has no failure story, and GC-thrashes — and iterate to a sharded warm-standby aggregator fleet with bucketed window-max + t-digest baselines closed on an event-time watermark, fed by a partitioned Kafka log, with backpressure and load-shedding that keeps the decision fresh under a slow sink or a runaway host.

NALSD Walkthrough • ~50 min read • LC #239 (Sliding Window Maximum)

LC kernel. Sliding Window Maximum (LC #239). Textbook structure: a monotonic deque — a double-ended queue of indices whose values are monotonically decreasing front-to-back. On each new element: pop from the back while the back's value ≤ the new value, push the new index; pop from the front while the front's index has fallen out of the window; the window max is always the front. O(1) amortized per element, O(k) space for a window of k elements. Four properties that break at 200 M series: (1) state size + GC — one deque per series × 200 M = hundreds of GB of small heap objects; it doesn't fit one node and the garbage collector thrashes; (2) no failure story — the deque lives only in RAM, a crash erases it, and rebuilding it requires replaying the last 5 min of samples from somewhere durable (minutes); (3) assumes a single in-order stream — real metric samples arrive out of order (agent retries, clock skew) and you cannot wait for stragglers (30-s decision deadline), so you need an event-time watermark and a structure that tolerates late updates, not a strict monotonic deque; (4) max only — the "diverges from the median by Nσ" half of the spec needs a streaming quantile, which the deque does not provide. The C8 production cousin replaces the deque with a bucketed sliding-window max (a fixed ring of per-sub-window maxes — checkpointable, late-tolerant, off-heap) paired with a mergeable t-digest baseline, both closed on a watermark.

S0. Scenario & Requirements

A large cloud provider runs ~5 M physical and virtual hosts and emits ~40 metrics per host (CPU%, mem%, disk-util%, NIC bytes, dropped packets, request rate, error rate, GC pause, queue depth, …) — a sample roughly every 10 s per series. An SRE team configures rules of two shapes: a threshold rule (“page if max(cpu_util, last 5 min) > 95%”) and an anomaly rule (“page if max(latency, last 5 min) is more than 4σ above its 1-hour rolling baseline”). The detector consumes the firehose, maintains a sliding-window max and a rolling baseline per (host, metric), evaluates the rules, and emits an alert to the on-call bus within 30 s of the sample that triggered it. This is the detector, not the metrics store — long-term sample storage, ad-hoc PromQL, and dashboards belong to a separate time-series database (see #36). The load-bearing tension: 200 M windows must never go dark, but the state to maintain them is too big for one node, the firehose is too fast for one consumer, and a crash erases history that takes minutes to rebuild.

System class

Class: automation pipeline (stream-processing) with an algorithmic kernel — see functional-requirements.html. It ingests an unbounded stream, maintains per-key derived state, evaluates fixed rules, and emits events; it is not a queryable database (that class would bring an ad-hoc-query FR and a read-latency NFR — out of scope here). The pipeline framing puts ingest throughput, end-to-end freshness, state size, and recovery time at the centre; the algorithmic-kernel framing brings the C8 deque→sketch swap.

Functional requirements (cite functional-requirements.html)

  • Ingest samples. Accept (host_id, metric_name, ts, value) tuples at the firehose rate from host agents; acknowledge fast and fire-and-forget.
  • Maintain a sliding-window max. For each (host, metric), expose max(value) over the trailing 5-minute window, updated as samples arrive and as old samples age out.
  • Maintain a rolling baseline. For each (host, metric), maintain a robust location and spread estimate (median ± MAD) over a longer trailing window (1 hour) so the anomaly rule has something to compare against.
  • Evaluate rules. Per series, evaluate the configured threshold rule(s) and anomaly rule(s) on every window update; debounce so a continuously-firing condition produces one alert + periodic re-fires, not one per sample.
  • Emit alerts. Deliver (series, rule, fired_at, value, baseline) to the on-call alert bus within 30 s of the triggering sample; emit a matching resolved event when the condition clears.
  • Manage rules. SREs create / update / delete rules through a control-plane API; rule changes propagate to the detector within seconds.

Out of scope (named, not silently dropped): ad-hoc metric queries / PromQL — that is the TSDB's job (#36), this is a fixed-rule detector; raw-sample dashboards and long-term sample storage — same; alert routing / escalation / paging-policy logic — that is the on-call system downstream of our bus; auto-remediation; seasonal / diurnal baselining (“median at this time-of-day over the last 7 days”) — the baseline here is a flat 1-hour rolling window, with seasonal baselining flagged as a probe.

Flagged assumptions (would confirm with the interviewer): (1) rules are mostly static — they change by config push, not per-sample, so a rule lookup is a cached in-RAM read; (2) “tolerate at most one missed sample” means at-most-once sample delivery is acceptable — we may drop a sample (and count it) rather than block waiting for it; (3) the 5-minute window is wall-clock-ish — ±30 s of window-boundary slop is within tolerance for a 5-minute anomaly window; (4) the baseline window is 1 hour and is robust-statistics-based (median/MAD), not mean/stddev, because the outliers we are trying to detect would poison a mean; (5) a single internal tenant (the provider monitoring its own fleet) — multi-tenant SaaS monitoring is a probe.

ID scheme (cite identifier-schemes.html): series_id = hash64(host_id ‖ metric_name) — a 64-bit content hash of the natural key. Why: deterministic, so any collector or aggregator derives it (and thus the owning shard) with zero coordination and no lookup table; fixed-size; uniform, so shard balance is automatic; the series universe is huge and churns as hosts come and go, so a monotonic sequence (needs a coordinator) or a UUIDv7 (not derivable from the key) would both force a registry lookup on the hot path. Rejected: Snowflake / monotonic-sequence (coordinator + registry on the hot path); UUIDv7 (random — can't be re-derived from (host, metric), so routing needs a lookup). For alert_id we use a KSUID — time-sortable (alerts are listed newest-first) and forgery-resistant; collisions across the fleet are a non-issue.

S2. API contracts (cite api-design-patterns.html)

Three surfaces: the ingest protocol (agents → collectors, then collectors → Kafka), the control plane (SREs manage rules), and the output (the alert bus, push). Patterns — idempotency, errors, versioning, pagination — cite the canonical; only the scenario-specific shape is inline.

POST /v1/samples            Content-Encoding: lz4    body: [(series_id, ts, value), ...]
                            Host-Identity: <mTLS client cert>
                            -> 202 Accepted   (collector appended the batch to Kafka)

Per-endpoint SLO

  • POST /v1/samples — p99 ≤ 50 ms to 202 (just “appended to the durable log”; the agent does not wait for processing).
  • End-to-end sample-arrival → ALERT-emitted — p99 ≤ 30 s (the headline SLO; drives the watermark slack and the aggregator headroom).
  • PUT /v1/rules/{id} — p99 ≤ 200 ms; rule propagated to all aggregators within ~5 s (eventual; a rule change does not need to be atomic across the fleet).

S3. Back-of-Envelope — the ratios that govern every iteration

Two or three numbers shape the whole arc. No optimization technique appears here — the bucketed window-max, the t-digest, and the watermark are named as future moves but are not used in any calculation below.

The firehose

ingest_peak = 20,000,000 samples/sec.
At ~1 us CPU/sample, that is 20 core-seconds of work per wall-clock second -> needs >= 20 cores busy at all times.
At ~12 B/sample on the wire, that is 240 MB/s = 1.92 Gbps of compressed sample traffic.
Conclusion: bandwidth is cheap (one 12.5 Gbps NIC carries it); CPU is the gate; one 16-vCPU node can't do it; you need a fleet.

The state

Naive per-series state (the LC structures, exact):
  monotonic deque    <= 30 entries x 16 B (8 B value + 8 B ts)            = 480 B
  1-h baseline buffer 360 samples x 8 B (value; ts implicit by ring pos)  = 2,880 B
  hashmap slot + object headers                                          ~ 150 B
  per series                                                             ~ 3.5 KB
Total = 200M x 3.5 KB = ~700 GB payload  ;  ~1-1.4 TB resident with hashmap load factor + heap headers.
Conclusion: doesn't fit the 512 GB box on our menu; you'd reach for a 2 TB x2iedn-class box (~$13-27/hr) -- and a TB-scale
heap GC-pauses for seconds-to-minutes, which blows the 30-s alert SLO, and one box is one failure domain. Memory-capacity-bound,
hard wall. (The fix that shrinks ~3.5 KB -> ~1 KB is the C8 sketch swap -- Iteration 2 -- not used in this section.)

The clock

alert_SLO = 30 s. inter_sample_int = 10 s. So a series gets ~3 fresh samples inside one decision budget.
A node loss must not cost more than 30 s of darkness for the affected slice. Re-consuming the last 5 min of one shard's
Kafka partitions to rebuild a deque from cold = (5 min x per-shard rate) / replay_rate -- on the order of a minute or more,
which is > 30 s. => cold rebuild alone is not enough; we will need a warm standby (Iteration 1).
Every iteration's "recovery time" column is checked against this 30-s clock.

S4. Naive Architecture — the simplest thing that meets the FRs

One process. A single Kafka consumer (or a raw TCP listener) reads all 20 M samples/sec; a giant in-RAM HashMap<series_id, {monotonic_deque, baseline_buffer}> holds the state — the LC #239 deque per series, plus a 1-hour ring buffer per series for the median/MAD baseline; on each sample the process updates the deque (pop-back-while-smaller, push, pop-front-while-old), pushes the value into the ring buffer, recomputes the window max and the baseline median/MAD, evaluates the configured rule(s), and on a trigger POSTs the alert to a webhook. Rules are loaded once at startup from a config file. One machine. It satisfies every FR — ingest, window-max, baseline, rule eval, emit. The numbers tell us how far short of the NFRs it falls.

Architecture

  • ① Host agents → Listener — agents push (series_id, ts, value) tuples; no batching guarantee, no acknowledgement that matters, no durable log behind it — if the box is down the samples are gone.
  • ② Listener → HashMap — for each sample: hash(series_id), find the entry, deque.push(value, ts) with the monotonic pop-back/pop-front, ring_buffer.push(value); recompute window_max (deque front) and baseline = median ± MAD over the ring buffer.
  • ③ HashMap → Rule eval — read window_max and baseline for the touched series; check window_max > static_threshold and window_max > baseline_median + sigma_mult × baseline_mad.
  • ④ Rule eval → Alert webhook — on a trigger (debounced), POST the alert; on clear, POST a resolve.

Why it's broken (per failing NFR)

  • State capacity & durability — FAILS, hard. ~700 GB payload / ~1.4 TB resident does not fit the largest box on the menu; the only single boxes that hold it (2 TB x2iedn) cost ~$10–20k/mo, are a single failure domain, and have catastrophic GC. This is the shape-determining failure: the only way out is to shard the state across a fleet. Classified memory-capacity-bound.
  • Alert latency — FAILS. A TB-scale heap (or even a 512 GB one) GC-pauses for seconds to minutes; the 30-s end-to-end SLO is blown by a single pause. (And there's no durable buffer to absorb the burst while the process is paused.)
  • Availability — FAILS. One node, one AZ. Its loss is a total outage; its restart is cold with nothing durable behind it → the whole fleet is blind for ≥ 5 min. 99.9% on alert delivery from a single node is not achievable.
  • Ingest throughput — borderline. A 64-vCPU box just keeps up at ~25 M/s effective; no burst headroom; one GC pause and the backlog grows unbounded (nothing durable to spill to).
  • Correctness — FAILS implicitly. No durable log → a crash loses every in-flight sample for every series, not ≤ 1; and there's no watermark, so an out-of-order sample either corrupts the deque or is silently dropped.

Cost of naive “at NFR-target load”: ~$10–20k/mo for a 2 TB x2iedn-class box — and that box still fails availability, alert latency, and correctness. It's not a real option; it's off the menu. Note the contrast with #36, where the naive design was wasteful (it stored raw samples it didn't need) and the arc cut the bill ~14×: here the naive design is cheap but fragile, and the arc will spend ~2× more to buy availability, freshness, and correctness. A staff designer names which way the bill goes — and why — before drawing the first box.

Iteration 0 — Shard the state by series_id; partition the Kafka log; stateless collectors

System after this iteration

Connections and line-style legend. Solid slate = sync hot path; dashed gray = async / config broadcast; dashed red = hedge / retry (not used).

  • ① Host agents → Collectors — POST /v1/samples, LZ4-batched (~20 samples/batch every ~5 s per host); collector authenticates the host's mTLS identity. (The per-host token bucket arrives in Iteration 3.)
  • ② Collectors → Kafka — for each sample, partition = hash64(series_id) mod 32; produce to topic samples with acks=all, min.insync.replicas=2. The collector holds no state — it's a hash-and-relay.
  • ③ Kafka → Aggregator fleet — 16 aggregators, each statically assigned 2 of the 32 partitions (so a series always lands on the same aggregator). The consumer pulls batches and folds each sample into that shard's HashMap<series_id, {deque, 1-h buffer}> — still the exact LC structures; the sketch swap is Iteration 2.
  • ④ Aggregator → Alert bus — per touched series, evaluate the rules; on a debounced trigger emit ALERT to the bus, which both fans it to the on-call system and appends it to a 30-day store keyed by (series_id, fired_at).
  • ⑤ Rule service → Aggregators — SREs PUT rules; the service bumps a version and broadcasts the new rule set; each aggregator caches it in RAM and applies it on the next sample. Eventual (~5 s), not atomic across the fleet.

Delta vs Naive: the giant single hash-map is split into 16 disjoint shards by series_id; a durable, partitioned Kafka log now sits in front (so a burst is absorbed and a restarting aggregator can replay its partitions); collectors are a thin stateless tier that turns the agent fan-in into the right Kafka partitions. The per-series structures and the rule logic are unchanged.

Bottleneck

Naive's shape-determining failure is state capacity & durability: ~700 GB payload / ~1.4 TB resident does not fit any box on the menu, and the only single boxes that hold it are a single failure domain with catastrophic GC. (Ingest throughput is borderline and availability is zero, but both are symptoms of “it's one box” — the capacity wall is what forbids a one-box answer at all.) Classified memory-capacity-bound — the signal that picks this entry: a derived state size that exceeds a single node's RAM and grows with the key count, not the request rate. Per bottleneck-to-decisions#memory-capacity: revisit the partition key (can the state be split across nodes?), the data structure (can a bounded sketch replace the exact structure? — that's Iteration 2), and whether a durable log belongs in front (so a restart isn't a data loss). Iteration 0 takes the partition-key lever; the durable log comes along for free because once the state is sharded a restarting shard needs something to replay.

Alternatives considered (C4)

  • Hash-shard the state by series_id across a fleet, with a partitioned Kafka log in front — chosen. The state partitions perfectly (200 M independent series, no cross-series queries), series_id is a content hash so routing needs no lookup, and Kafka gives the durable replay-able buffer that makes a shard restart survivable. The natural answer for “per-key state too big for one node, stream input.”
  • One giant box (2 TB x2iedn, off-heap state) — rejected. Even with off-heap arrays it's one failure domain (a crash or a rolling deploy = a fleet-wide blind spot), it's ~$10–20k/mo, and it still has no burst buffer. (Constraint-flip: 🔀 if the fleet were ~20 M series instead of 200 M and the availability target were 99% not 99.9%, a single big box plus a cold standby would be a defensible MVP.)
  • Push the per-series state into an external KV store (Redis Cluster / Cassandra), keep the detector stateless — rejected. Every sample becomes a read-modify-write round trip to the store — 20 M RMW/s at ~0.5 ms each needs the store to do 20 M ops/s and the network round trip eats the latency budget; co-locating state with compute (the “stateful stream processor” pattern: Flink, Samza, Kafka Streams) is dramatically cheaper for this access shape. We'd only externalize state if the per-key state were huge or rarely touched — neither holds here.
  • Kinesis instead of Kafka — rejected. 1 MB/s write per shard → 240 MB/s / 1 MB/s = 240 Kinesis shards just for ingest, with per-shard limits and resharding ops dominating; Kafka's ~80 MB/s+/broker is ~80× the per-unit throughput. Cite storage-engines#heap-append (the durable-log engine).

The chosen move

Insert a partitioned Kafka topic samples (32 partitions — comfortably more than we need now, so we can grow aggregators without re-partitioning) keyed by series_id, and run a fleet of stateful aggregators, each statically assigned a contiguous subset of partitions. A series' samples always land on the same partition and therefore the same aggregator, which owns that series' state for life. Collectors become a thin stateless tier: receive agent batches, compute hash64(series_id) mod 32 per sample, produce. Sizing: 16 aggregators, each owning 2 partitions, so each holds 200M × 2/32 = 12.5M series ≈ 12.5M × 3.5 KB = ~44 GB of state (~88 GB resident with overhead — comfortable in 128 GB) and folds 20M × 2/32 = 1.25M samples/sec (well under the ~5 M/s/node planning capacity). 12 Kafka brokers: 240 MB/s × 3 (RF) = 720 MB/s of cluster write ÷ ~80 MB/s/broker ≈ 9, round up to 12 for headroom; topic retention 1 hour (enough for a cold aggregator to rebuild its 5-min deques and warm its 1-h baselines) → 240 MB/s × 3600 s × 3 (RF) ≈ 2.6 TB across the cluster ≈ ~220 GB/broker on gp3 — trivial. ~20 collectors (c7g.4xlarge): 5 M agents pushing a batch every ~5 s ≈ 1 M batched-push req/s ÷ ~50 k req/s/node ≈ 20. Cite sharding-strategies#hash (uniform hash partition — the access pattern is write-heavy-uniform over the key space, no inter-key queries) and storage-engines#heap-append (Kafka as the durable buffer).

Bottleneck

Iteration 0's open wound: a shard owner's loss blinds 1/16 of series for ~75 s while a replacement re-consumes 5 min of Kafka — and a rolling deploy multiplies that across all 16 nodes (~20 min of cumulative partial blindness). 75 s > the 30-s alert SLO for that slice. This is a recovery-time / availability failure: the design has no warm copy, so every recovery is a cold rebuild. It's adjacent to cold-start (the slice is “cold” after a loss) and to leader-loss in the failure taxonomy. Per bottleneck-to-decisions#cold-start: keep a warm replica so failover is a handoff, not a rebuild; checkpoint the state so even a cold start is bounded; fence the old owner to avoid two emitters. Iteration 1 does all three — one coherent move (“make recovery fast”): warm standby for the common single-node loss, S3 checkpoints for the rarer double loss / scaling / rebalance, lease-fencing for safety.

Alternatives considered (C4)

  • Warm standby (parallel consume) + S3 checkpoint + etcd lease-fencing — chosen. The standby is always caught up because it reads the same log — failover is sub-second and loses nothing; the checkpoint bounds the cold path; the lease makes “exactly one emitter” a single atomic fact. Standard for stateful stream processors that can't afford a rebuild gap.
  • Checkpoint-only (no warm standby) — rejected (but it's the cost knob). Half the aggregator fleet (16, not 32) — ~$5.8k/mo cheaper. But a node loss then means a cold restore (download ~44 GB + replay since checkpoint) ≈ ~1 min blind for that 1/16 — and node losses are frequent (spot reclaims, rolling deploys, hardware). 1 min > the 30-s SLO for that slice during a routine event. We keep the standby; in S6 we call this out as the explicit cost-vs-availability trade.
  • Replicate state synchronously between the pair on every sample (chain/quorum) instead of parallel-consume — rejected. Doubles the per-sample work with a network hop and couples the pair's latency — a slow standby slows the primary. Parallel-consume decouples them: the standby falling behind is a detectable, fence-able condition, not a primary-side stall.
  • Lean on Kafka Streams / Flink's built-in changelog + checkpointing instead of rolling our own — a real option. Flink's keyed state + RocksDB-backed checkpoints + exactly-once would give much of this for free; we'd trade operational simplicity for less control over the failover latency and the on-heap layout. For a 200 M-key, 30-s-SLO workload most teams would reach for a managed stream processor here; we describe the mechanism directly so the trade is visible — the C8 sketch swap in Iteration 2 is the same idea inside whichever framework you pick.

The chosen move

For each shard, run a primary aggregator (in AZ-a) and a warm standby (in AZ-b) that consume the same Kafka partitions in parallel; only the primary holds the emit role. The primary holds a short-TTL lease (a few seconds) in etcd; if it loses the lease — crash, network partition, a GC pause longer than the TTL — the shard manager fences it (revokes emit, so a paused-then-resumed old primary can't double-fire) and promotes the standby, which already has the state and just re-reads a couple of seconds of Kafka to be sure it's at the head: failover ≈ ~seconds, zero data loss. A fresh standby is then started; if a warm one isn't on hand it cold-restores from the latest S3 checkpoint and replays Kafka from the checkpoint's offsets. Checkpoints: every ~30 s the primary writes its serialized state + consumed offsets to s3://aggr-checkpoints/{shard}/{epoch}.snap, keeping the last 3 (lifecycle-expire older). With the exact structures a full ~44 GB snapshot streams in ~3–4 min at a background ~200 MB/s — longer than the 30-s cadence, so checkpoints are best-effort and the cold-restore RPO is effectively a few minutes of baseline state (the 5-min deque is always rebuilt from Kafka regardless). That's the soft spot Iteration 2 closes: fixed-size flat structures make a snapshot a memcpy, and incremental checkpoints make it seconds. AZ placement: primary and standby in different AZs, and the checkpoint bucket is cross-AZ-replicated — a single-AZ loss takes out at most the primaries (or standbys) in that AZ, and their partners in surviving AZs promote.

What breaks (C5; cite failure-modes.html)

  • Split-brain on a partition. Primary and standby can't see each other; both think they should emit. Defence: the etcd lease — emit is gated on holding it; a partition can't grant two leases; the fenced side drops its alerts (and counts them). Blast radius: bounded to a brief overlap window (< the lease TTL); detection: the shard manager sees the lease change; recovery: automatic on partition heal.
  • Lease-expiry storm under a control-plane blip. If etcd itself hiccups, many primaries can lose their leases at once → a wave of promotions. Defence: lease TTLs longer than a typical etcd blip; promotions are cheap (warm standbys) and idempotent (the new primary fences and continues); the shard manager rate-limits concurrent promotions. Blast radius: a transient emit gap on the affected shards; detection: promotion-rate metric spikes.
  • Checkpoint storm. All 8 shards snapshotting at the same wall-clock instant → an S3 PUT burst + a synchronized CPU/IO spike across the fleet → latency wobble. Defence: stagger the checkpoint phase across shards (shard i checkpoints at t mod period == i × period/8); cap snapshot bandwidth. Blast radius: none if staggered; detection: checkpoint-duration metric.
  • Standby lag. The standby's consumer falls behind (its node is slower / busier) → a promote would yield a stale window. Defence: a standby-lag metric per shard; if lag exceeds a threshold the promote path skips the warm standby and goes straight to cold-restore-from-checkpoint (slower but correct); chronically-lagging standbys are rescheduled. Blast radius: a slower-than-ideal failover for that shard; detection: the lag metric.

Bottleneck

Three intertwined symptoms of one root cause — the exact LC structures are too heavy, too fragile, and too order-sensitive for a 200 M-series, 30-s-SLO stateful tier: (1) slow checkpoints — serializing ~44 GB of ~12.5 M variable-size objects takes minutes, so the cold-restore RPO is minutes of baseline state (Iteration 1's soft spot); (2) GC pressure — ~40 M live small objects per node, plus churn as hosts come and go, means sub-second-but-real pauses and CPU spent collecting, eating into the latency margin and the per-node throughput; (3) order-sensitivity — the textbook monotonic deque assumes in-order arrival, but real metric streams reorder (agent retries, clock skew), and with a 30-s decision deadline you can't wait for stragglers — the deque has no answer, so “tolerate ≤ 1 missed sample, nothing silent” isn't actually met. This is the C8 moment: the production cousin of the LC #239 deque. Per bottleneck-to-decisions#memory-capacity: revisit the data structure — can a bounded sketch replace the exact structure? Yes, for both halves: a bucketed window-max for the sliding max, a t-digest for the baseline quantiles, both closed on a watermark.

Alternatives considered (C4)

  • Bucketed window-max (10 × 30-s buckets) + 2× rolling t-digest baseline + 15-s watermark, all in flat off-heap arrays — chosen. Fixed size, O(1) update, memcpy checkpoint, no GC, late-tolerant within the watermark, and the digest is mergeable so primary and standby converge and a re-shard can merge digests. The window is quantized to 30 s (the window is “the last 270–300 s” depending on phase) — well within the ±30-s tolerance the FRs flag for a 5-minute anomaly window.
  • Fixed-bucket histogram (64 log-spaced buckets, ~256 B) instead of a t-digest — rejected (but it's the cheaper option). Smaller, simpler, mergeable, O(1) insert — at the cost of bucket-resolution quantiles (fine for a coarse Nσ test, worse if rules later want a precise p95). We pick the t-digest for robustness to skewed / multimodal metric distributions and because it gives all quantiles cheaply; if RAM ever got tight, the histogram is the drop-in downgrade. Cite sketches#t-digest (and #dd-sketch for the relative-error variant).
  • P² estimator (median only, ~5 markers, ~120 B) + EWMA mean/var for σ — rejected. Tiny, but not mergeable (so primary and standby can drift) and gives only the one quantile you ask for — no headroom for richer rules. We note it as the absolute-minimum-state option.
  • Keep the exact deque + buffer but move them off-heap (arenas, manual layout) — rejected. Kills the GC pause but not the slow checkpoint (still variable-size, still ~44 GB to serialize) and not the order-sensitivity. Half a fix.
  • Bigger buckets (60 s × 5) — rejected. Halves the window state and the digest-touch rate but doubles the window slop to ±60 s — out of tolerance for a 5-min window. (Smaller buckets — 15 s × 20 — would be tighter but doubles the per-series window state for a precision the FRs don't need.)

The chosen move

Sliding-window max → bucketed window-max. Divide time into fixed 30-s buckets; per series keep a ring of 10 buckets covering the last 300 s, each holding the max of the values that fell in it (plus the ring's head index and the head bucket's end-timestamp). Update: bucket[floor(ts/30) mod 10] = max(…, v) if the bucket is still open. Read: window_max = max over the buckets overlapping [now−300s, now]. ~100 B/series, O(1) update, O(10) read, and — the point — a flat array indexed by a dense series index, so a checkpoint is a memcpy and there are no heap objects to collect. Late sample within 30 s of now? It still updates its bucket. Later than the 15-s watermark? Dropped, late_dropped++.

1-hour exact buffer → 2× rolling t-digest. Maintain two t-digests per series — one for the current 30-minute half-hour, one for the previous — and rotate (drop the older, start a fresh current) every 30 min; a baseline query merges the two and reads the median and the MAD (the latter from a small companion digest of |v − running_median|, or computed from quantiles). At compression ~25 centroids each that's ~2 × ~400 B ≈ ~800 B/series — ~3.5× smaller than the 2.88 KB exact buffer, but the size win is the lesser point: it's that the digest is mergeable (primary and standby, both inserting the same samples, stay in agreement; a re-shard merges digests), bounded regardless of sample rate (a runaway host emitting 1 kHz can't blow the per-series buffer — the digest caps at 25 centroids), and all-quantile (median, MAD, p95, … all available without sorting a buffer). Cite sketches#t-digest.

Watermark close. Per partition, wm = max(event_ts seen) − 15 s; a bucket whose end < wm is sealed — no more updates — and the rule is evaluated on the now-final window_max. A sample arriving with ts < wm is too late: dropped + counted, never silently absorbed-or-lost. 15 s of watermark slack on a 30-s budget leaves 15 s for the collector → Kafka → consumer → rule → bus path — comfortable. Re-size the fleet: state is now ~1 KB/series → ~200 GB total → ~4 nodes for RAM (off-heap, ~50 GB usable per 128 GB node) and ~4 nodes for CPU (20 M/s ÷ ~5 M/s), so 8 primary + 8 standby = 16 aggregators, each owning 4 of the 32 partitions (~25 GB state, ~2.5 M samples/s — 2× CPU headroom). What breaks (C5; cite failure-modes.html)

  • Clock skew on host agents. An agent whose clock is 2 min fast stamps samples in the future; max(ts) jumps, the watermark jumps, and buckets seal early — later (correctly-timed) samples for other series on the same partition now look “late.” Defence: clamp accepted ts to [now − W_baseline, now + small] at the collector; track per-partition watermark from clamped timestamps; alert on hosts with persistent skew. Blast radius: a partition's watermark wobble; detection: a clock_skew{host} metric.
  • Poison sample. A malformed or NaN value, or a metric whose units changed mid-stream, corrupts a bucket-max or a digest. Defence: validate at the collector (finite, in a sane range for the metric); a per-series “digest looks degenerate” guard that resets that series' baseline and emits a metric rather than firing garbage anomalies. Blast radius: one series; detection: a validation-reject metric.
  • Watermark stall. A partition stops receiving samples (all its hosts went quiet, or a collector died) → max(ts) stops advancing → the watermark freezes → buckets never seal → no alerts for that slice — a false silence. Defence: a wall-clock fallback — if no sample for > some bound, advance the watermark on processing time and emit a partition_idle metric (an idle partition is itself worth alerting on). Blast radius: 1/32 of series go quiet; detection: the idle metric.
  • Bucket aliasing across a checkpoint restore. A cold-restored aggregator must reconstruct “which bucket is current” from the checkpoint's epoch + the replayed samples; an off-by-one would shift the window. Defence: the checkpoint stores the head bucket's absolute end-timestamp, not just an index; restore recomputes the ring position from wall-clock + replayed max(ts). Tested in the recovery drill.

Iteration 3 — Backpressure & load-shedding: bounded alert queue, per-host rate cap, lag-skip

System after this iteration

Connections and line-style legend. Solid slate = sync hot path; dashed gray = async / out-of-band metric; dashed red = backpressure signal (429) / shed.

  • ① Host agents → Collector — same protocol; now the collector also clamps ts, validates value, and meters per host.
  • ② Collector → Kafka — only the within-budget samples are produced; samples shed by the token bucket increment rate_capped{host}.
  • ③ Kafka → consumer (lag-skip) — the consumer watches per-partition lag; if the head sample is > ~25 s old (i.e., it can't be acted on within the 30-s SLO) it fast-forwards the offset to now − W − slack — re-reading only the last ~5 min, enough to rebuild the window — and increments lag_skip{partition}. Freshness over completeness, by the SLO's own terms.
  • ④ Aggregator → Alert bus — alerts go through a bounded in-RAM queue (~100 k); on overflow they spill to a local disk-backed queue (durable, replayed when the sink recovers) with a hard cap (~10 M); beyond that, for a given (series, rule) only the latest “still-firing” entry is kept and intermediate re-fires are coalesced — a first-fire for a series is never dropped.
  • ⑤ Alert bus → aggregator (429 / slow) — the sink signalling backpressure (429, rising latency) is what triggers the spill-to-disk; the aggregator never blocks its consumer on a slow sink.
  • ⑥ Collector → Host-owner alert — a host that's persistently over its sample budget gets an auto-alert to its owning team (“your agent is emitting 100× normal — fix it”); the cap protects everyone else's freshness while the human loop fixes the source.

Delta vs Iteration 2: three bounded shedding paths — a per-host token bucket at the collector, a lag-skip in the aggregator's consumer, and a bounded-then-spilling-then-coalescing alert queue on the way out — plus collector-side ts/value sanitisation. No new tier, no fleet-size change; +a small local SSD per aggregator for the spill queue.

Bottleneck

Three paths to a runaway backlog, all of which currently let one problem bleed into a wider blast radius: (a) the alert sink slows or 429s → alerts queue in the aggregator → unbounded RAM growth → OOM or the consumer stalls → that whole shard goes blind — a slow downstream takes out the detector; (b) a misconfigured agent on one host emits 100× normal (a debug build, a metric-cardinality bug) → that host's 40 series cluster on one or two partitions → that aggregator's consumer lag grows → its other series go stale — one bad host blinds a 1/8 slice; (c) a transient aggregator slowdown (a deploy, a checkpoint, a GC—rare now—a CPU spike) → lag spikes → samples pile up in Kafka → on recovery it's processing 2-min-old samples it can't act on within the SLO anyway. Classified backlog-growth — the signal: a queue depth (Kafka lag, the alert queue) that grows faster than it drains; the cliff: OOM / consumer stall / a freshness SLO blown the instant you fall behind. Per bottleneck-to-decisions#backlog: bound every queue; shed at the edge (admission control on the producer); shed by recency (drop or coalesce the stale work, not the fresh work); signal the source (so the human loop can fix it). One coherent move — “shed, don't topple” — applied at all three choke points.

Alternatives considered (C4)

  • Bounded queues + disk-spill + coalesce-on-overflow (alerts) + per-host token bucket (ingest) + lag-skip (consumer) — chosen. Every path that could grow without bound is given a bound and a defined behaviour at that bound that preserves the thing that matters (first-fires, freshness) and sheds the thing that doesn't (re-fires, stale samples), while emitting a metric so the bound being hit is visible. Standard “graceful degradation” engineering.
  • Just provision for the worst case (size the fleet for a fleet-wide debug-build burst) — rejected. The worst case for a misconfigured agent is unbounded (an infinite loop emitting metrics); you can't provision against it — you can only cap it. And provisioning for it would multiply the bill for an event that should be fixed at the source in minutes.
  • Drop the oldest alerts on overflow (FIFO eviction) instead of coalescing re-fires — rejected. The oldest alert might be a series' first-fire — the one the on-call must see. Coalescing keeps every series' “is it firing right now” truth and drops only the redundant ticks; first-fires are sacrosanct.
  • Block the consumer on a slow sink (apply backpressure all the way to Kafka) — rejected. That trades a bounded alert delay for an unbounded freshness loss on every series in the shard — far worse. The detector must stay current; alerts that can't be delivered now are spilled and delivered later, but the windows keep updating.

The chosen move

(a) Bounded alert queue → disk spill → coalesce. The aggregator's outbound alert path is a bounded in-RAM ring (~100 k entries); when the sink is keeping up this is nearly empty. When the sink slows / 429s, the ring spills to a local disk-backed queue (durable, ordered, replayed when the sink recovers) with a hard cap (~10 M entries ≈ hours of alerts). Beyond that hard cap — a sink down for hours — the queue switches to coalesce mode for re-fires: for each (series, rule) it keeps only the latest still-firing entry and drops intermediate re-fires; a first firing for any (series, rule) is never coalesced away. Degradation contract: a slow sink degrades the alert stream to “delayed, and re-fire-deduplicated” — never “lost a first alert” and never “aggregator OOM.” Cite failure-modes#slow-dependency.

(b) Per-host token bucket at the collector. Each host gets a sample budget (e.g., ~200 samples/s — 40 metrics × a generous 5/s — tier-able by host class so a busy DB host with 200 metrics gets a bigger bucket). Over budget → sample down 1-in-K, increment rate_capped{host}, and (on a sustained breach) auto-alert the host's owning team. This is “tolerate ≤ 1 missed sample” generalised: a pathological producer is throttled, not allowed to starve everyone else's freshness; the human loop fixes the source. (Contrast #20's rate limiter, where the limit is the product; here it's self-defence.)

(c) Consumer lag-skip. The aggregator's Kafka consumer watches per-partition lag; if the head sample is > ~25 s old — it physically cannot be acted on within the 30-s SLO — the consumer fast-forwards its offset to now − W − slack (~5 min back), re-reading only enough to rebuild the window, and increments lag_skip{partition}. An explicit freshness-over-completeness trade that the “decision ≤ 30 s” and “tolerate ≤ 1 missed sample” NFRs license — and a chronically-skipping partition is a capacity alarm (page on the lag_skip rate), not a silent data loss. No fleet-size change — this iteration adds logic and a small local spill volume (~50 GB gp3 per aggregator), not capacity. Cost ≈ unchanged at ~$27k/mo (+~$60/mo for the spill volumes).

What breaks (C5; cite failure-modes.html)

  • Spill-queue exhaustion. The sink down for many hours → the ~10 M-alert disk cap is hit → coalesce mode → re-fire granularity is lost (you still learn “X is firing,” just not every tick) but first-fires survive. Detection: a spill_depth metric; recovery: drain on sink recovery (ordered replay), or, if the on-call console is down too, the alerts are still on disk for forensic replay.
  • Rate-cap false positive. A legitimately high-cardinality host (a busy hypervisor with 200 metrics) gets throttled because its budget is the default. Defence: per-host-class budgets (tiers), and the auto-alert to the owner doubles as the path to request a higher tier; the cap errs on the side of protecting the fleet. Detection: rate_capped on a host that's known-large.
  • Lag-skip masking a real under-provisioning. If lag_skip fires constantly, the fleet is too small — but skipping keeps it from looking broken (it stays current by skipping). Defence: page on the lag_skip rate, not just on lag; lag-skip is a relief valve, not a capacity strategy.
  • Agent retry storm (averted). Worth noting what we don't do: the agent's POST is fire-and-forget — on a 429/503 it drops the batch and counts it, it does not retry. 5 M agents retrying a degraded collector tier would be a self-inflicted DDoS; the “tolerate ≤ 1 missed sample” budget is precisely what buys us the right to not retry. Cite failure-modes#retry-storm for the anti-pattern we're sidestepping.

Probe iterations (the interviewer pulls the thread — beyond the 5-Core budget)

Each in a <details> block; same NFR-driven structure, one level deeper.

Probe A — Multi-region (the provider runs hosts in ~20 regions)

Bottleneck. A single global aggregator fleet would have to ingest samples cross-region: ~240 MB/s of inter-region traffic ≈ 240 MB/s × 86,400 × 30 ≈ 622 TB/mo × ~$0.02/GB ≈ ~$12k/mo in egress alone — and a region-down event would still need the global fleet to keep working, making the home region a single point of failure for everyone's alerting. Classified cross-region.

Move. Per-region aggregator fleets — a series belongs to one host in one region, so the natural partition is region-then-series_id; there are no cross-region series. A thin global alert aggregator simply unions the per-region alert streams (alerts are tiny — KB/s, not MB/s) so the on-call sees one feed. DR: a region's checkpoints replicate to a bucket in a designated buddy region (~200 GB × cross-region-storage ~$0.02/GB-mo ≈ trivial), so a whole-region loss is reconstituted elsewhere in minutes (acceptable for a region-down). Cite cross-cutting#dr and #cells (region = cell). Constraint that drives it: the 622 TB/mo egress + the SPOF-region risk; both vanish under per-region fleets.

Probe B — Multi-tenant SaaS monitoring (Datadog-style, external customers)

Bottleneck. Now the hosts belong to external customers, not the provider — so one customer's 50 k hosts must not starve another's 50, a runaway tenant must not blind the fleet, and each tenant brings their own rules. The per-class NFR additions for a multi-tenant pipeline (per-tenant fairness, per-tenant quotas, noisy-tenant isolation) become load-bearing.

Move. (1) Per-tenant token buckets at the collector (the Iteration 3 mechanism, keyed by tenant rather than host) plus weighted-fair consumer scheduling so a big tenant gets proportional, not unbounded, share. (2) Per-tenant quotas (max series, max ingest rate — billed; over-quota → reject + a billing event). (3) Cells: shard the aggregator fleet by tenant-group so a runaway tenant takes down one cell, not all; route a tenant to its cell by a stable hash of tenant_id. (4) The rule store becomes per-tenant; the broadcast becomes a per-tenant subscription. Cite cross-cutting#cells. This is genuinely a different product (its FR list gains tenant onboarding, billing, per-tenant dashboards) — mostly out of scope here, but the cell + per-tenant-quota shape is the part that transfers.

Probe C — Hot host / hot series within a shard

Bottleneck. A single host with 10 k custom metrics, or one metric sampled at 1 kHz, makes that host's series cluster on one or two Kafka partitions → that aggregator runs hot (CPU on t-digest inserts; state for the extra series). Classified hot-key (a partition-key value, or a small set of them, that draws disproportionate load).

Move. Detect a hot partition (samples/s/partition >> the mean), then spread — re-hash that host's series across a wider key (hash64(host_id ‖ metric_name ‖ sub_bucket) where sub_bucket = hash(metric_name) mod M), distributing them over M partitions instead of one; or pin the hot host to a dedicated mini-shard. Note: the Iteration 3 per-host rate cap already absorbs most of this — a 1 kHz metric is exactly what the token bucket is for; this probe is the residual case of a host that's legitimately high-cardinality (a big hypervisor) and so gets a big budget. Cite bottleneck-to-decisions#hot-key.

Probe D — Doing “30-day retention” properly

What we keep 30 days. The alerts — (series_id, fired_at, kind, value, baseline, resolved_at) — are tiny: even 1 M alerts/day × ~200 B × 30 ≈ ~6 GB in the LSM alert store, TTL-expired automatically. Optionally a daily rolled-up baseline per series (the day's median/MAD) for post-hoc “was this normal a week ago?” investigations — 200 M series × ~16 B/day × 30 ≈ ~96 GB to S3 ≈ ~$2/mo.

What we do not keep. The raw 20 M/s sample stream — 240 MB/s × 30 days ≈ ~620 TB — is a TSDB's job; the metrics-storage system (#36) owns long-term raw and rolled-up samples. This detector only needs minutes-to-hours of samples (the window + the baseline + a Kafka replay buffer). Cite cost-shapes#telemetry (the “keep alerts + rollups, not raw” pattern) and cross-link #36 for where the raw samples actually live.

Architecture

Degradation contract

When the alert sink is down → alerts spill to local disk, replayed on recovery; if the spill hard cap is hit, re-fires for an already-firing series are coalesced (you still learn “X is firing” — just not every tick) — a first-fire is never dropped. When a primary aggregator dies → its warm standby promotes in ~s; if the standby is also gone → cold-restore from the latest S3 checkpoint + Kafka replay (~30 s blind for that 1/8 of series). When Kafka is degraded (below min.insync.replicas) → producers get errors → collectors drop the batch + emit produce_failed (no agent retry storm); on recovery, normal flow resumes — the dropped samples are the “≤ 1 missed” budget. When a shard's consumer falls > 30 s behind → lag-skip to the head (freshness over completeness). When a host runs away → its samples are 1-in-K'd and its owner is auto-alerted. The system never silently loses an alert, never exceeds the 30-s decision SLO by waiting on stale data, and never lets one slow downstream or one bad host blind a slice of the fleet — it sheds, counts, and stays fresh. Cite failure-modes#slow-dependency + #backlog-overflow.

Operations (C6)

  • Metrics (SLIs → NFRs). Per-partition Kafka consumer lag (→ alert-latency); samples/s/aggregator and per-series-op p99 — bucket update + digest insert (→ throughput, latency); end-to-end alert latency p99, sample-ts → alert-emitted (→ the headline SLO); late_dropped/s, rate_capped host count, lag_skip/s, produce_failed/s, alert-spill depth (→ the “≤ 1 missed sample” budget — these are the budget; watch them); checkpoint duration + age (→ recovery-time); standby-lag per shard + promotion count + partition_idle count (→ availability + false-silence).
  • Rollout. Canary one aggregator carrying a shadow copy of 1–2 partitions — it consumes, computes, and evaluates rules, but its alerts go to a /dev/null sink that is diffed against the live aggregator's stream for 24 h; then promote partition-by-partition (the shard manager reassigns; the new binary becomes the standby first, then is promoted, so a bad binary is a sub-second rollback away). Rollback trigger: alert-stream divergence, a regression in end-to-end latency p99, or any rise in the shed-counters.
  • Migration (naive → sharded). Stand up the new fleet consuming the same Kafka topic in shadow; diff the alert streams for 24 h; cut the alert bus over; keep the old monster box running as a fallback for a week, then decommission. The Kafka topic is created first (collectors dual-write to it and to the old listener during the transition).
  • Headroom. +50% on the aggregator fleet: 8 primaries could absorb ~12 partitions' worth if several standbys are promoted during a deploy + an AZ loss; CPU at 2.5 M/s/node has 2× headroom to ~5 M/s; RAM is ~25 GB of 128 GB. Kafka and collectors carry their own ~50% over their steady-state need (the 2× ingest burst). Reason: routine spot reclaims + rolling deploys + a single-AZ loss happening together is a Tuesday, not a black-swan.

Real-system comparison

The closest production cousins are Uber's M3 metrics platform (M3DB + M3 Aggregator: a tier of stateful aggregators that fold a metric firehose into rolled-up windows, sharded by series, with a coordinator handing out shard assignments — the same “co-locate state with compute, shard by series, replicate for failover” shape we derived) and Netflix's RAD outlier-detection work (robust streaming statistics — median/MAD-style baselines — over high-cardinality metric streams, which is exactly the t-digest-baseline half here). Both confirm the load-bearing choices: a durable partitioned log in front, per-series sharding, replicas for failover, and sketch-based (not exact-buffer) baselines. Where a real team would diverge from this page: most reach for a managed stream processor (Flink / Kafka Streams) rather than rolling the failover and checkpointing themselves — we derived the mechanism directly so the trade is visible, but in practice you'd carry less of it. And a real fleet's metric cardinality is messier than “5 M × 40” — tag explosions and custom metrics make the per-host rate cap (Iteration 3) and the in-shard hot-key split (Probe C) load-bearing from day one, not afterthoughts. Sources: see cribs/_sources.md for this scenario (Uber M3, Netflix RAD, Cloudflare per-host metrics).

Anticipated pushbacks

Stress tests against the derivation above — depth rehearsal, not interview rehearsal. If a question can't be answered in 3–6 sentences from the text above, the page is missing a probe.

What if the fleet were 10× tomorrow — 50 M hosts, 2 B series, 200 M samples/s? (scale flip)

State is fine — 2 B × ~1 KB = ~2 TB, ~40 aggregators for RAM. CPU is the gate: 200 M/s ÷ ~5 M/s/node ≈ ~40 nodes for compute, +50% headroom → ~64 primaries + 64 standbys. Kafka grows to ~120 brokers (2.4 GB/s × RF=3 ÷ ~80 MB/s); 32 partitions is too few — you'd re-partition to 256 (the reason we picked 32, not 8, at the start: cheap headroom for exactly this). The new bottleneck shifts from “state” (Iter 0–2) to network-bandwidth at the Kafka brokers and the collector tier (2.4 GB/s is ~19 Gbps — past one NIC, so collectors and brokers both need to be more numerous or fatter). The +50% headroom buys roughly a year of organic host growth before re-provisioning is load-bearing.

Why not just use Flink / Kafka Streams / a managed stream processor and skip the bespoke failover? (alt-engine)

You probably would, and the page says so (Iter 1, last alternative). Flink's keyed state + RocksDB-backed incremental checkpoints + exactly-once would give you the sharding, the checkpointing, and the failover for free; the bucketed-window-max and the t-digest become a user-defined keyed-state function inside it. The trade: less control over the failover latency (Flink's checkpoint-and-restore is barriered — a restore re-reads from the last barrier, which at this volume can be tens of seconds, vs. our warm-standby ~seconds) and over the on-heap layout (Flink's RocksDB state is on local disk, not flat off-heap RAM — great for huge state, an unnecessary serialization tax for our ~1 KB/series). At 200 M keys with a 30-s SLO, a team that owns its operational story end-to-end might keep the warm-standby; a team that doesn't would absolutely take Flink. The derivation — shard by series, durable log in front, replica for failover, sketch not buffer — is identical either way; cite storage-engines#kv-engine for the RocksDB-state-backend trade.

What changes if this is a regulated environment — the metrics include something HIPAA/PCI-scoped? (regulatory)

Most of it doesn't change — metric values are numbers, and the design already scrubs PII-bearing metric names/tags at the collector (X2). What gains: (1) an audit log of rule changes and alert deliveries (who saw which alert when) — cite cross-cutting#privacy; (2) encryption-at-rest on the Kafka topic, the checkpoints, and the alert store (SSE + KMS, key rotation policy) — mostly already there; (3) data-residency: the multi-region probe's per-region fleets become a requirement, not an optimization, and the buddy-region for DR must be in-jurisdiction; (4) retention controls: the 30-day alert TTL becomes a policy-enforced minimum-and-maximum, with legal-hold exceptions. Naming what stays the same matters: the window/baseline math, the sharding, the failover, and the shedding logic are untouched — regulation adds governance around the data, not new bottlenecks.

An entire AZ goes dark right now — what's the blast radius and the recovery sequence? (failure)

Primaries and standbys are placed in different AZs, so an AZ loss takes out roughly half the primaries (and half the standbys) but never both halves of a shard. Sequence: the shard manager sees those primaries' etcd leases expire (~3–5 s); it fences them and promotes their (surviving-AZ) standbys — ~seconds, zero data loss, because each standby was reading the same Kafka partitions. Now those shards have no standby; the manager schedules fresh standbys on surviving-AZ capacity, which cold-restore from S3 + replay (~30 s each to warm, but the shard is already serving via its new primary throughout). Kafka (RF=3 across AZs) keeps quorum on 2 AZs. The on-call sees a brief promotion-storm metric spike and a temporary “no standby” window for half the shards. Cite failure-modes#leader-loss + #lease-expiry-storm.

Where does the ~$30k/mo bill actually come from, and which knob moves it most? (cost)

~42% is the aggregator fleet (16 r6i.4xlarge), and ~half of that — ~$5.8k/mo — is the warm-standby replica; ~30% is the collector tier, ~24% Kafka, the rest (S3, alert store, spill, bus) is rounding error. So the single biggest knob is the standby: dropping it to checkpoint-only saves ~$5.8k/mo (~21% of the bill) at the cost of turning single-node-loss recovery from ~seconds into ~30 s of partial blindness — which violates the SLO for that 1/8 slice during a routine spot reclaim, so we don't. The second-biggest is the collector count, which is driven by the agent push cadence — if agents pushed every 15 s instead of 5 s you'd cut collectors ~3× (~$5.6k/mo), at the cost of ~10 s more ingestion latency, which eats hard into the 30-s budget — also a no. Cite cost-shapes#stream-processing.

How do you cut over from the existing single-box detector without a blind spot? (migration)

Phase 1: create the Kafka samples topic; have the collectors (or a shim in front of the old box) dual-write — samples go both to the old listener and to Kafka. Phase 2: stand up the new sharded + standby aggregator fleet consuming Kafka in shadow — it computes windows, baselines, and rule evals, but emits to a /dev/null sink that's diffed against the old box's alert stream; run this for 24 h and chase down every divergence (usually a tie-break in debouncing or a window-edge case). Phase 3: cut the alert bus over to the new fleet; keep the old box running (now ignored) for a week as an instant rollback; then decommission and stop the dual-write. Rollback trigger at any phase: alert-stream divergence beyond a tiny tolerance, or any regression in end-to-end alert latency p99. The risk is entirely in Phase 2's diff — if the shadow stream matches for a day, the cutover is boring.

The on-call says “we stopped getting alerts for a chunk of hosts” — where do you look first? (monitoring/debug)

The SLI dashboard, in order: (1) partition_idle count and per-partition Kafka produce rate — if a partition's producer rate cratered, the problem is upstream (a collector died, or a whole rack of agents went quiet) → this is real silence, page the upstream owner; (2) per-partition consumer lag and lag_skip rate — if lag is high, an aggregator is behind (under-provisioned, GC—rare now—or a hot host) → check rate_capped for a runaway host on that partition; (3) promotion-count and standby-lag for the shards covering those hosts — a recent promotion + a lagging new standby explains a transient gap; (4) end-to-end alert latency p99 broken down by shard — localizes which 1/8 is affected; (5) the alert-spill depth and produce_failed rate — if alerts are being generated but not delivered, the sink is the problem, not the detector. Each signal maps to a specific failure mode from the What-Breaks blocks and a runbook entry; the design's whole “count everything you shed” discipline exists so this triage is a dashboard read, not a guess.

S7. Key Takeaways

  • Per-key state too big for one node → shard the state, put a durable log in front. The naive single-box detector fails on capacity first (200 M × ~3.5 KB ≈ 700 GB > any sane box), and that's the failure that forbids a one-box answer at all; the rest (no failover, GC, no burst buffer) are symptoms. The fix is structural: hash-shard by series_id, partitioned Kafka behind it so a restart isn't a data loss.
  • “Survives a node loss” isn't free — you pay for it in a warm replica. A cold rebuild (re-consume 5 min of Kafka) is ~75 s — over the 30-s SLO. A warm standby (parallel-consume the same partitions, shadow the state, promote on a fenced lease) makes failover ~seconds, and it costs a 2× aggregator fleet. Name the cheaper option (checkpoint-only, ~1 min recovery) and why you didn't take it.
  • The LC #239 deque is a teaching structure, not a production one. At 200 M series it's too heavy (GC, slow to checkpoint), too fragile (RAM-only), and too order-sensitive (assumes in-order). The cousin: a fixed bucket-ring of sub-window maxes (exactness traded for ±30-s slop, in exchange for memcpy checkpoints and zero GC) + a mergeable t-digest for the baseline quantiles the deque never gave you + a watermark to bound the decision in time.
  • A 30-s decision SLO is a license, not just a constraint. It licenses lag-skip (a sample > 25 s old can't be acted on in time, so skip it), the 15-s watermark (don't wait for stragglers), and ±30-s window slop — all of which make the design cheaper and simpler. Read your latency NFR for what it permits, not just what it forbids.
  • Bound every queue; shed by recency; signal the source; count everything. A slow alert sink → bounded queue → disk spill → coalesce re-fires (never first-fires). A runaway host → per-host token bucket + auto-alert its owner. An aggregator behind → lag-skip + a lag_skip-rate alarm. The “≤ 1 missed sample” budget is the sum of named, counted shed-counters — never a silent loss.
  • Not every NALSD arc cuts the bill. #36's arc cut cost ~14× because its naive design was wasteful (kept raw samples it didn't need). This arc spent ~2–3× more because its naive design was cheap but fragile — the delta is the price of availability + a durable log; the C8 sketch swap then clawed half of it back. A staff designer says which way the bill goes, and why, before drawing the first box.
  • Co-locate state with compute unless the state is huge or rarely touched. Externalizing per-series state to Redis/Cassandra would turn every sample into a network RMW — 20 M RMW/s plus a round trip in the latency budget. The stateful-stream-processor pattern (state in the aggregator's RAM, durable log for recovery) is dramatically cheaper for a hot, small-per-key access shape; reach for an external store only when that stops being true.
