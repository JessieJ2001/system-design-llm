Message Queues

The shock absorber of distributed systems — decouple producers from consumers, buffer traffic spikes, and guarantee delivery even when downstream services are down.

45 min read · Intermediate

What Is It?

A message queue is a middleware component that enables asynchronous communication between services. Instead of Service A calling Service B directly and waiting for a response, Service A drops a message into a queue and moves on. Service B picks up the message whenever it is ready. This simple indirection unlocks three superpowers: decoupling (services don't need to know about each other), buffering (the queue absorbs traffic spikes), and load leveling (consumers process at their own pace).

There are two fundamental messaging patterns. In point-to-point (producer-consumer), each message is consumed by exactly one consumer — think of a task queue where workers grab jobs. In publish-subscribe (pub/sub), a message is broadcast to all interested subscribers — think of an event bus where multiple services react to the same event. Most modern systems (Kafka, Pulsar, SQS+SNS) support both patterns, but the underlying semantics and tradeoffs differ significantly.

At its core, a message queue is a durable, ordered buffer. Producers enqueue messages at the tail, and consumers dequeue from the head. The queue persists messages to disk (or replicates them) so that no data is lost if a consumer crashes. Once a consumer successfully processes a message and sends an acknowledgement, the queue deletes or marks it as consumed. If the consumer fails, the message becomes visible again for retry — this is the foundation of at-least-once delivery.

The Producer-Consumer Pattern: A Restaurant Kitchen Analogy

To really understand what "decoupling" means, imagine a restaurant. Waiters are the producers — they take orders from customers. Cooks are the consumers — they prepare the food. Between them sits a rail (the queue) where waiters clip order tickets.

Without the rail, the waiter would have to walk into the kitchen, hand the order directly to a cook, and wait until the cook finishes before going back to take more orders. If the cook is busy, the waiter stands there idle. If the waiter is fast, the cook gets overwhelmed with shouted orders. This is tight coupling — the waiter's speed is limited by the cook's speed, and vice versa.

With the rail, the waiter clips the ticket and immediately goes back to take more orders. The cook picks up tickets from the rail in order, one at a time, at their own pace. This is decoupling. The waiter doesn't know (or care) which cook picks up the ticket. The cook doesn't know (or care) which waiter wrote it. They operate independently.

Now observe what happens under different conditions:

  • Cook is slow (backpressure): Tickets pile up on the rail. The rail gets full. Eventually, the manager tells waiters to slow down ("we're at capacity"). This is backpressure — the system signals upstream to reduce the rate of incoming work.
  • A cook goes home (fault tolerance): The remaining cooks simply pick up more tickets from the same rail. No tickets are lost. The rail absorbs the temporary reduction in capacity. This is exactly what happens when a consumer in a consumer group crashes — the remaining consumers take over its partitions.
  • Dinner rush (load leveling): Orders spike from 10/minute to 50/minute. The cooks can't go 5x faster, but the rail can hold 100 tickets. The rail absorbs the spike, and cooks work through the backlog at a steady pace. Customers wait longer, but no orders are lost and cooks don't crash.
  • Adding a new cook (horizontal scaling): A new cook joins and starts picking tickets from the rail. No changes needed for the waiters. Throughput increases immediately. This is why consumer groups in Kafka scale so well — just add more consumers.

Every concept in message queues maps directly to this analogy: the topic is the rail for a specific type of order (appetizers vs. entrees), partitions are separate sections of the rail (one per cook), offsets are the ticket numbers, and acknowledgment is the cook removing the ticket after plating the dish.

How Messages Flow

Producers push messages into the queue (horizontal pipe). Messages accumulate when consumers are slower than producers. Consumers pull messages and acknowledge them for deletion.

How It Works

Lifecycle of a Message

The full lifecycle of a message, step by step:

  1. Produce: The producer serializes the message (JSON, Protobuf, Avro) and sends it to the queue broker with a topic/queue name and optional partition key.
  2. Enqueue: The broker assigns the message an offset or message ID and appends it to the end of the queue or log segment. In Kafka, this means appending to a partition's commit log.
  3. Persist: The broker writes the message to disk and (if configured) waits for replication to N replicas before acknowledging the producer. With acks=all in Kafka, the message is durable across broker failures.
  4. Consumer poll/push: Consumers either poll the broker for new messages (Kafka, SQS) or the broker pushes messages to registered consumers (RabbitMQ). Polling gives consumers backpressure control; push gives lower latency.
  5. Process: The consumer deserializes the message and executes business logic — send an email, update a database, trigger a workflow.
  6. Acknowledge: After successful processing, the consumer commits the offset (Kafka) or sends an ACK (RabbitMQ/SQS). This tells the broker the message has been handled.
  7. Delete / compact: The broker either deletes the message immediately (RabbitMQ), marks it invisible (SQS after visibility timeout), or retains it until the retention period expires (Kafka log retention). Log compaction in Kafka keeps only the latest value per key.

Delivery Guarantees

When a producer sends a message and a consumer processes it, things can go wrong at every step: the network can drop the message, the consumer can crash mid-processing, or retries can cause duplicates. Delivery guarantees describe how the system behaves in these failure scenarios. There are exactly three levels:

Guarantee Mechanism Failure Behavior Example
At-most-once Fire and forget — producer sends once, no retry Message may be lost, but is never delivered twice UDP packets, basic publish without ACK, logging pipelines where loss is acceptable
At-least-once Producer retries until ACK received from broker Message is never lost, but may be delivered multiple times Kafka (default config), SQS, RabbitMQ with publisher confirms
Exactly-once Idempotent producers + transactional consumers No duplicates, no loss (within the queue system) Kafka with enable.idempotence=true + transactions

Why Exactly-Once Is Hard: The Two Generals Problem

To understand why true exactly-once delivery is impossible in a distributed system, consider this classic thought experiment:

Two generals command armies on opposite sides of a city. They must agree on a time to attack simultaneously — if only one attacks, they lose. They can only communicate by sending messengers through enemy territory. The messenger might be captured (the message might be lost).

  1. General A sends: "Attack at dawn."
  2. Did General B receive it? A doesn't know. So B sends back: "I confirm: attack at dawn."
  3. Did General A receive the confirmation? B doesn't know. So A sends: "I confirm your confirmation."
  4. Did B receive that? B doesn't know. And so on... infinitely.

No matter how many rounds of confirmation you add, you can never be sure the last message was received. This is the fundamental impossibility: in any system where messages can be lost, you cannot guarantee that both sides have the same state after a single exchange. This is why true exactly-once delivery is impossible — the sender can never be 100% sure the receiver processed the message exactly once without any further communication, and that further communication itself might fail.

How Kafka Achieves "Effectively Exactly-Once"

Kafka doesn't solve the Two Generals Problem (nobody can). Instead, it uses two mechanisms that together eliminate duplicates within Kafka:

1. Idempotent Producer

When you set enable.idempotence=true, each producer gets a unique Producer ID (PID), and each message it sends gets a sequence number (0, 1, 2, 3...). The broker stores the last sequence number per (PID, partition). If the producer retries a message (because the ACK was lost in the network), the broker sees the duplicate sequence number and silently discards it: 

# What happens inside the Kafka broker:
# Producer PID=7 sends to partition 3:
#   Msg(PID=7, seq=42, data="order-123")  → Broker appends. Last seq for (7,3) = 42.
#   Msg(PID=7, seq=42, data="order-123")  → DUPLICATE! seq 42 already seen. Discard.
#   Msg(PID=7, seq=43, data="order-456")  → New. Broker appends. Last seq for (7,3) = 43.

# The producer doesn't need to know if the first send succeeded —
# it can always retry safely. The broker deduplicates.

2. Transactional Writes

For consume-transform-produce pipelines (read from topic A, process, write to topic B), Kafka supports transactions. The producer begins a transaction, writes to one or more partitions, then commits. If the producer crashes before committing, the transaction is aborted and all writes are rolled back. Consumers configured with isolation.level=read_committed only see messages from committed transactions — they never see partial or aborted writes.

Together, these two mechanisms guarantee: each message is written exactly once to Kafka (idempotent producer) and consume-transform-produce pipelines are atomic (transactions). But critically, this only applies within Kafka. If your consumer calls an external API (charge a credit card, send an email), Kafka cannot prevent that side effect from happening twice. You must make external side effects idempotent yourself, using techniques like idempotency keys or deduplication tables.

Backpressure

Backpressure is what happens when a consumer can't keep up with the rate of incoming messages, and the system needs a way to signal "slow down" upstream. Without backpressure, unbounded queues grow until memory is exhausted and the entire system crashes.

The Math of Unbounded Growth

Consider a concrete scenario:

  • Producer sends 10,000 messages/sec
  • Consumer processes 2,000 messages/sec
  • Queue grows by 8,000 messages/sec

That's 480,000 messages per minute, or 28.8 million messages per hour. At 1 KB per message, the queue accumulates 28.8 GB per hour. A broker with 64 GB of RAM runs out of memory in about 2 hours. When the broker's memory is exhausted, it either crashes (losing all queued messages) or starts rejecting writes from all producers across all topics — not just the one with the slow consumer. One slow consumer can take down the entire messaging infrastructure.

TCP Flow Control as an Analogy

Backpressure isn't a new idea — TCP has been doing it since 1981. In TCP, the receiver advertises a receive window: the amount of buffer space it has available. When the receiver processes data slowly, the buffer fills up, and the window shrinks. When the window reaches zero, the sender pauses until the receiver frees up space. This is backpressure at the transport layer — the receiver controls the sender's rate without any explicit "slow down" message.

Message queues need the same concept at the application layer. Without it, a fast producer will bury a slow consumer.

Backpressure Strategies

1. Block the Producer When the Queue Is Full

RabbitMQ uses a mechanism called credit flow. Each connection is given a credit limit (e.g., 200 messages). When the credit is exhausted and the broker hasn't processed the messages, the producer's connection is blocked — it simply can't send more messages until credit is replenished. This propagates backpressure all the way to the original caller.

2. Drop Messages (Acceptable for Some Workloads)

For metrics, logs, or telemetry data, losing some messages is acceptable. The queue can be configured with a maximum size, and new messages are dropped when the queue is full. This is the simplest strategy and avoids any risk of memory exhaustion. Kafka's buffer.memory setting on the producer side does this — when the producer's local buffer is full, new sends either block or throw an exception depending on max.block.ms.

3. Spill to Disk

Kafka's approach: messages are always written to disk as log segments. The broker doesn't keep all messages in memory — it relies on the OS page cache for fast reads. This means the queue can hold terabytes of messages (limited by disk, not RAM). As long as the consumer eventually catches up, the system remains stable. The tradeoff: reading old messages from disk is slower than reading from memory.

4. Rate-Limit Producers

Apply a rate limiter (token bucket or sliding window) at the producer's entry point. Kafka supports producer-side quotas (producer_byte_rate) that limit how fast a producer can send data. When the quota is exceeded, the broker throttles the producer by delaying responses.

Consumer Groups & Partitions

In Kafka, a consumer group is a set of consumers that cooperate to consume a topic. Each partition in the topic is assigned to exactly one consumer within the group. This is how Kafka achieves parallel processing while maintaining ordering within each partition.

Worked Example: Assignment and Rebalancing

Suppose topic orders has 6 partitions (P0 through P5), and consumer group order-processors has 3 consumers (C1, C2, C3). Kafka's partition assignor distributes partitions evenly:

  • C1 → P0, P1
  • C2 → P2, P3
  • C3 → P4, P5

Each consumer reads messages from its assigned partitions independently. Messages within P0 are processed in order by C1. Messages across P0 and P2 have no ordering guarantee relative to each other — they are processed concurrently by C1 and C2.

What Happens When a Consumer Crashes?

Suppose C3 crashes at time T1. Here is the timeline:

TimeEventC1 PartitionsC2 PartitionsC3 PartitionsC4 Partitions
T0Normal operationP0, P1P2, P3P4, P5—
T1C3 crashesP0, P1P2, P3(dead)—

At T3, the two surviving consumers each take on one extra partition. They were handling 2 partitions each; now they handle 3 each. Messages on P4 and P5 are not lost — they just weren't consumed during the ~30 seconds between the crash and the rebalance. Once the rebalance completes, C1 and C2 start reading P4 and P5 from the last committed offset.

At T4, a new consumer C4 joins. Kafka triggers another rebalance and reassigns P4 and P5 back to C4, restoring the original 2-partitions-per-consumer distribution.

The Critical Rule: Max Consumers = Number of Partitions

Each partition can be assigned to at most one consumer within a group. This means:

  • 6 partitions, 3 consumers → each consumer gets 2 partitions. All consumers are busy.
  • 6 partitions, 6 consumers → each consumer gets 1 partition. Maximum parallelism.
  • 6 partitions, 8 consumers → 6 consumers get 1 partition each, 2 consumers sit completely idle. Adding more consumers beyond the partition count provides zero benefit.

This is why the number of partitions is such a critical design decision. It sets the ceiling for consumer parallelism. If you create a topic with 3 partitions, you can never have more than 3 active consumers in a group — even if you need more throughput later. Increasing partition count requires careful planning (Kafka supports adding partitions but not reducing them).

Rebalancing Protocols

Eager Rebalancing (Legacy)

The original protocol. When a rebalance is triggered, all consumers stop processing, release all partitions, and wait for the group coordinator to reassign them. During this window (seconds to minutes), no messages are processed. This is the "stop the world" approach.

Incremental Cooperative Rebalancing (Kafka 2.4+)

Only the partitions that need to move are revoked and reassigned. Consumers continue processing their remaining partitions during the rebalance. This reduces the downtime from "all consumers stop" to "only the migrating partitions stop." For a group with 50 consumers where one consumer dies, instead of stopping all 50 consumers, only the 2 partitions being migrated are paused. This is a dramatic improvement for large consumer groups.

Kafka Architecture: The Commit Log

Kafka's architecture is built on one core idea: a commit log. Understanding Kafka means understanding four concepts: topics, partitions, offsets, and segments.

Topic

A topic is a named category of messages — similar to a database table. Examples: user-events, orders, payments, click-stream. Producers write to a topic. Consumers read from a topic. A topic can have many producers and many consumer groups reading from it simultaneously.

Partition

A topic is split into N partitions. Each partition is an ordered, immutable sequence of messages — a commit log. When a producer sends a message, it goes to one specific partition (determined by the partition key, or round-robin if no key is specified).

Partitions are the unit of parallelism. Different consumers in a consumer group read different partitions simultaneously. More partitions = more parallelism = higher throughput. But partitions also add overhead. Understanding these costs is critical for choosing the right partition count.

Partition Overhead: Why More Isn’t Always Better

1. Each partition is a separate directory on disk. Every partition is stored as its own folder on the broker’s filesystem (e.g., /kafka-logs/user-events-0/, /kafka-logs/user-events-1/, etc.). Inside each directory are the segment .log and .index files. This means each partition requires its own set of open file handles. A broker with 10,000 partitions might have 20,000–30,000 open files (at least 2–3 per partition: active log segment, index file, and timeindex file). Operating systems have file descriptor limits (often 65,536 by default), and hitting that limit causes broker failures. Beyond file handles, each partition’s active segment requires a memory-mapped buffer, which consumes kernel page cache. With thousands of partitions, the OS spends more time managing memory mappings and less time doing useful I/O.

2. Each partition has its own leader and replicas. Kafka replicates each partition across multiple brokers for fault tolerance (typically replication.factor=3). For each partition, one broker is the leader (handles all reads and writes) and the others are followers (replicate data from the leader). This means every partition generates its own replication traffic: followers continuously fetch new messages from the leader. Here’s the math for a concrete example — 10,000 partitions, 5 brokers, replication factor 3:

  • Leaders per broker: Each partition has exactly 1 leader. 10,000 partitions ÷ 5 brokers = 2,000 leaders per broker.
  • Total copies per broker: Each partition exists as 3 copies (1 leader + 2 followers) = 10,000 × 3 = 30,000 total copies. Spread evenly: 30,000 ÷ 5 = 6,000 copies per broker.
  • Follower replicas per broker: Of those 6,000 copies, 2,000 are leaders, so 6,000 − 2,000 = 4,000 are follower replicas.
  • Fetch connections: Each follower replica maintains an active fetch connection to its leader (on another broker). That’s 4,000 active fetch connections per broker, each consuming network bandwidth and CPU.

In short: replication factor multiplies the per-partition overhead. Doubling the replication factor from 2 to 4 doesn’t just double storage — it also doubles the number of fetch connections, network traffic, and disk I/O on every broker.

When a broker fails, every partition it led needs a new leader. If Broker 2 dies and it led 2,000 partitions, the Kafka controller must elect 2,000 new leaders. Each leader election involves updating cluster metadata in ZooKeeper (or the KRaft metadata log) and notifying all affected brokers. With older ZooKeeper-based Kafka, this could take seconds to minutes because ZooKeeper writes are serialized. During this window, those 2,000 partitions are unavailable for writes. This is why Kafka documentation recommends keeping partitions per broker under ~4,000 (ZooKeeper mode) or ~20,000 (KRaft mode).

3. Each partition contributes to rebalancing complexity. Within a consumer group, each partition is assigned to exactly one consumer. When a consumer joins or leaves the group (due to scaling, deployment, or crash), Kafka must rebalance — reassign partitions across the remaining consumers. Here’s why more partitions makes this worse:

  • Stop-the-world pause (eager rebalancing): In Kafka’s default eager rebalancing protocol, all consumers in the group stop processing, release all their partitions, and then get a fresh assignment. If a group has 100 partitions and one consumer of 5 leaves, all 100 partitions pause — not just the 20 that were on the departed consumer. Processing halts completely for the duration of the rebalance (typically 5–30 seconds depending on partition count and consumer session.timeout.ms). More partitions = longer pause.
  • Cooperative/incremental rebalancing: Kafka 2.4+ introduced the CooperativeStickyAssignor, which avoids the full stop. Only the partitions that actually need to move are revoked and reassigned. But even here, the assignment algorithm must compute the optimal mapping of P partitions to C consumers — and with very high partition counts (e.g., 50,000), the computation itself takes noticeable time.
  • State rebuild cost: For stateful consumers (like Kafka Streams), each partition corresponds to a local state store (e.g., a RocksDB instance for a KTable). When a partition moves to a new consumer, that consumer must rebuild the state store from the changelog topic — which can take minutes for large stores. More partitions = more state stores = more potential rebuild work during rebalancing.

Why Not Use Consistent Hashing to Reduce Partition Movement?

If you’ve read about consistent hashing, you might wonder: why doesn’t Kafka place partitions and consumers on a hash ring, so that adding or removing a consumer only moves ~K/N partitions (where K is the total partitions and N is the number of consumers)? It seems like this would avoid the stop-the-world problem entirely. The answer is that partition–consumer assignment has constraints that consistent hashing doesn’t handle well:

  1. Even distribution matters more than minimal movement. Consistent hashing is famously uneven without virtual nodes — some consumers could get 3 partitions while others get 15. Kafka doesn’t technically require exactly equal counts, but it strongly prefers the maximum imbalance to be at most 1 partition (e.g., 10 partitions across 3 consumers = two get 3, one gets 4). Here’s why that matters:
    • Partitions are the unit of parallelism and throughput. Each partition can push roughly the same message rate. A consumer with 10 partitions does ~10× the work of a consumer with 1 partition. If consistent hashing gives one consumer 15 partitions and another 3, you’ve created a 5× load skew — the overloaded consumer becomes a bottleneck, falls behind (increasing lag), and may get kicked out of the group for being too slow (missing max.poll.interval.ms), triggering yet another rebalance.
    • Consumers are typically identical machines. Unlike a cache cluster where different nodes might have different capacities, consumer group members are usually the same instance type with the same CPU/memory. There’s no reason to give one more work than another.
    • Stateful consumers (Kafka Streams) amplify the problem. Each partition maps to a local RocksDB state store. A consumer with 15 partitions needs ~5× the memory and disk of one with 3. Uneven distribution means you must provision every consumer for the worst-case load, wasting resources on the lightly-loaded ones.
    Adding virtual nodes to consistent hashing improves balance but still can’t guarantee a max imbalance of 1. Kafka’s RoundRobinAssignor and CooperativeStickyAssignor do guarantee this, which is why they’re preferred.
  2. Co-partitioning constraints. In Kafka Streams, if you join two topics (e.g., orders and payments), partition N of both topics must go to the same consumer — otherwise the join can’t happen locally. Consistent hashing assigns each partition independently, so it can’t enforce “partition 7 of topic A and partition 7 of topic B must land on the same consumer.” Kafka’s assignors handle this explicitly.
  3. The real problem is the rebalance protocol, not the algorithm. The stop-the-world pause in eager rebalancing happens because the protocol requires every consumer to revoke all partitions before reassignment — regardless of which assignment algorithm is used. Even if you plugged consistent hashing into the assigner, every consumer would still pause during the revoke phase. The CooperativeStickyAssignor fixes this at the protocol level: it only revokes the partitions that actually need to move, which already achieves what consistent hashing would give you (minimal movement) while also respecting the constraints above.
  4. Small N makes the benefit negligible. Consistent hashing shines when N is large (hundreds of servers in a cache cluster). Consumer groups typically have 5–50 consumers. With 30 partitions and 5 consumers, a rebalance moves at most ~6 partitions — the overhead of consistent hashing (the ring, virtual nodes, lookups) isn’t justified for such small numbers.

In summary: CooperativeStickyAssignor already gives you minimal partition movement (comparable to consistent hashing) plus exact balance and co-partitioning support. It’s the recommended assignor for all new Kafka deployments.

Practical guideline: Start with the number of partitions equal to the expected peak number of consumers in the largest consumer group (since a partition can only be read by one consumer in a group). If you need 12 consumers for throughput, start with 12 partitions. You can always increase partition count later (Kafka supports this), but you can never decrease it without recreating the topic — so don’t over-provision “just in case.” A common sweet spot for most workloads is 6–30 partitions per topic.

Offset

Each message in a partition gets a sequential ID called an offset: 0, 1, 2, 3, and so on. Offsets are never reused. The consumer tracks its position in the partition using the offset. After processing message at offset 42, the consumer commits offset 43 (meaning "I've processed everything up to 42; give me 43 next").

This is powerful because:

  • Replay: To reprocess all messages, simply reset the consumer's offset to 0. The messages are still there.
  • Resume: If a consumer crashes and restarts, it reads its last committed offset and picks up where it left off.
  • Multiple consumers: Two different consumer groups each maintain their own offset. One might be at offset 1,000,000 while the other is at offset 500. They don't interfere with each other.

Segment

Each partition is stored on disk as a sequence of segment files. A segment is typically 1 GB (configurable via log.segment.bytes). As the active segment fills up, Kafka creates a new one. Old segments can be deleted independently when they fall outside the retention period (e.g., 7 days). This is much more efficient than scanning the entire partition to find expired messages.

Each segment consists of two files:

  • .log file: The actual message data, appended sequentially.
  • .index file: A sparse index mapping offsets to byte positions in the .log file, enabling fast lookups by offset.

What Is a Broker?

A broker is a server process that sits between producers and consumers and manages the queue. Think of it like a post office: producers drop off letters (messages), the post office (broker) stores and organizes them, and consumers come to pick them up. The producer and consumer never talk to each other directly — the broker is the intermediary that decouples them.

Concretely, a broker is a running instance of the queue software (e.g., one Kafka process, one RabbitMQ node). It accepts incoming messages over the network, writes them to disk, tracks which messages have been consumed, and serves messages to consumers on request. A single broker can manage many topics/queues simultaneously.

In production, you run a cluster of multiple brokers (typically 3–9 for Kafka) for fault tolerance and throughput. The brokers coordinate with each other: they replicate data so no single broker is a single point of failure, and they distribute partitions across the cluster so the load is shared. If one broker crashes, the others continue serving — clients automatically reconnect to a surviving broker. The cluster appears as one logical system to producers and consumers, even though it’s multiple processes running on multiple machines.

Where Do Brokers Fit?

The diagram above shows the cluster as one green box. But inside that box are individual broker servers. Each broker is a separate machine (or process) that owns some partition leaders and some replicas. The diagram below zooms in to show how three partitions (P0, P1, P2) with replication factor 3 are distributed across three brokers. Each partition has one leader (marked with ★) that handles all reads and writes, and two followers that replicate data from the leader. Leaders are spread evenly so no single broker is a bottleneck.

Producers and consumers only talk to the partition leader. Replicas exist solely as backups. If Broker 1 crashes, Broker 2 or 3 promotes its P0 replica to become the new P0 leader — clients automatically reconnect to the new leader with no data loss (as long as min.insync.replicas was satisfied before the crash).

Leaders and Replicas Store the Same Data

Yes — the P0 leader on Broker 1 and the P0 replicas on Brokers 2 and 3 contain the exact same messages. A replica is a full, byte-for-byte copy of the partition’s log segments. The difference is purely about role, not content:

  • Leader: The only copy that accepts reads and writes from producers/consumers. All client traffic for that partition goes here.
  • Follower (replica): Continuously fetches new messages from the leader to stay in sync. It serves no client traffic — it exists only as a standby backup. (Kafka 2.4+ added an opt-in replica.selector.class feature that lets consumers read from the closest replica to reduce cross-datacenter latency, but by default replicas are read-only backups.)

How Replicas Stay in Sync: The ISR

Replication is asynchronous by default — the leader doesn’t wait for every replica to catch up before acknowledging the producer. This means replicas can briefly lag behind the leader. To track which replicas are caught up, Kafka maintains an ISR (In-Sync Replica) list for each partition.

A replica stays in the ISR as long as it has fetched all messages within replica.lag.time.max.ms (default: 30 seconds). If a replica falls behind — say its broker is under heavy garbage collection or has a slow disk — Kafka removes it from the ISR. When it catches back up, it’s re-added.

This matters because of the acks setting on the producer:

  • acks=0 — Producer doesn’t wait for any acknowledgment. Fastest, but messages can be lost if the leader crashes before writing to disk.
  • acks=1 — Producer waits for the leader to write the message. If the leader crashes after acknowledging but before replicas fetch it, the message is lost.
  • acks=all — Producer waits for all ISR replicas to confirm they’ve received the message. No data loss as long as at least one ISR member survives. This is the safest setting.

The setting min.insync.replicas (e.g., 2) prevents writes when too few replicas are in sync. With acks=all and min.insync.replicas=2, if only the leader is alive (both replicas are lagging), the producer gets an error rather than writing to a single copy — protecting against data loss at the cost of temporary unavailability.

Log Compaction

For some topics, you don't want time-based retention — you want to keep the latest value per key forever. This is what log compaction does. Consider a topic user-profiles with key=userId:

Before compaction:

Offsets 0 and 1 are removed because offsets 2 and 4 contain newer values for the same keys. The compacted log now acts as a snapshot of the latest state for every user. A new consumer can read the compacted log to rebuild the full current state without processing the entire history. This is commonly used for changelog topics, materialized views, and CDC (Change Data Capture) pipelines.

Dead Letter Queues

A Dead Letter Queue (DLQ) is a separate queue where messages go after they've failed processing a specified number of times. It's the safety net that prevents a single bad message from blocking an entire pipeline.

The Flow

  1. Consumer reads a message from the main queue.
  2. Processing fails (bad format, missing dependency, bug in consumer code, downstream service unavailable).
  3. The message is retried — up to max_retries times (e.g., 3), typically with exponential backoff (wait 1s, then 2s, then 4s).
  4. After all retries are exhausted, the message is moved to the DLQ instead of being retried again.
  5. The consumer commits the offset and moves on to the next message. The partition is unblocked.
  6. A separate process (or a human operator) inspects DLQ messages, diagnoses the problem, fixes the bug or data issue, and replays the messages back to the original queue.

The Poison Message Problem

A poison message is a message that always fails processing, no matter how many times you retry. Examples: a message with invalid JSON that can't be deserialized, a message referencing a database record that doesn't exist, or a message that triggers a code bug (null pointer exception on an unexpected field).

Without a DLQ, here's what happens: the consumer reads the poison message, fails, retries, fails, retries, fails... forever. Because Kafka preserves ordering within a partition, the consumer can't skip the poison message — it must process messages in offset order. Every message after the poison message on that partition is stuck. If the consumer group has 6 partitions and one gets a poison message, you've lost 1/6 of your processing capacity indefinitely.

With a max retry count and DLQ configured, the poison message is quarantined after 3 failures, and the consumer moves on. The partition resumes normal processing within seconds instead of being blocked for hours.

DLQ Implementation in Code

MAX_RETRIES = 3

def process_with_dlq(consumer, dlq_producer, dlq_topic):
    for message in consumer:
        retries = 0
        while retries < MAX_RETRIES:
            try:
                process(message)       # Business logic
                consumer.commit()      # Mark as processed
                break                  # Success — move to next message
            except Exception as e:
                retries += 1
                if retries >= MAX_RETRIES:
                    # All retries exhausted — send to DLQ
                    dlq_producer.send(
                        dlq_topic,
                        key=message.key,
                        value=message.value,
                        headers={
                            "original-topic": message.topic,
                            "original-partition": str(message.partition),
                            "original-offset": str(message.offset),
                            "failure-reason": str(e),
                            "retry-count": str(retries),
                        }
                    )
                    consumer.commit()  # Advance past the poison message
                else:
                    time.sleep(2 ** retries)  # Exponential backoff

Notice that the DLQ message includes metadata about where the original message came from and why it failed. This metadata is essential for debugging — when an engineer inspects the DLQ, they need to know which topic and partition the message came from, and what error occurred.

Ordering Guarantees

Not all ordering is created equal. There are three distinct levels, and choosing the right one is a fundamental design decision that affects throughput, complexity, and correctness.

Three Levels of Ordering

LevelGuaranteeThroughputExample System
Total order Every consumer sees every message in the exact same order Low — limited to one partition/consumer Kafka topic with 1 partition, Raft log
Partition order Messages within a partition are ordered; messages across partitions have no ordering guarantee High — scales with number of partitions Kafka (default), Kinesis
No order Messages may arrive in any order Highest — maximum concurrency SQS Standard, many pub/sub systems

Why Global Ordering Doesn't Scale

If all messages must be totally ordered, they must all go through a single partition. A single partition means a single consumer (only one consumer can read a partition in a consumer group). Throughput is capped at what one consumer can handle. If that consumer processes 1,000 messages/sec, your system tops out at 1,000 messages/sec — no matter how many servers you add.

This is why Kafka uses partition-level ordering as its default model. You get ordering where you need it (within a partition) and parallelism everywhere else (across partitions).

Design Pattern: Choose the Right Partition Key

The partition key determines which messages end up in the same partition (and therefore are ordered relative to each other). The key insight: messages that must be ordered relative to each other must share the same partition key.

E-commerce example: For an order processing system, partition by order_id. All events for order #123 (created, paid, shipped, delivered) go to the same partition and are processed in order. Events for different orders can be processed in parallel across different partitions:

Partition Key Pitfalls

  • Hot partitions: If one key generates disproportionate traffic (e.g., a celebrity user, or a "default" account), its partition becomes a bottleneck. All other partitions sit idle while the hot partition is overwhelmed. Solution: use a compound key (e.g., user_id + date) to spread load, or add a random suffix for non-ordering-sensitive messages.
  • Too-coarse key: Partitioning by country when the US has 80% of traffic means one partition gets 80% of the work. Choose finer-grained keys.
  • Too-fine key: Partitioning by request_id (unique per request) means every message goes to a potentially different partition. You get maximum parallelism but zero ordering guarantees for related messages.

When to Use / When NOT to Use

Use When

  • Async processing: The caller doesn't need an immediate result — email sending, image resizing, analytics ingestion.
  • Decoupling services: You want producers and consumers to evolve independently without direct dependencies.
  • Load leveling: Traffic is bursty and you need to smooth out spikes so downstream services aren't overwhelmed.
  • Event-driven architecture: Multiple services need to react to the same event (order placed, user signed up).
  • Guaranteed delivery: You can't afford to lose messages, even if consumers are temporarily down.

Don't Use When

  • Synchronous response required: The user is waiting for the result (e.g., "is this username available?"). Use direct RPC instead.
  • Sub-millisecond latency: Queues add at minimum a few milliseconds of overhead. For hot-path latency-sensitive operations, use in-process calls or shared memory.
  • Simple request-response: If you don't need durability, retry, or decoupling, a queue adds unnecessary complexity. A plain HTTP call is simpler and easier to debug.
  • Tiny payload, high frequency: Millions of tiny messages per second may be better served by a streaming protocol or in-memory pub/sub (Redis Streams, NATS).

Choose queues over direct RPC when:

  • The consumer can tolerate seconds (or minutes) of delay.
  • You need to survive consumer downtime without losing work.
  • You want to add new consumers without changing the producer.

Classic vs. Modern Approaches

Classic: Producer-Consumer Queue

The traditional message queue (RabbitMQ, ActiveMQ, IBM MQ) works like a mailbox. Producers put messages in; consumers take them out. Once consumed and acknowledged, the message is gone. This model is simple and works well for task distribution, but it has limitations: messages can only be consumed once, there is no replay capability, and scaling consumers requires careful coordination to avoid duplicate processing.

Modern: Log-Based Queues (Kafka)

Kafka changed everything by treating the queue as an append-only log rather than a mailbox. Messages are not deleted after consumption — they are retained for a configurable period (days or weeks). Each consumer tracks its own offset (position) in the log. This single architectural choice unlocks three game-changing capabilities:

  • Replay: A new consumer can start from the beginning and reprocess all historical messages. Essential for rebuilding state, backfilling, and debugging.
  • Multiple consumers: Different consumer groups each maintain their own offset, so the same data stream feeds analytics, search indexing, and real-time alerts simultaneously.
  • High throughput: Sequential disk writes (append-only) are faster than random I/O, enabling millions of messages per second per broker.

For a detailed breakdown of Kafka's internal architecture — topics, partitions, offsets, segments, and log compaction — see the Kafka Architecture: The Commit Log section above.

Exactly-Once Semantics Evolution

Early queues offered at-most-once (fire and forget) or at-least-once (retry until ACK) delivery. Kafka 0.11 (2017) introduced idempotent producers and transactional messaging, enabling exactly-once semantics within Kafka. This uses producer IDs and sequence numbers to deduplicate retries, and two-phase commits to atomically write to multiple partitions.

Redpanda: Thread-Per-Core

Redpanda is a Kafka-compatible broker written in C++ using the Seastar framework's thread-per-core architecture. Each CPU core runs a single thread with its own memory, eliminating locks and context switches. The result: 10x lower tail latency than Kafka and no JVM garbage collection pauses. It is a drop-in replacement for Kafka's protocol but with dramatically simpler operations (single binary, no ZooKeeper).

Pulsar: Tiered Storage

Apache Pulsar separates the serving layer (brokers) from the storage layer (Apache BookKeeper). This enables tiered storage: hot data lives on SSDs in BookKeeper, while older data is automatically offloaded to cheap object storage (S3, GCS). You get infinite retention without infinite disk cost. Pulsar also natively supports multi-tenancy and geo-replication.

Queuing Models: Assumptions & Formulas

Kendall's Notation: How Queuing Models Are Named

Every queuing model is described using Kendall's notation: A/S/c/K/N/D, where:

  • A = Arrival process distribution
  • S = Service time distribution
  • c = Number of servers (consumers)
  • K = Queue capacity (omitted = infinite)
  • N = Population size (omitted = infinite)
  • D = Queue discipline (omitted = FIFO)

Common distribution codes:

  • M = Markovian (memoryless) = exponential distribution. "M" stands for "Markov" because the exponential distribution has the memoryless property: P(X > s+t | X > s) = P(X > t). The future doesn't depend on how long you've already waited.
  • D = Deterministic (constant/fixed time). Every service takes exactly the same time. Variance = 0.
  • G = General = any arbitrary probability distribution. This means you make no assumption about the shape of the distribution — it could be exponential, normal, log-normal, bimodal, heavy-tailed, or anything else. The only requirement is that the distribution has a well-defined mean and variance. "General" is powerful because you don't need to know which distribution your data follows; you just measure the mean and variance from production data and plug them into the formula. This makes G-based models (M/G/1, G/G/1, M/G/c) the most practical for real systems where you can't assume a specific distribution shape.
  • Ek = Erlang-k distribution (sum of k exponentials). Useful as a middle ground between M and D. As k→∞, Erlang-k approaches deterministic.

M/M/1: The Foundation

Assumptions

  • Poisson arrivals (M): Messages arrive independently at rate λ. Inter-arrival times are exponential with mean 1/λ. Reasonable when arrivals come from many independent sources (Palm-Khintchine theorem).
  • Exponential service (M): Processing time ~ Exp(μ), mean = 1/μ. Memoryless: "how long a message has been processing tells you nothing about remaining time."
  • Single server (1): One consumer, sequential processing.
  • Infinite queue, FIFO, infinite population.
  • Stability: ρ = λ/μ < 1.

Breaks down when

  • Traffic is bursty (not Poisson)
  • Service times are not exponential
  • Multiple consumers exist

W = (1/μ) / (1 - ρ) = 1 / (μ - λ)

M/M/c: Multiple Consumers (Erlang C)

Assumptions

  • Same as M/M/1, but with c parallel servers, each with service rate μ.
  • Stability: ρ = λ / (cμ) < 1. Total capacity = cμ.
  • Shared queue: All consumers pull from one queue. Idle consumer takes next message.
  • Use for: Kafka consumer groups, worker pools, thread pools.

Breaks down when

  • Consumers have different speeds (heterogeneous)
  • Coordination overhead between consumers (rebalancing, locking)

M/D/1: Deterministic Service

Assumptions

  • Poisson arrivals (same as M/M/1).
  • Every message takes exactly the same time to process (deterministic = 1/μ). Variance = 0.
  • This is the best case for a given ρ — no service variance means less queuing.
  • Use for: Fixed-size processing, hash lookups, constant-time encoding.

Breaks down when

  • Processing time has any variance at all
  • Even "nearly constant" service deviates from M/D/1

Formulas (from P-K with σS2 = 0)

Queue length (exactly half of M/M/1):

Lq = ρ2 / (2(1 - ρ))

Wait times:

Wq = ρ / (2μ(1-ρ))
W = (2 - ρ) / (2μ(1-ρ))

Intuition: Constant service eliminates "unlucky long service" — only arrival randomness causes queuing, so it's halved.

M/G/1: General Service (Pollaczek-Khinchine)

Assumptions

  • Poisson arrivals, but service time follows any distribution — you only need to know mean E[S] = 1/μ and variance σS2.
  • The most practical single-consumer model: measure mean and variance from production, plug in.
  • CS = σS/E[S] controls queuing severity: 0 = M/D/1, 1 = M/M/1, >1 = worse than exponential.
  • Use for: Any single-consumer scenario where you can measure service time stats.

Breaks down when

  • Arrivals are not Poisson (bursty, correlated, batch-triggered)

Pollaczek-Khinchine Formula

Derived via embedded Markov chain at departure epochs:

Lq = ρ2(1 + CS2) / (2(1 - ρ))

Equivalently:

Wq = λ · E[S2] / (2(1 - ρ))

where E[S2] = σS2 + (1/μ)2

Key insight: Service variance directly increases queuing. CS = 2 gives 2.5× the queue of M/M/1.

M/G/c: Multiple Consumers with General Service

Assumptions

  • Poisson arrivals at rate λ, c parallel servers, each with general (arbitrary) service time distribution.
  • You need: mean E[S], variance σS2, and server count c.
  • Stability: ρ = λ / (cμ) < 1.
  • Use for: The most common real-world scenario — a pool of workers processing tasks with variable duration (e.g., web server thread pool, Kafka consumer group with variable message sizes).

Why M/G/c matters

  • M/M/c assumes exponential service — too optimistic for low-variance tasks, too optimistic for heavy-tailed tasks depending on ρ
  • M/G/1 is single-server — but most systems have multiple workers
  • M/G/c bridges the gap: realistic service + multiple workers

Formulas (Approximations)

No exact closed-form exists for M/G/c. The standard approximation uses the Allen-Cunneen formula:

WqM/G/c ≈ WqM/M/c · (1 + CS2) / 2

This corrects the M/M/c result by the service variance factor. When CS = 1 (exponential), you get M/M/c exactly. When CS = 0, you get half — the M/D/c result.

More accurate (Kimura approximation):

WqM/G/c ≈ WqM/M/c · (CS2 + 1) / 2 · f(ρ, c)

where f(ρ, c) is a correction factor near 1 for moderate ρ. For practical use, the Allen-Cunneen approximation above is sufficient.

Rule of thumb: Use M/M/c formula, then multiply Wq by (1+CS2)/2.

M/M/1/K: Bounded Queue

Assumptions

  • Same as M/M/1, but queue has finite capacity K (including item being served).
  • Messages arriving to a full queue are rejected (dropped or must retry).
  • ρ ≥ 1 is allowed — queue won't explode because excess is dropped.
  • Use for: Systems with backpressure, bounded buffers, memory-limited queues.

Key trade-off

  • Larger K = lower rejection but higher latency
  • Smaller K = faster response but more rejections

Formulas

Steady-state probabilities:

P(n) = ρn(1-ρ) / (1-ρK+1)   n = 0..K

Rejection probability (fraction dropped):

P(K) = ρK(1-ρ) / (1-ρK+1)

For <0.1% rejection: need K ≥ ~3/ρ roughly.

Effective throughput:

λeff = λ(1 - P(K))

G/G/1: The Most General Single-Server Model

Assumptions

  • Both arrivals and service follow arbitrary distributions.
  • Only need means and variances of inter-arrival times and service times.
  • No exact closed-form — use Kingman's formula (heavy-traffic approximation, becomes exact as ρ → 1).
  • Use for: Bursty arrivals (not Poisson): batch uploads, cron-triggered traffic, correlated requests. Most realistic for production.

Breaks down when

  • System is non-stationary (rate changes over day)
  • Priority classes or batch processing
  • Dependencies between messages

Kingman's Formula

Wq ≈ (ρ / (1-ρ)) · ((CA2 + CS2) / 2) · E[S]

CA = σA/E[A] (arrival variability), CS = σS/E[S] (service variability).

Intuition: (CA2+CS2)/2 = average variability. When both = 1, recovers M/M/1. The ρ/(1-ρ) captures non-linear utilization. E[S] converts to time.

Most useful formula for real systems — measure CA and CS from production data.

Sizing & Tradeoffs

Why Target 60-70% Utilization? (Derivation)

The "60-70% target" isn't arbitrary. It comes from balancing three factors:

Response time multiplier (blue) and cost efficiency (green) vs. utilization. The sweet spot (shaded gold region) is where response time is still manageable but you're not wasting capacity. Hover over the graph to see exact values.

Factor 1: Non-linear response time. From the M/M/1 formula W = (1/μ)/(1-ρ), the response time multiplier is 1/(1-ρ). At 70%, this is 3.3×. At 80%, it's 5×. The derivative d(1/(1-ρ))/dρ = 1/(1-ρ)2 tells us how fast things get worse. At ρ=0.7, a 1% increase in utilization adds 11% more response time. At ρ=0.9, a 1% increase adds 100% more response time. The knee of the curve is roughly at ρ = 0.6-0.7 — visible in the graph above as where the blue curve starts bending sharply upward.

Factor 2: Spike absorption. The orange dashed line in the graph shows what happens if traffic spikes 40%. At baseline ρ = 0.7, a 40% spike pushes effective ρ to 0.98 — response time goes from 3.3× to 50×, painful but the queue remains stable. At baseline ρ = 0.75, the same spike pushes ρ past 1.0 — the queue grows unbounded. You need headroom = (1 - ρ) ≥ expected spike ratio. With 30% headroom (ρ = 0.7), you can absorb up to a ~40% traffic spike without instability.

Factor 3: Cost efficiency. The green dashed line shows cost efficiency (fraction of capacity used). Running at 30% utilization means 70% of capacity is idle. At 70%, you're using most of your capacity. The sweet spot (gold region) is where response time is still on the flat part of the curve but cost efficiency is reasonable.

For G/G/1 (real systems): The target should be lower if your CA or CS is high. With bursty traffic (CA = 2), queuing at 70% is 2.5× worse than Poisson. You might need to target 50% utilization to get the same response time that M/M/1 gives at 70%.

Quick Guide: Utilization Target by Variance

  • Low variance (CA ≤ 1, CS ≤ 0.5): Target 70-80% — predictable traffic and service.
  • Medium variance (CA ≈ 1, CS ≈ 1): Target 60-70% — the default recommendation.
  • High variance (CA > 1.5 or CS > 1.5): Target 40-55% — heavy tails require more headroom.
  • Very bursty (CA > 3): Target 30-40% or add autoscaling — queuing theory alone can't save you.

SLO-Driven Capacity Planning

The correct way to size a system is to start from your SLO and work backward to the required utilization and server count. Here's the framework:

  1. Define the SLO: "p99 latency < X ms" or "average latency < Y ms."
  2. Measure service time: How long does processing one message take? Get the mean (E[S] = 1/μ) and standard deviation (σS).
  3. Measure arrival pattern: What's the mean arrival rate (λ) and its variance? Calculate CA.
  4. Choose the model: M/M/1, M/G/1, G/G/1 depending on your distributions.
  5. Solve for maximum ρ: Given the SLO target, what's the highest utilization that meets it?
  6. Compute server count: c = ⌈λ / (ρmax × μ)⌉ + headroom for spikes.

Worked Examples (SLO-Driven)

Example 1: E-Commerce Order Processing

SLO: Average order processing time (queue wait + processing) must be < 100ms. Orders arrive at λ = 500/s during peak. Each order takes 20ms to process on average (μ = 50/s per consumer) with exponential variance (M/M/c model).

Step 1: Single consumer check. ρ = 500/50 = 10. Way over capacity — need multiple consumers.

Step 2: Find minimum consumers from utilization. We need W = Wq + 1/μ < 100ms, so Wq < 80ms. For M/M/c, we need ρper-server low enough that Wq stays under 80ms.

Step 3: Try c = 12 consumers. ρper-server = 500/(12 × 50) = 0.833. Using Erlang C: P(wait) ≈ 0.68, Wq = P(wait)/(cμ - λ) = 0.68/(600-500) = 6.8ms. Total W = 6.8 + 20 = 26.8ms. Well under SLO.

Step 4: Can we use fewer? Try c = 11: ρ = 500/550 = 0.91. P(wait) ≈ 0.87, Wq = 0.87/50 = 17.4ms. W = 37.4ms — still under SLO. Try c = 10: ρ = 1.0 — unstable. Not possible.

Step 5: Add spike headroom. With c=11, a 20% traffic spike (λ=600) would give ρ = 600/550 > 1 — unstable. With c=12, spike gives ρ = 600/600 = 1.0 — borderline. With c=14, spike gives ρ = 600/700 = 0.857, Wq ≈ 8ms. Safe.

Answer: 14 consumers to meet the 100ms SLO with 20% spike headroom. Cost: 14 × ~$50/month = $700/month for the consumer fleet.

Example 2: Payment Notification Service (Variable Processing)

SLO: p99 notification delivery time < 500ms. Notifications arrive at λ = 200/s (Poisson). Processing involves calling external APIs: mean 30ms, stddev 60ms (CS = 60/30 = 2, heavy-tailed). Single consumer per partition (M/G/1 model per partition).

Step 1: SLO conversion. For M/G/1, the p99 is approximately 2.5× the mean response time (this is a rough heuristic; exact p99 requires knowing the full distribution). So we need mean W < 500/2.5 = 200ms.

Step 2: Find max ρ. W = 1/μ + Wq. Wq for M/G/1 = (ρ(1 + CS2)) / (2μ(1-ρ)). With CS = 2: Wq = ρ(1+4) / (2 × 33.3 × (1-ρ)) = 5ρ / (66.6(1-ρ)). We need 30 + Wq(ms) < 200, so Wq < 170ms = 0.17s. Solving: 5ρ/(66.6(1-ρ)) < 0.17 ⇒ ρ < 0.69.

Step 3: Compute partitions. Each partition has one consumer with μ = 33.3/s. Max λ per partition = 0.69 × 33.3 = 23/s. Partitions needed = ⌈200/23⌉ = 9 partitions.

Step 4: Spike headroom. With 12 partitions, ρ per partition = 200/(12 × 33.3) = 0.50. A 40% spike (λ=280) gives ρ = 0.70 — right at the limit. Safe enough.

Answer: 12 Kafka partitions with 12 consumers. If CS were lower (say 0.5 instead of 2), we'd need only ⌈200/28.5⌉ = 8 partitions. Reducing processing variance saves infrastructure.

Example 3: Batch Analytics Pipeline (Bursty Arrivals)

SLO: Average batch processing latency < 2 seconds. Events arrive in bursts from cron jobs: mean λ = 1000/s but CA = 3 (very bursty). Processing is constant at 5ms per event (CS = 0, M/D/1-like but with bursty arrivals → G/G/1).

Step 1: Use Kingman. Wq ≈ (ρ/(1-ρ)) × (CA2 + CS2)/2 × E[S]. We need W = 5ms + Wq < 2000ms, so Wq < 1995ms per consumer.

Step 2: Find max ρ per consumer. Wq = (ρ/(1-ρ)) × (9+0)/2 × 5ms = 22.5ρ/(1-ρ) ms. Need < 1995: ρ/(1-ρ) < 88.7 ⇒ ρ < 0.989. That seems fine for a single consumer!

Step 3: But what about during a burst? CA = 3 means instantaneous arrival rate can be 4-5× the mean. During a burst, λpeak ≈ 4000/s for short periods. Per consumer μ = 200/s. So we need at least ⌈4000/200⌉ = 20 consumers just to survive peak bursts without growing the queue unboundedly.

Step 4: With 20 consumers at steady state, ρ = 1000/(20 × 200) = 0.25. Kingman: Wq = (0.25/0.75) × 4.5 × 5ms = 7.5ms. During 4× burst: ρ = 4000/4000 = 1.0 — queue grows. Add buffer: 25 consumers gives peak ρ = 4000/5000 = 0.8, Wq(peak) = (0.8/0.2) × 4.5 × 5ms = 90ms. Well under SLO.

Answer: 25 consumers. Note: at steady state, ρ = 1000/5000 = 0.2 — feels wasteful. This is exactly why bursty systems (CA > 1) require lower average utilization. An autoscaler that ramps from 10→25 during bursts would save ~60% on compute.

Example 4: Chat Message Fan-Out (Latency-Sensitive)

SLO: p99 message delivery < 50ms end-to-end. Message fan-out queue: λ = 10,000/s, processing = 2ms per message (μ = 500/s per consumer), exponential service (M/M/c).

Step 1: SLO → mean target. For M/M/c, p99 ≈ 4-5× mean W for moderate ρ. Need mean W < 50/5 = 10ms. So Wq < 10 - 2 = 8ms.

Step 2: Try c = 24. ρ = 10000/(24 × 500) = 0.833. Erlang C gives P(wait) ≈ 0.68, Wq = 0.68/(12000-10000) = 0.68/2000 = 0.34ms. W = 2.34ms. p99 estimate ≈ 5 × 2.34 = 11.7ms. Under SLO.

Step 3: Can we go lower? c = 22: ρ = 0.91, Wq ≈ 1.2ms, W ≈ 3.2ms, p99 ≈ 16ms. Still fine. c = 21: ρ = 0.95, Wq ≈ 3.5ms, W = 5.5ms, p99 ≈ 27.5ms. Fine. c = 20: ρ = 1.0 — unstable.

Step 4: Spike headroom. 30% spike with c=24: ρ = 13000/12000 = 1.08 — unstable. With c=28: spike ρ = 13000/14000 = 0.93, p99 ≈ 40ms. Just under SLO.

Answer: 28 consumers for a 50ms p99 SLO with spike tolerance. At $0.03/hour per consumer, this is $28 × 0.03 × 730 = $613/month.

Partition Count Tradeoffs

In Kafka, the number of partitions determines the maximum parallelism. More partitions means more consumers can work in parallel (M/M/c model applies per-partition), but each partition consumes broker memory (~10MB), increases leader election time during broker failures, and makes end-to-end latency worse (more partitions to replicate). Start with partitions = 2× expected peak consumer count and increase as needed. Reducing partition count later requires creating a new topic and migrating — so err on the side of slightly more than fewer.

Scaling Journey

0 – 1K users

In-Memory Queue

A simple in-process queue (Python queue.Queue, Go channel, or a thread pool). No dedicated infrastructure needed. Processing is synchronous or uses background threads. If the process crashes, queued work is lost — acceptable at this scale.

1K – 100K users

Single Redis / SQS

Introduce a dedicated queue: Redis lists with BRPOPLPUSH for simple task queues, or SQS for zero-ops managed queuing. This gives durability (messages survive process restarts) and allows separating producer and consumer into different services. SQS handles up to ~3,000 messages/sec per queue without any tuning.

100K – 10M users

Kafka Single/Multi-Partition

When you need ordering guarantees, replay, and multiple consumer groups reading the same stream, migrate to Kafka. Start with a small cluster (3 brokers) and partition topics based on expected throughput. Use consumer groups for parallel processing. Introduce schema management (Avro + Schema Registry) to prevent producer/consumer contract drift.

10M+ users

Multi-Region Replication

Deploy Kafka clusters in multiple regions with MirrorMaker 2 or Confluent Cluster Linking for cross-region replication. Use tiered storage to offload cold data to S3. Run dedicated clusters per domain (payments, analytics, events) to isolate blast radius. Consumer lag monitoring becomes critical — a lagging consumer in one region can cascade failures globally.

Migration Signals

  • In-memory to Redis/SQS: You start losing work during deployments or crashes. Or you need multiple services to consume the same events.
  • Redis/SQS to Kafka: You need message replay, strict ordering per key, or you have multiple consumer groups. SQS ordering (FIFO) maxes out at 300 msg/sec per group.
  • Single-region to multi-region: Regulatory requirements (data residency), latency constraints, or disaster recovery SLAs drive this.

What Can Go Wrong

1. Consumer Lag Spiral into Backpressure Collapse

What happened: A downstream database became slow, causing consumers to take longer per message. Consumer lag grew from seconds to hours. As lag grew, messages accumulated, broker disk usage spiked, and eventually the broker ran out of disk space, stopping all producers across all topics — not just the one with the slow consumer.

Root cause: No per-topic disk quota, no consumer lag alerting, and no backpressure mechanism to slow producers when consumers can't keep up.

Mitigation: Set log.retention.bytes per topic to cap disk usage. Alert when consumer lag exceeds N minutes. Implement backpressure: if lag exceeds a threshold, producers should slow down or write to a dead letter topic. Ensure broker disk has 30%+ free space at all times.

2. Poison Message Blocking a Partition

What happened: A producer sent a malformed message (invalid JSON) to a Kafka partition. The consumer deserialized it, threw an exception, and retried. Since Kafka preserves order within a partition, all subsequent messages on that partition were blocked behind the poison message. The consumer retried indefinitely, processing zero messages on that partition for hours.

Root cause: No dead letter queue (DLQ) configured. No maximum retry count. The consumer treated all failures as retriable.

Mitigation: Configure a maximum retry count (e.g., 3 attempts). After exhausting retries, send the message to a dead letter topic and advance the offset. Monitor DLQ depth. Implement schema validation at the producer level to catch malformed messages before they enter the queue.

3. Exactly-Once Processing Failures

What happened: A payments service used Kafka transactions for exactly-once processing. During a broker upgrade, the transaction coordinator moved to a new broker. In-flight transactions were aborted, but the consumer had already committed the side effect (charged the customer's credit card) before the Kafka transaction committed. The retry re-charged the customer.

Root cause: Exactly-once in Kafka only applies within Kafka — it cannot prevent duplicate side effects on external systems. The service relied on Kafka's exactly-once guarantee to also mean "exactly-once external effect," which is impossible without idempotency.

Mitigation: External side effects must be idempotent. Use an idempotency key (e.g., Kafka message offset + partition) to deduplicate at the external system. Store the last processed offset in the same database transaction as the side effect (transactional outbox pattern).

4. Rebalance Storms

What happened: A Kafka consumer group with 50 consumers experienced a "rebalance storm." One consumer's GC pause exceeded session.timeout.ms, causing a rebalance. During the rebalance, other consumers stopped processing, causing them to also time out, triggering another rebalance. The group spent 45 minutes in continuous rebalancing, processing zero messages.

Root cause: session.timeout.ms (10s) was too close to the GC pause time. max.poll.interval.ms was too low for the batch processing time.

Mitigation: Increase session.timeout.ms to 30-45s. Set max.poll.interval.ms higher than your worst-case batch processing time. Use static group membership (group.instance.id) to avoid rebalances on transient disconnects. In Kafka 3.x+, use the new incremental cooperative rebalancing protocol instead of eager rebalancing.

Interactions & Pitfalls

Queues + Distributed Transactions

The most common mistake: assuming "I put it in the queue, so it will happen exactly once." A consumer might process a message, write to the database, then crash before committing its Kafka offset. On restart, it reprocesses the same message, creating a duplicate database entry. Solution: Make consumers idempotent. Use a unique message ID or offset as an idempotency key. Store it in the same database transaction as the business write (transactional outbox pattern). If the ID already exists, skip processing.

Queues + Ordering Guarantees

Kafka guarantees ordering only within a partition. If you need events for the same entity to be processed in order (e.g., user sign-up before user profile update), you must use a partition key (e.g., user ID) so all events for that entity go to the same partition. But a bad partition key choice causes hot partitions — if one user generates 90% of traffic, one partition becomes a bottleneck. The design tension: finer partition keys give better ordering but risk skew; coarser keys spread load but lose ordering where you need it. See the Ordering Guarantees section earlier on this page for a detailed comparison of total order vs. partition order vs. no order, and specific guidance on choosing partition keys.

Queues + Rate Limiting

If your consumer calls a rate-limited external API (e.g., Stripe, Twilio), the queue's throughput is capped by the API's rate limit. Without backpressure propagation, the queue grows unboundedly while consumers are throttled. Solution: Implement a token bucket on the consumer side, use delayed/scheduled message delivery (SQS delay queues, Kafka timestamp-based filtering), or use a rate-limited consumer pool that dynamically adjusts concurrency based on API response headers (X-RateLimit-Remaining).

Real-World Examples

Kafka at LinkedIn

Kafka was born at LinkedIn in 2011 to solve their data pipeline problem. Today, LinkedIn runs 7+ trillion messages per day across hundreds of Kafka clusters. Every user action — profile view, connection request, message sent — flows through Kafka. The key architectural insight: Kafka is not just a queue but a central nervous system that connects ~100 internal services. Each service produces events to Kafka and consumes events from other services' topics. This decoupling allowed LinkedIn to scale from a monolith to microservices without cascading failures during service deployments.

SQS at Amazon

Amazon SQS was one of the first AWS services (launched 2004). Internally, Amazon uses SQS extensively for order processing pipelines. When you place an order on Amazon, it doesn't process synchronously. Instead, the order event goes into a queue, and separate services handle payment processing, inventory reservation, shipping label generation, and notification sending — each consuming from the queue independently. SQS processes hundreds of billions of messages per day across AWS customers, with a durability guarantee of 99.999999999% (11 nines) by storing each message redundantly across multiple availability zones.

RabbitMQ in Practice

RabbitMQ remains popular for workloads that need complex routing. Its exchange model (direct, topic, fanout, headers) allows sophisticated message routing without consumer-side filtering. Companies like Bloomberg use RabbitMQ for financial data distribution where messages need to be routed to specific consumers based on instrument type, market, and priority. RabbitMQ's strength is flexibility and low latency for moderate throughput (<100K msgs/sec). Its weakness is horizontal scaling — clustering adds complexity and partition handling in RabbitMQ is notoriously tricky.

Code Example

A simple in-memory pub/sub system in Python demonstrating the producer-consumer-callback pattern:

import threading
from collections import defaultdict
from queue import Queue, Empty

class MessageBroker:
    def __init__(self):
        self.topics = defaultdict(list)       # topic -> [callback]
        self.queues = defaultdict(Queue)       # topic -> Queue
        self.lock = threading.Lock()

    def subscribe(self, topic, callback):
        """Register a callback for a topic (pub/sub pattern)."""
        with self.lock:
            self.topics[topic].append(callback)

    def publish(self, topic, message):
        """Send a message to all subscribers and the topic queue."""
        with self.lock:
            subscribers = list(self.topics[topic])
        # Fan-out to all subscribers (pub/sub)
        for cb in subscribers:
            cb(topic, message)
        # Also enqueue for point-to-point consumers
        self.queues[topic].put(message)

    def consume(self, topic, timeout=1.0):
        """Pull one message from the queue (point-to-point pattern)."""
        try:
            return self.queues[topic].get(timeout=timeout)
        except Empty:
            return None

# --- Usage ---
broker = MessageBroker()

def on_order(topic, msg):
    print(f"[Analytics] {topic}: order #{msg['id']} = ${msg['total']}")

def on_order_email(topic, msg):
    print(f"[Email] Sending confirmation for order #{msg['id']}")

broker.subscribe("orders", on_order)
broker.subscribe("orders", on_order_email)
broker.publish("orders", {"id": 42, "total": 99.95})

# Point-to-point: only one worker gets the message
msg = broker.consume("orders")
print(f"[Worker] Processing order: {msg}")

How to Validate Your Design

Load Testing

Use kafka-producer-perf-test and kafka-consumer-perf-test (bundled with Kafka) to measure raw throughput and latency. Key parameters to sweep: message size (100B to 10KB), batch size (batch.size: 16KB to 1MB), linger time (linger.ms: 0 to 100ms), and compression (none vs. LZ4 vs. zstd). For end-to-end testing, use k6 or Locust to simulate realistic producer patterns (bursty traffic, variable message sizes) and measure end-to-end latency from publish to consumer processing.

Common benchmarking mistake: Testing with a single partition and single consumer, then extrapolating to N partitions. Scaling is not linear — replication overhead, network bandwidth, and consumer coordination all reduce per-partition throughput as you add partitions.

Chaos Testing

  • Kill a broker: Verify that leader election happens within leader.election.timeout.ms and producers/consumers resume without data loss. Check that min.insync.replicas prevents writes to under-replicated partitions.
  • Inject network latency: Use toxiproxy to add 500ms latency between producers and brokers. Verify that producers back off gracefully and don't OOM from buffering.
  • Poison message: Inject a malformed message and verify the consumer sends it to the DLQ after N retries and continues processing subsequent messages.
  • Consumer crash during processing: Kill a consumer mid-batch and verify that uncommitted messages are reprocessed by another consumer in the group without data loss.

Benchmarking Methodology

Measure throughput vs. latency at different utilization levels. Plot the curve: as you increase producer rate, at what point does p99 latency spike? That is your system's practical capacity. Always measure p99 and p99.9, not just average — averages hide the tail latency that real users experience. Run benchmarks for at least 10 minutes to capture GC pauses (JVM-based brokers), compaction cycles, and log segment rollovers.

Interview Questions

Design a notification system for 100M users

Start with the scale: 100M users, assume 10% are daily active, and each triggers ~5 notification-worthy events per day. That is 50M events/day or ~580 events/second average, with peaks of 10-50x during major events (breaking news, flash sales). This is well within a single Kafka cluster's capacity.

Architecture: Services produce notification events to a Kafka topic partitioned by user ID (ensures ordering per user). A notification routing service consumes events, determines delivery channels (push, email, SMS, in-app), and enqueues to channel-specific topics. Each channel has its own consumer group that handles delivery at the appropriate rate. Push notifications go through APNs/FCM with batching. Email goes through SES/SendGrid with rate limiting. SMS goes through Twilio with strict per-number rate limits.

Key design decisions: Use a fan-out pattern — one event can generate notifications on multiple channels. Implement user preference filtering early (before fan-out) to avoid wasted work. Use a dead letter queue for failed deliveries with exponential backoff retry. Store notification state (sent, delivered, read) in a separate database for the notification center UI.

Follow-ups: How would you handle notification deduplication? (Idempotency key per user+event.) How would you implement "do not disturb" schedules? (Check user timezone and preferences before delivery, use delayed message delivery.) How would you prioritize urgent vs. marketing notifications? (Separate topics with different consumer SLAs.)

How would you ensure exactly-once processing?

First, clarify what "exactly-once" means. True exactly-once delivery is impossible in a distributed system (proven by the Two Generals problem). What we can achieve is effectively exactly-once processing through a combination of at-least-once delivery plus idempotent consumers.

Approach 1 — Idempotent consumer: Assign each message a unique ID (or use Kafka offset + partition). Before processing, check if this ID exists in your results database. If yes, skip. If no, process and store the ID atomically with the result in the same database transaction. This is the most practical approach and works with any queue.

Approach 2 — Kafka transactions: Use Kafka's transactional API to atomically read from input topic, process, and write to output topic. This gives exactly-once within Kafka but does NOT cover external side effects (database writes, API calls). You still need idempotency for anything outside Kafka.

Approach 3 — Transactional outbox: Write the business result and the "message processed" marker to the same database in one transaction. A separate process (CDC via Debezium, or a polling outbox reader) picks up new results and publishes downstream. This gives exactly-once semantics end-to-end because the database transaction is the single source of truth.

Follow-ups: What is the performance overhead of exactly-once? (~10-20% throughput reduction for Kafka transactions.) When is at-least-once acceptable? (Analytics, logging — duplicates are tolerable.)

Kafka vs. RabbitMQ — when to choose which?

Choose Kafka when: You need high throughput (>100K msgs/sec), message replay/reprocessing, multiple consumer groups reading the same data, event sourcing, or stream processing. Kafka excels as a durable event log that serves as the system of record for events.

Choose RabbitMQ when: You need complex routing (topic exchanges, header-based routing), per-message TTL, priority queues, low latency for moderate throughput (<50K msgs/sec), or a simpler operational model for smaller teams. RabbitMQ is better for traditional task queues where messages should be consumed once and deleted.

Key differences: Kafka retains messages (log-based); RabbitMQ deletes after ACK. Kafka consumers pull; RabbitMQ pushes. Kafka scales by adding partitions; RabbitMQ scales by adding queues and using shovel/federation. Kafka has a steeper learning curve but handles larger scale. RabbitMQ has better built-in support for delayed messages, dead lettering, and complex routing topologies.

Follow-ups: What about SQS? (Choose SQS for zero-ops, serverless architectures, or when you don't need ordering/replay.) What about Pulsar? (Choose Pulsar when you need Kafka-like semantics plus multi-tenancy and tiered storage without operational complexity of managing BookKeeper separately.)

How do you handle poison messages?

A poison message is one that consistently fails processing, blocking the consumer (especially in ordered queues where you must process in sequence). The strategy has three layers:

Layer 1 — Prevention: Validate messages at the producer using a schema registry (Avro, Protobuf). Reject malformed messages before they enter the queue. This catches 90% of poison messages.

Layer 2 — Detection and isolation: Configure a retry policy with a maximum retry count (e.g., 3 attempts with exponential backoff). After exhausting retries, route the message to a dead letter queue (DLQ) and advance the consumer offset. The DLQ is a separate topic/queue where failed messages are stored for manual inspection and replay.

Layer 3 — Recovery: Monitor DLQ depth (alert on non-zero). Build tooling to inspect DLQ messages, fix the bug, and replay them back to the original topic. In SQS, this is built-in (DLQ redrive). In Kafka, you need to build it yourself or use a framework like Spring Kafka's SeekToCurrentErrorHandler.

Follow-ups: What if the poison message is a valid message that triggers a code bug? (Fix the consumer code, then replay from DLQ.) How do you handle ordering when you skip a poison message? (Downstream consumers must handle gaps in sequence numbers. If strict ordering is critical, the partition must halt and alert for manual intervention.)

Design a system that processes 1 million events per second with ordering guarantees

Scale analysis: 1M events/sec with each event ~1KB = 1 GB/sec throughput. A single Kafka broker can handle ~200K msgs/sec, so you need a minimum of 5 brokers, but with replication factor 3, plan for ~15 brokers.

Partitioning strategy: Ordering is guaranteed within a partition, so events that must be ordered relative to each other must share a partition key. Choose the key carefully — user_id, account_id, or entity_id depending on the domain. With 1M events/sec and ~200K events/sec per partition, you need at least 5 partitions, but plan for 50-100 to allow consumer parallelism and handle partition key skew.

Consumer design: Use consumer groups with one consumer per partition. Each consumer processes messages sequentially within its partition (preserving order) but partitions are processed in parallel across consumers. If processing involves I/O (database writes), use a thread pool per consumer but maintain per-key ordering within the thread pool (route same key to same thread).

Follow-ups: How would you handle a hot partition? (Re-partition the topic with a composite key, or use sub-partitioning within the consumer.) What happens during a broker failure? (Leader election for affected partitions, ~5-30 seconds of unavailability per partition depending on config.) How do you monitor this? (Consumer lag per partition, broker CPU/disk, network saturation.)

How would you migrate from SQS to Kafka without downtime?

Phase 1 — Dual write: Modify the producer to write to both SQS and Kafka simultaneously. This is temporarily inconsistent (if one write fails), but it lets you build confidence in the Kafka path. Run a shadow consumer on Kafka that processes messages but discards results — compare with SQS consumer output to verify correctness.

Phase 2 — Kafka primary, SQS shadow: Flip the consumers to read from Kafka as the primary source. Keep the SQS consumer running as a fallback. Monitor consumer lag, throughput, and error rates on both paths. If Kafka has issues, you can instantly fall back to SQS.

Phase 3 — Decommission SQS: After 1-2 weeks of stable Kafka operation, stop the SQS producer and consumer. Delete the SQS queue.

Key risks: Message ordering may differ between SQS (best-effort FIFO or standard) and Kafka (strict per-partition). Consumers designed for SQS's visibility timeout model may not work correctly with Kafka's offset commit model. Test thoroughly for idempotency edge cases — during dual-write, consumers may process some messages from both SQS and Kafka.

Follow-ups: How would you handle the dual-write consistency problem? (Accept temporary inconsistency, or use a transactional outbox that writes to a DB and a CDC connector publishes to both.) What if message volume is too high for dual-write? (Write to Kafka only and use a Kafka Connect SQS sink for the legacy consumers during migration.)

Key Takeaways

  • Message queues decouple producers from consumers, enabling async processing, load leveling, and independent scaling.
  • Point-to-point queues deliver each message to one consumer; pub/sub broadcasts to all subscribers. Most systems need both.
  • Kafka's log-based architecture (append-only, consumer-tracked offsets, retention) changed the game by enabling replay, multiple consumer groups, and high throughput.
  • True exactly-once delivery is impossible. Achieve effectively exactly-once processing through idempotent consumers + at-least-once delivery.
  • Response time explodes non-linearly with utilization. Target 60-70% utilization; at 80% response time is already 5x service time.
  • Poison messages can block entire partitions. Always configure a dead letter queue with a maximum retry count.
  • Partition key design is the most important Kafka decision — it determines ordering guarantees and load distribution.
  • Monitor consumer lag religiously. Growing lag is the first signal of trouble and the root cause of most queue-related outages.
  • Start simple (SQS or Redis). Move to Kafka only when you need replay, multiple consumer groups, or strict ordering at scale.
