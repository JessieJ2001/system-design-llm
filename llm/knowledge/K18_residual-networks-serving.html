K18 — Residual Networks (the serving slice only — deliberately the narrowest page)

What are the only two serving-relevant consequences of the residual/skip connection — (1) it decides where you may cut a model across GPUs (PP between blocks, TP within, the residual add stays GPU-local), and (2) it decides how much activation memory prefill burns ([batch×seq×d_model] per layer) so that long prefill OOMs before the KV cache does? This page derives those two consequences and D46 (the prefill-activation-OOM vs KV-OOM crossover) — and honestly states it has no independent rule-11 because it is a structural constraint, not a policy.

What This Page Is (and Is Not)

TL;DR. The residual/skip connection (x → x + f(x)) has exactly two serving-relevant consequences: it makes a block an atomic, same-width unit (so you may cut between blocks but not inside the residual add), and it makes prefill materialize [batch×seq×d_model] activations per layer (so long prefill OOMs before KV). Nothing else about residuals is in serving scope.

  • Is: the two serving consequences (placement constraint + activation-memory footprint), derived; D46 (the prefill-activation-OOM vs KV-OOM crossover seq; heavy GQA → activation dominates sooner).
  • Is not: residual-network theory / why skip connections let you train deep nets (training-side); activation-checkpointing-for-backprop (training-side — the inference analog is chunked prefill, K4/D1); model-architecture design (the residual structure is taken as given); chunked prefill as the fix (K4/D1 owns it — K18 owns only the footprint + crossover; rule of two).
  • Scoped out, named not covered (siblings, owned elsewhere): the logits/unembed [batch,seq,vocab] prefill spike (a sibling activation-OOM — vocab > d_model, so it can exceed the residual stream; mitigated by computing logits for the last position only during prefill) is K0-adjacent, not a residual-stream consequence — flag it, do not cover. The decode-side residual add is a memory-bound elementwise op fused into the adjacent kernel — K17 owns kernel fusion; there is no K18 decode consequence (seq=1 ⇒ negligible).

The Residual-Serving Spine (stated up front, before §1)

  • The residual/skip connection is a per-block identity path (x → x + Attn(LN x) → x + MLP(LN x)).
  • Consequence 1 — placement: the x + identity makes input/output the same width d_model and the block an atomic unit ⇒ you may cut between blocks (PP) but not inside the residual add; TP splits the within-block matmuls but the element-wise add stays GPU-local.
  • → that is the legal-cut constraint K6/K7/K8 obey.
  • Corollary of consequence 1 — the cut-edge payload: the only edge that survives a block boundary is the residual stream, so the PP cross-stage P2P payload is that tensor — size [microbatch, chunk, d_model]·dtype, which couples PP micro-batch sizing to interconnect BW. K8/K20 own the cost; K18 owns the identity (rule of two).
  • Consequence 2 — memory: during prefill every layer’s residual + attention/MLP intermediates are live at [batch, seq, d_model] ⇒ scales with seq.
  • Problem: at long context this rivals KV and prefill OOMs before decode — the hidden OOM (D46).
  • D46 holds only under four production caveats (this is what makes the crossover a staff derivation, not a toy formula): (i) it assumes FlashAttention removed the O(seq²) score-matrix term — without it that term dominates and OOMs far sooner (cite K17); (ii) TP replicates the residual stream, it does not shard it ⇒ more-TP does not move the crossover (only CP/SP/chunked-prefill do); (iii) weight-quant does not move it — the residual stream stays BF16 while weights go FP8/INT4 (cite K14/D36); (iv) the activation peak and the resident KV coexist in the same HBM at different timescales ⇒ the design response is an activation-headroom reservation carved out of the KV pool (cite K2), not merely “which OOMs first”.
  • → chunked prefill caps activation at O(C·d_model) independent of seq (K4/D1 owns the fix — rule of two).
  • Objective: the sole knob is the chunk size (K4/D1) + the GQA factor that sets the crossover — no independent K18 rule-11 (scope-honest).

3. The System-Design View

Where the constraint sits in the GPU topology

Stub. The legal cut boundaries are the block edges: PP places contiguous blocks on a stage (K8), TP splits the matmuls inside a block across the NVLink domain (K7), and the residual add executes GPU-local on every rank. The corollary made concrete here: the tensor that physically crosses each PP stage boundary is the residual stream — [microbatch, chunk, d_model]·dtype per P2P send — so the PP cut’s wire volume and the long-prefill activation footprint are the same tensor at two scopes; this couples PP micro-batch sizing to the interconnect tier (K8/K20 own that cost, K18 owns the identity — rule of two). Deep dive D46 — the prefill-activation-OOM vs KV-OOM crossover (rule-of-two with K4/D1)

Deep-dive stub. Problem: engineers size HBM for weights + KV and hit a surprise prefill OOM at long context. Solution: derive both footprints — resident KV ≈ 2·L·n_kv_heads·d_head·seq·dtype (persists, grows with seq); peak prefill activation ≈ c·L·batch·seq·d_model·dtype (transient per forward, grows with seq) — and the crossover seq where activation > KV. Heavy GQA shrinks KV → activation dominates sooner (the non-obvious staff point). Chunked prefill caps activation at O(C·d_model) independent of seq → moves the OOM ceiling. Rule of two: K18 owns the footprint + crossover; K4/D1 owns chunked prefill as the fix.

The four production caveats that make this a staff derivation, not a toy formula (each Problem→Solution):

Caveat (i) — the FlashAttention precondition. Problem: the c·L·batch·seq·d_model term is linear in seq — but a naive attention kernel materializes the [batch, n_heads, seq, seq] score matrix, an O(seq²) term that dominates and OOMs far sooner, making the crossover meaningless. Solution: the residual-stream term is the relevant one only because FlashAttention (K17) never materializes the score matrix — state this assumption explicitly; cite K17, do not re-derive the kernel (rule of two).number: the O(seq²) attention-matrix bytes vs the O(seq·d_model) residual term at the trace seq — shows which dominates with/without FA — cites K17 (FlashAttention), K1 (bytes)

Caveat (ii) — more-TP does not move the crossover. Problem: the staff reflex for “long context OOMs” is “add TP” — but TP replicates the residual stream on every rank (only the matmul intermediates are sharded), so the activation footprint is unchanged by TP degree. Solution: only CP / sequence-parallel / chunked-prefill shrink it — this is why D40’s “more-TP” branch loses on the activation side; cross-link K7 (sequence-parallel) / K11 (CP).number: residual-stream bytes/rank at TP=1 vs TP=8 (unchanged) on the trace — cites K7/K11 (the axes that DO shrink it)

Caveat (iii) — weight-quant does not move the crossover. Problem: a reader arriving from D36 (quantize-to-fit) expects FP8/INT4 to shrink activation memory — it does not; the residual stream stays BF16 (low-precision repeated residual adds accumulate error). Solution: weight-quant fixes the weight-OOM; the prefill-activation-OOM is exactly where it was — D46’s crossover is invariant to weight precision; cite K14/D36.number: weight bytes FP16→FP8 (halved) vs prefill-activation bytes (unchanged) on the trace — cites K14/D36

Caveat (iv) — activation and KV coexist; D46 is a headroom reservation, not just “which OOMs first”. Problem: the activation peak is transient per-forward; the KV is resident — they contend for the same HBM at different timescales, so sizing the KV pool to fill HBM OOMs the instant a long prefill’s activation spikes, even though steady state “fit”. Solution: the design response is an explicit activation-headroom reservation carved out of the KV pool budget = peak prefill activation; K2 owns pool sizing, K18 owns why the reservation exists (the transient residual peak) — rule of two.number: the activation-headroom GB to reserve out of the KV pool at the trace prefill seq — cites K2 (pool sizing), K1 (bytes)

4. Alternatives & When Each Wins

Stub (≥2, disqualifying constraint per alt — alternatives to handling the activation footprint). (a) Just buy more HBM / bigger GPU — disq: activation grows with seq unboundedly → any fixed HBM has a context ceiling; wins only for moderate context where the headroom is cheap. (b) Reduce batch during long prefill — disq: activation ∝ batch×seq, so dropping batch helps but tanks throughput; wins as a stopgap, not a design. (c) Chunked prefill (K4/D1) — the actual fix: caps activation at O(C·d_model) independent of seq; disq for K18 to own: it is a scheduler policy — cite K4/D1, do not re-derive. (d) Activation-checkpointing-for-backprop — disq: training-side; the inference analog is chunked prefill; out of scope — flag the boundary, do not cover.number: the context ceiling per fixed HBM (a); throughput cost of batch-drop (b) on the trace — cites K1 (activation bytes), K4/D1 (the fix) · contrast L3 (long-context, where this is the bottleneck)
