K5 — Disaggregated Serving

How do you build scalable disaggregated (prefill/decode-split) serving — and should you? Prefill is compute-bound; decode is memory-bandwidth-bound. They have opposite rooflines and different SLOs (TTFT vs TPOT), so colocating them couples two things that should scale independently. The page’s central question is a derived inequality (D35): disaggregate iff colocation’s interference cost exceeds disaggregation’s transport + pool-quantization cost — the popular “hyperscale yes / small no” rule-of-thumb is that inequality’s corollary, not its premise.

SLIs This Page Reduces

SLIs this page reduces (the reverse index of K1 §3.12):
  • TTFT — prefill runs to completion at full MFU on a dedicated pool with no decode stealing its step.
  • TPOT / ITL — decode runs at the steady memory-bound rate with zero prefill interference (no Δ_intf).
  • MFU — the prefill pool stays compute-bound at peak; the decode pool batches without prefill chunks displacing it.
  • Goodput & $/req — the two pools autoscale independently to the traffic-derived ratio, so neither is over- or under-provisioned for the other’s roofline.

The Spine — the D35 Decision (stated up front, before §1)

K5’s central question is “When is disaggregation better or worse than aggregation, exactly?” The answer is an inequality; the rule-of-thumb is its corollary.

  • The two designs, quantified. Colocated: one pool runs prefill + decode interleaved via chunked prefill (K4 D34); a step carrying a C-token prefill chunk costs max(decode_step, prefill_compute(C)) → TPOT inflation Δ_intf ≈ φ·(prefill_compute(C) − decode_step) (φ = fraction of steps carrying a chunk). Disaggregated: prefill runs at full MFU on a prefill GPU (TTFT minimal), KV is shipped to a decode GPU, decode runs with zero prefill interference (TPOT minimal & smooth); price = a KV handoff T_xfer = KV_bytes / BW_link, mostly hidden under prefill compute by layer-wise pipelining → exposed cost f_x·T_xfer, f_x ≪ 1.
  • The crossover inequality (the exact answer). Disaggregate iff Δ_intf(ρ_p, C) measured against the TPOT SLO > f_x·T_xfer (added to TTFT) + Imb(N)·GPU_cost, where Imb(N) = ⌈p:d ratio⌉ rounding-error / N_total = pool-quantization waste (no fractional pools — minimum 1 prefill + 1 decode GPU). The naive form stops there; the honest RHS also carries L_ctrl (the control-plane + double-queue latency the split adds to TTFT — every request now waits in two scheduler queues, not one) and C_ops (the fixed two-fleet engineering/operational cost — two autoscalers, a transport control plane, a global KV index, doubled on-call/attribution — independent of N, so it does not amortize with scale). Both are why the mid-scale verdict usually resolves to colocate even when the pure GPU math is neutral.
  • The three levers that decide it. (a) SLO tightness — dominant: colocation absorbs Δ_intf only while base_TPOT + Δ_intf ≤ TPOT_SLO. (b) Scale via Imb(N): →0 as N→∞, huge at a few GPUs — an entirely small-scale tax. (c) Mix variance: stable ρ_p → one chunked-prefill knob suffices; bursty → two independently-autoscaled pools track it.
  • The rule-of-thumb, derived (the same inequality at three scales). Hyperscale (Imb→0, tight SLO, transfer hidden): LHS ≫ RHS → disaggregate. Mid-size (Imb≠0, variable SLO): inequality close → benchmark both. Single-tenant / small / research (Imb dominates, loose SLO): LHS < RHS → colocate + chunked prefill (K4) — simpler.

2. The Mechanism — the Tokenized-Prompt Walkthrough

Where the split lives — the prefill→decode boundary, a deployment topology, not a model change

Problem→Solution stub (architecture placement, hard req 6). Problem: the K0 forward pass is identical whether prefill and decode run on the same GPU or different ones — nothing in the model says “split here”; the question is purely where the KV produced by the last prefill step is consumed by the first decode step. Solution: disaggregation cuts at exactly that boundary — prefill GPUs run the full forward pass over the prompt and emit the complete KV; the KV is transported; decode GPUs consume it and autoregress. The walkthrough on "I like cat, why?" — colocated vs disaggregated, prefill vs decode

Why prefill and decode want different hardware — derived

Problem→Solution stub (the page’s opening derivation, builds on the walkthrough). Sub-bulleted: (a) prefill reads each weight matrix once and reuses it across all N prompt tokens → arithmetic intensity high → compute-bound, wants peak FLOP/s and runs at high MFU (cite K1 #prefill/#roofline); (b) decode reads all weights to produce one token → AI ≈ 1 → memory-BW-bound, wants HBM bandwidth and big batch (cite K1 #decode); (c) opposite rooflines + different SLOs (TTFT vs TPOT) ⇒ the optimal batch, the optimal parallelism, even the optimal GPU SKU differ → colocation forces one configuration to compromise both, which is exactly the Δ_intf the D35 inequality quantifies. This is why the split exists; D35 decides whether it pays.numbers: prefill vs decode AI & MFU on the trace; B* — cited from K1 #prefill #decode #roofline · cites K1 only, forward-links D35

Prefill batch ≈ 1, decode batch large — and why colocation can’t optimize both [EXPANDED — makes §2 clause (c) “the optimal batch differ” explicit; user-requested]

Problem→Solution stub (the concrete form of clause (c); K2 owns the derivation, K5 owns the disaggregation consequence — rule of two). Problem: the two phases want opposite batch sizes. Prefill is compute-bound even at batch 1 — one prompt’s N tokens already fill the GEMMs (the weight-load is amortized across the prompt’s own tokens within a single request), so the prefill batch is typically 1 (or a handful); a bigger prefill batch mostly adds queueing/head-of-line, not throughput. Decode is memory-BW-bound at small batch (AI ≈ 1) — one token amortizes the weight-load over nothing, so you must batch many concurrent sequences to climb toward the roofline ridge B* (≈295 on the trace); the decode batch is large. The colocation consequence (the point): one engine runs one batch composition per GPU step that must hold both prefill chunks and decodes — it cannot simultaneously be “batch≈1, compute-shaped” for prefill and “batch≈B*, bandwidth-shaped” for decode; the chunked-prefill step is exactly that forced compromise, and the MFU it leaves on the table is part of the Δ_intf the D35 inequality measures. The disaggregation win: separate pools each pick their own optimal batch independently — the prefill pool small-batch at full MFU, the decode pool large-batch near B* — and each autoscales to its own traffic-derived ratio. This independent batch-sizing is a concrete component of why the D35 LHS is large and feeds directly into the pool-ratio R derivation below.numbers: prefill batch=1 GEMM utilization vs decode B*≈295 on the trace; MFU prefill@1 vs decode@small-batch; the colocated single-batch compromise — cites K1 #decode #roofline, K2 (batching-flips-decode’s-roofline derivation — cite, don’t re-derive) · forward-links D35, §3 pool-ratio & knobs

Per-pool parallelism & hardware-SKU asymmetry — and the cross-TP KV re-shard the handoff is forced to do [ADDED — not in plan]

Problem→Solution stub (the other half of §2 clause (c); K6/K7/K8 own parallelism selection, K5 owns the handoff-layout consequence — rule of two). Problem: clause (c) also said the optimal parallelism and GPU SKU differ — concretely: prefill (compute/TTFT-bound) wants high TP on high-FLOP GPUs (B200-class) for the fastest prefill; decode (BW/throughput-bound) wants a throughput-shaped TP+batch on cheaper high-HBM-BW or older GPUs. Colocated ⇒ one engine = one parallelism tuple + one SKU serving both → the same forced compromise as batch-asymmetry. The hard consequence (the missing depth): if prefill is TP=8 and decode is TP=4 (or the PP degrees differ), the KV tensor is partitioned differently across the two pools → the handoff is not a copy — it is a cross-TP re-partition (a scatter/all-gather-shaped reshape on the wire); the transport engine must reconcile KV layouts, adding a re-shard cost on top of T_xfer. Decode-pool concurrency ceiling = ΣHBM_decode / E[KV] — the real decode sizing equation [ADDED — not in plan]

Problem→Solution stub (the decode pool is sized differently from colocated — this equation is derived nowhere else). Problem: the pool ratio R fixes the flow balance, not the decode pool’s stock ceiling. Colocated, prefill and decode share one GPU’s HBM; disaggregated, the decode pool is a shared KV memory pool fed by many prefill servers, so its max concurrency ≈ ΣHBM_decode / E[KV_per_req] — and the tail breaks it: a few long-context requests blow E[KV] and collapse achievable batch, so the decode roofline ridge B* is unreachable because KV-capacity, not BW, binds first. Heterogeneous & cross-generation pools — the dominant $/req lever, and its constraints [ADDED — not in plan]

Problem→Solution stub (§2 config-asymmetry named per-pool SKU as a parallelism point; this is the cost-shape reason disaggregation exists, and the constraints it imposes). Problem: the headline economic argument for the split is not interference — it is that decode (BW/capacity-bound) runs well on cheaper, older, high-HBM GPUs while prefill (compute/TTFT-bound) wants the newest compute GPUs; colocated forces both phases onto one SKU and one phase massively overpays. The KV-transport policy spectrum (0)–(4) [rule 11]

Selection-knob sweep (rule 11) — how the KV crosses prefill→decode is a first-class knob. Deep dive D8 — the forward handoff (prefill→decode): layer-wise pipelined KV transfer

The control-plane handoff — the request carries more than KV [ADDED — not in plan]

Problem→Solution stub (the companion to D8: same direction, the non-KV half of the handoff). Problem: a request is physically split across two machines mid-life. D8 moves the KV bytes, but the decode pool also needs the request’s full generation context — sampling params (temperature/top-p/top-k), logit bias, stop sequences, seed, max_tokens — and the gateway↔decode streaming-channel binding (the response streams from the decode pool, but the client connected to a gateway). Deep-dive stub (the asymmetric reverse of D8; K5 owns the byte transport, K2 Role 2 owns the route — D7 below). Problem (threaded on the running trace): at T3 the user sends a follow-up turn, so the reusable KV for T1+T2 already exists — but it sits on the decode server that finished T2, while T3 must prefill on a prefill server that does not hold it. The forward handoff (D8) is push, synchronous, hidden under prefill compute; the reverse has the opposite trigger — a cache hit discovered at admission, decode→prefill, pull. Solution — the four byte-level mechanisms, each stated exactly: (a) transfer-back pull — the prefill server looks up a cluster KV index, RDMA-pulls the matched blocks straight into its own block table (K2 D4), prefills only the uncached suffix; (b) skip-prefill reroute — whole-prompt hit ⇒ never prefill, route the request straight to a decode server already holding the KV (zero transfer); (c) shared global KV pool read — both pools map one KV store, the prefill server reads the blocks in place (+one network hop per access, no copy); (d) recompute — no transfer at all, just re-prefill (the fallback when the link is the bottleneck). The crossover (both directions): transfer-back beats recompute iff KV_bytes/RDMA_BW < prefill_FLOPs(matched_span)/throughput — long shared prefix + fast RDMA → pull; short span or slow link → recompute. The consistency failure unique to the reverse: between the index lookup and the pull the decode server is free to LRU-evict the block, or a deploy version-bumps it → stale-KV / pull-miss; fix = refcount-pin on lookup + model/version/quant/position folded into the key (cite K2 D5) + fallback-to-recompute on miss. Decode-side retention contends decode HBM: keeping turn-1 KV warm on a decode GPU for a likely turn-2 (so the reverse-handoff can hit) competes with that GPU’s active-decode batch HBM — the decode pool now doubles as a between-turn cache; rule of two: K2 owns the eviction policy, K5 owns the cross-pool placement (retain on decode vs push to a store vs accept recompute).number: transfer-back vs recompute crossover on T3’s matched span on the trace; decode-HBM split active-batch vs retained-KV — cites K2 D4 (block table the pull DMAs into), K2 D5 (key/version), K2 Role 2 (the route — D7 below), K20 (RDMA BW) · real: Mooncake transfer-back / global KV index, NVIDIA Dynamo KV-aware + URL · canonical: gpu-failure-modes.html (evict / version-stale mid-transfer)

Cross-pool backpressure & the KV-handoff buffer [ADDED — not in plan]

Problem→Solution stub (a real production gap the plan’s pool-ratio derivation assumes away). Problem: the pool ratio R is derived from average traffic, but instantaneous mix drifts — the prefill pool can momentarily outrun the decode pool; without flow control the KV-handoff queue grows unbounded (KV produced with nowhere to land) or the shared transport NIC head-of-lines when many prefills finish at once (a TTFT spike from transport queueing, not compute). The handoff buffer’s physical home — decode congestion silently taxes prefill capacity [ADDED — not in plan]

Problem→Solution stub (the sharp coupling that survives the split — sharper than the queue-growth framing above). Problem: a request whose prefill finished but whose decode slot is not yet free must park its KV somewhere — and the default somewhere is prefill-pool HBM. That KV occupies HBM the prefill pool would otherwise batch with ⇒ decode-pool congestion shrinks the prefill batch and lowers prefill throughput — the exact prefill↔decode coupling disaggregation was sold to remove, reintroduced through the buffer’s location. Solution (a placement crossover): (i) park in prefill HBM — zero-copy, but taxes the prefill batch; (ii) evict to a host/NVMe or global KV store — frees prefill HBM, adds a hop + write BW; (iii) reserve the decode slot before prefill commits so the KV is born with a home — cleanest, but couples admission across pools (the control-plane section). Pick by how often decode back-pressures: rare → (i); sustained → (iii) + (ii) as overflow.number: prefill-batch shrinkage per GB of parked KV vs the decode-backpressure rate on the trace; the (i)→(iii) crossover — cites the backpressure section (the flow-control bound), K2 #d4 (block table) #tier-ladder (the spill path), the control-plane section (slot-reserve-before-commit) · real: Mooncake KV-store overflow, Splitwise · canonical: gpu-inference-cost-shapes.html (stranded prefill HBM)

Speculative-decoding interaction — it moves R and adds a draft-placement question [ADDED — not in plan]

Problem→Solution stub (K13 changes the decode roofline the pool ratio is derived from — entirely absent otherwise). Problem: if the decode pool runs speculative decoding (K13), each decode step verifies K draft tokens → the step is more compute-heavy and accepted-tokens/step is variable ⇒ D_decode shifts, so the traffic-derived R is wrong if computed from the non-speculative step. And the draft model is a new placement decision: in the decode pool (competes for its HBM/compute), its own micro-pool, or co-resident with prefill. Solution: derive R from the effective decode step under the measured acceptance rate α (cite K13/D11), and place the draft as a tiny co-resident model on the decode pool by default (its HBM ≪ the target’s, and it must see the same KV) — flip to a separate micro-pool only if draft compute starves target decode.number: R recomputed with the speculative effective decode-step at α vs the non-spec R on the trace — cites K13 (acceptance math), D11 (spec-under-batching — cite, don’t re-derive), K1 #decode · cross-link L8

Asymmetric pool scale-down — decode drains long generations, prefill drains instantly [ADDED — not in plan]

Problem→Solution stub (the K15 cross-link covers autoscaling generically; this asymmetry is K5-specific). Problem: the two pools have opposite scale-down costs. A prefill GPU drains in ≈ one prompt’s prefill time (sub-second–seconds) — cheap to reclaim. A decode GPU may hold 30k-token in-flight generations — draining it means waiting out (or migrating, K22) minutes of generation; reclaiming it abruptly throws away the most accumulated compute. A single symmetric autoscaler reclaims a decode GPU as readily as a prefill GPU and torches in-flight work. Solution: per-pool scale-down policy — prefill: aggressive, drain-and-reclaim; decode: lazy, longest-generation-aware draining + a reclaim-cost-weighted victim choice. Scale-up is symmetric (both cold-start, K21); scale-down is not.number: decode-GPU drain time at the reasoning-length tail vs prefill-GPU drain time on the trace; wasted GPU-s of an abrupt decode reclaim — cites K15 (per-pool autoscale), K22 (in-flight recovery on reclaim), K21 (symmetric scale-up cold-start) · canonical: gpu-inference-cost-shapes.html
