K15 — GPU-Fleet Autoscaling & Multi-Tenancy (scale on the right signal, pack many tenants)

How do you build a scalable, multi-tenant GPU fleet — when GPUs are expensive and take tens of seconds to warm, traffic is 10:1 bursty, and many models/tenants share the hardware — by scaling on a leading signal (not GPU-util, which saturates after latency degrades) with headroom ≥ the cold-start budget, then bin-packing and isolating tenants on what is left? This page derives why GPU-util is the wrong signal, then D12 (right signal + prewarm, incl. capacity-stockout & cache-warm headroom), D58 (scale-down / drain asymmetry), D19 (power/thermal ceiling), the K15 half of D18 (placement/quota + burstable-reclaim), and D43 (heterogeneous bin-packing across GPU generations).

SLIs This Page Reduces

SLIs this page reduces (the reverse index of K1 §3.12):
  • Queue-depth / TTFT under burst (primary) — scale on a leading signal with headroom ≥ cold-start so capacity is hot before the queue grows.
  • Cost-shape ($/mo) — minimise idle GPU-hours: scale-to-zero idle models, pack heterogeneous models, share the base across tenants.
  • Per-tenant tail latency — fleet-level placement + quota bounds the noisy-neighbour blast radius (the K15 half of D18; on-GPU isolation is K4’s).
  • Achieved MFU vs spec — D19: sustained power/thermal derate means the fleet must be sized on achieved, not spec, throughput.
  • Lost-work / killed-request rate on scale-down & reclaim — D58 drains in-flight long generations before tear-down; D18 bounds burstable-quota reclaim so a scale-in or tenant-reclaim doesn’t kill in-flight work.

The Autoscaling Spine (stated up front, before §1)

  • GPUs are expensive and slow to warm; traffic is 10:1 bursty → scale the fleet with load.
  • Problem: cold-start is tens of seconds, so reactive (util-based) scaling blows the SLO — the scale-up arrives after the breach.
  • → scale on a leading signal (queue-depth / TTFT-slope) + warm headroom ≥ cold-start time (D12).
  • Problem [added]: the scale-up can fail — the cloud returns no capacity (spot reclaim / on-demand stockout / quota cap / region-out) → capacity-tiered fallback (committed floor + on-demand + spot); headroom is also a reserved-capacity decision, not only a time budget.
  • Problem [added]: a fresh replica is not at steady-state — its K2-Role-2 reuse cache is cold → headroom must cover cold_start + cache_warm, not cold_start alone.
  • Problem: headroom is expensive → scale-to-zero idle models → cold-start tax → snapshot/restore (K21 owns the mechanism — cite, don’t duplicate).
  • Problem [added] — the twin nobody states: scaling down ≠ scaling up — a replica picked for tear-down may hold in-flight 30k-token generations; kill = lost work + SLO breach, wait = slower scale-down = keep paying for idle GPUs → D58: stop-accept→lame-duck→drain-timeout→kill, the drain-wait $ vs killed-work crossover.
  • Problem: many models/tenants share the fleet → bin-packing + fragmentation + noisy-neighbour.
  • → multiplex (S-LoRA shared base) + isolate (WFQ — K4/D18; + burstable/work-conserving quota & the reclaim-latency tax [added]) + MIG + fleet placement (D18 K15-half) + heterogeneous-GPU-generation [added] packing (D43, across A100/H100/H200/B200).
  • And: sustained power/thermal derates achieved MFU below spec, and datacenter-kW — not budget — is the real ceiling on “just add GPUs” (D19); and the control loop’s dead-time = cold-start → high gain oscillates [added] (feed-forward prewarm + dead-time-aware hysteresis, not just min-dwell).
  • Objective: meet the SLO through a 10:1 burst at the minimum $ — minimum idle GPU-hours and the right reserved-floor + on-demand + spot purchase-mix [added].

1. The Problem It Solves

Problem→Solution stub. Problem: a GPU is the most expensive unit in the system and takes tens of seconds to load a model and warm up (cite K21), while real traffic swings ~10:1 diurnally and bursts faster than that — static peak-provisioning wastes most of the fleet most of the time, and naive reactive scaling cannot add capacity fast enough to hold the SLO. Solution (what the page builds): a fleet controller that scales on a leading signal with headroom matched to the cold-start budget, then packs and isolates many models/tenants onto the capacity it keeps.number: idle GPU-hours wasted by static peak-provisioning at 10:1 on the trace (the $ the page attacks) — cites K1 (fleet/cost), K21 (cold-start budget) · canonical: gpu-inference-cost-shapes.html (idle GPU-hours)

[ADDED] Problem→Solution stub (G6 — the scale unit is itself a knob). The walkthrough on the canonical trace — one request through a fleet that scales right vs wrong

Problem→Solution stub (hard requirement 6 — reader’s first contact). A burst begins; the canonical request T1 "I like cat, why?" arrives at t=0 as the fleet hits its knee. Why GPU-util is the wrong signal (derived — the page’s home)

Problem→Solution stub (the page’s opening derivation, sub-bulleted). Problem: the obvious autoscale signal — GPU utilisation — is structurally lagging. Solution, derived: (a) the memory-bound decode loop keeps the SM busy reading weights, so util saturates while the queue is still draining; (b) at the knee, util ≈ flat (~60%) but queue ∝ (λ−µ)·t grows linearly ⇒ TTFT explodes while util still reads ~60%; (c) therefore the signal must be the derivative of latency / the queue depth, and the control horizon must be ≥ the cold-start time or the correction lands after the breach (cite K1 #decode for why decode keeps util high; K21 for the horizon). This is why D12’s signal spectrum exists.number: the (util, queue, TTFT) curves at the knee on the trace — util ~60% flat while TTFT 10×s — cites K1 #decode, K21 (cold-start horizon) · diagram: three stacked time-series (util flat, queue linear, TTFT exploding) with the knee marked

[ADDED] Problem→Solution stub (G2 + G5 — D12’s headroom is incomplete). Problem (G2 — the scale-up can fail): headroom = cold_start × ramp assumes the GPUs exist; at frontier scale the dominant scale-up tail risk is the cloud returning no capacity (spot reclaim, on-demand stockout, quota cap, region-out). Solution: capacity-tiered targets — a committed/reserved floor + on-demand for the diurnal band + spot for the burst tail; an unsatisfiable-scale-up fallback (degrade per K4/D13 rather than wait forever); headroom expressed as a reserved-capacity reservation, not only a time budget. Deep dive D58 [ADDED] — scale-down ≠ scale-up: draining in-flight long generations before tear-down

[ADDED] Deep-dive stub (the page’s biggest structural hole — the whole arc above is scale-up). Problem: the autoscaler decides to remove a replica (scale-in, scale-to-zero, or a spot reclaim it must front-run) — but that replica may hold in-flight 30k-token reasoning generations. Kill them → lost GPU-hours already spent + a hard SLO breach for those users; wait for them → scale-down is slow, so you keep paying for the idle GPU exactly when the cost objective wants it gone — the asymmetry that makes scale-down a first-class policy, not the mirror of scale-up. Solution: a staged drain — (1) deregister from the router (stop accepting new requests), (2) lame-duck (finish in-flight, optionally migrate short ones), (3) drain-timeout, (4) force-kill survivors with recovery per K22/D23 (replay-from-prompt vs drop). Crossover (a formula, not a vibe): set the drain-timeout where marginal idle-GPU-$ of waiting Δt > P(req still running at Δt) × (lost-work GPU-$ + SLO-violation cost) — the long tail of generation length (cross-link K19/D47) sets the survival curve, so a reasoning fleet needs a far longer drain than a short-output one. Rule of two: K22/D23 owns unplanned group-fault recovery; L7 owns drain-on-deploy; K15 owns the autoscaler’s planned scale-in — when to reclaim a replica and the drain-vs-kill cost knob — cite K22 for the in-flight-recovery mechanism, don’t duplicate.number: drain-timeout that minimises (idle-GPU-$ + killed-work-$) on the trace using the K19/D47 generation-length tail; killed-request count vs drain-wait minutes, both directions — cites K1 (GPU-$/min), K19/D47 (length tail), K22/D23 (recovery), K4 (lame-duck stop-accept) · real: Kubernetes preStop + terminationGracePeriodSeconds, Ray Serve graceful-shutdown drain, vLLM abort, KServe drain + URLs · cross-link L5 (spot reclaim = an induced scale-down), L7 (planned-drain twin)

Deep dive D19 — power/thermal: the sustained-MFU derate & the datacenter-kW ceiling (co-host K1)

[ADDED] Problem→Solution stub (G7 — the missing half of D18-K15). Problem: strict per-tenant fleet quota wastes GPUs — a tenant’s reserved slice sits idle while another tenant is throttled below the idle fleet. Work-conserving (burstable) quota lets a tenant borrow idle capacity — but when the owner’s burst returns the borrowed GPU must be reclaimed: the borrowing model is evicted/cold-started off it → an induced cold-start (a reclaim-latency tax), and a reclaim storm if many tenants burst together. Solution: burstable quota with a guaranteed floor + a bounded, prioritised reclaim path (preempt borrowed lowest-priority first — K4/D18 on-GPU; K15 chooses which borrowed replica), reclaim-rate limiting + hysteresis to avoid the storm. Crossover: borrow aggressiveness — more borrowing recovers more idle GPU-$ but raises reclaim-latency risk for the owner; pick the floor/ceiling where owner-tail-latency stays under SLO. Rule of two: K4/D18 owns the on-GPU preemption; K15 owns the fleet-level borrow/reclaim policy.number: idle GPU-$ recovered by burstable vs strict quota, vs the owner reclaim-latency it costs, on the trace — cites K4 #d18 (on-GPU preempt), K21 (induced cold-start) · real: YARN/K8s burstable QoS, Run:ai over-quota + fairness, Slurm preemption + URLs

Deep dive D43 — heterogeneous-fleet bin-packing & GPU fragmentation

Deep-dive stub. Problem: a fleet serving a 70B (2 GPUs), several 13B (1 GPU), and 50k LoRA adapters over a shared 70B base — naive placement strands GPUs (a node with 1 free GPU can’t host a 2-GPU model = fragmentation) and a hot model can’t grow because its GPUs are scattered. Solution: best-fit-decreasing placement by GPU footprint, MIG slicing for sub-GPU models, S-LoRA/Punica shared-base multiplexing (50k adapters ≈ 1 base + tiny per-adapter delta), gang-scheduling for multi-GPU models, periodic defrag/consolidation. Crossover: MIG slice granularity (finer = better packing, worse per-slice perf + isolation); consolidate vs migration cost.number: stranded GPUs under naive vs best-fit on the trace fleet mix; S-LoRA base-sharing factor — cites K1 (footprints), K10 (replica unit) · real: Run:ai, NVIDIA MIG, S-LoRA/Punica, KubeRay placement groups + URLs

[ADDED] Problem→Solution stub (G4 — one fleet, many GPU generations). Fleet cost-mix — the reserved / on-demand / spot purchase portfolio [ADDED, G3]

[ADDED] Problem→Solution stub (the real $ knob the objective hides). Problem: “minimum idle GPU-hours” ignores that the same GPU-hour has three prices — the dominant fleet cost lever is the purchase portfolio, not just how many GPUs run. Solution: a reserved/committed-use floor sized to the diurnal trough (cheapest $/hr, must commit) + on-demand for the predictable diurnal band + spot/preemptible for the burst tail (cheapest, can vanish — ties D58 reclaim + the G2 stockout fallback). Crossover (a formula): commit reserved up to the load level where P(running) × (on-demand − reserved) > (1 − P(running)) × reserved-idle-$ — over-commit pays for idle reserved, under-commit pays the on-demand premium + spot-stockout risk. 4. Alternatives & When Each Wins

Stub (≥2, disqualifying constraint per alt). (a) Static peak-provisioning — disq: pays peak GPU-hours 24/7 (the naive baseline); wins only when traffic is flat and the SLO is unforgiving and budget is unconstrained. (b) Reactive util-based HPA — disq: lags the SLO by the cold-start time (derived in §2); wins when cold-start « SLO (small CPU services, not GPU LLM). (c) Over-provision + no multi-tenancy (dedicated GPU per model) — disq: strands capacity, no base-sharing; wins for a single huge tenant with strict isolation needs. (d) Per-tenant billing / API rate-limiting as the control — disq: API-product layer, not a GPU mechanism; out of scope — flag the boundary, do not cover. [ADDED] (e) all-on-demand (no reserved/spot mix) — disq: pays the on-demand premium on the whole diurnal band; wins only when traffic is wholly unpredictable and commitment is impossible (see the G3 cost-mix stub).
