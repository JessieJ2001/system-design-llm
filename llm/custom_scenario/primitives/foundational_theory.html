Foundational Theory

The five impossibility / framing results every other primitive in this catalogue dances around: CAP, PACELC, FLP, the Two Generals problem, and the End-to-End argument. They aren’t deployable artefacts — they’re the laws that explain why every deployable primitive looks the way it does. Naming them is half of pattern recognition; the other half is knowing which one a given symptom is bumping up against.

The five laws on one map

TL;DR. CAP and PACELC describe the cost of consistency under (and outside) partitions. FLP and Two Generals describe what consensus and delivery cannot guarantee at all. The End-to-End argument decides where in the stack to put the check that closes the gap the other four leave open.

The five laws by scope. Top row = partition-time and consensus-impossibility laws. Bottom row = the law that generalises CAP to no-partition operation (PACELC) and the law that says where to put the check that closes any of these gaps (End-to-End).

Pattern signatures — the recognition cards

One card per law — the recognition card for that pattern; the thing you scan when a symptom hits your desk and you haven’t yet named what you’re looking at.

Why this format exists. The hard skill in distributed systems isn’t algorithm recall (textbooks have that); it’s seeing a symptom and naming the right pattern. A long deep-dive helps you act on a pattern; it doesn’t help you recognise one in the first place. The cards compress “recognise” into ~6 lines so you can scan all 5 in under a minute during an incident — and then dive into the matching primitive’s full structure only once you’ve named it. Read these first; the deep-dives below are for after the name is on the board.

CAP theorem

Brewer 2000 · Gilbert & Lynch 2002

Statement — in an asynchronous network with possible message loss, no replicated data store can simultaneously provide linearizability (C) and availability (A) when a network partition (P) occurs. Since partitions are a fact of distributed deployment, every operation during a partition picks C-or-A; the “pick 2 of 3” folklore is the loose version of this.

Smell test — “what does the service do when the WAN to us-west drops?” · “the database froze when one AZ went unreachable” · “we built it to be 100 % consistent and always available.”

Confusables — PACELC (same axis, also names the no-partition trade — ask “is the bug only during a netsplit?”) · FLP (consensus liveness, not partition-time C-vs-A — ask “is the symptom ‘election never finishes’ or ‘reads return stale’?”).

PACELC

Abadi 2012 — the “else” clause CAP misses

Statement — if a Partition (P) exists, the system must choose between Availability (A) and Consistency (C) — same as CAP; else (E), in steady state, it must still choose between Latency (L) and Consistency (C). Every synchronous-replication knob is the ELC trade made explicit; CAP only covers the rare partition case, PACELC covers the every-write case.

Smell test — “writes are 8 ms locally but 80 ms because we wait for the EU replica — can we relax?” · “the SLO is fine but p99 doubled when we turned on synchronous replication.”

Confusables — CAP (CAP covers only partition-time; if there’s no partition and the trade still hurts, it’s PACELC’s ELC half).

FLP impossibility

Fischer · Lynch · Paterson 1985

Statement — in a purely asynchronous message-passing system where even one process may crash, no deterministic consensus protocol can guarantee both safety (no two nodes decide differently) and liveness (every correct node eventually decides). At least one execution exists where the protocol runs forever without deciding. The escape hatch every real consensus library uses is timeouts — trading pure asynchrony for partial synchrony.

Smell test — “the Raft cluster keeps electing and re-electing — never stable” · “why does every consensus library expose an election timeout?” · “a single slow task is stalling the whole quorum.”

Confusables — CAP (CAP is about C-vs-A; FLP is about whether consensus terminates) · Two Generals (FLP is internal to a consensus group; Two Generals is between two endpoints over a lossy link).

Two Generals problem

Gray 1978 / Akkoyunlu et al. 1975

Statement — two parties communicating over an unreliable channel (messages may be lost) cannot achieve common knowledge of a coordinated decision via any finite exchange of messages. Inductive proof: whatever the last message in the protocol is, the sender doesn’t know if it arrived — so it can’t be the basis of agreement. The practical consequence: “exactly-once delivery” over an unreliable network is impossible; the only fix is making the effect idempotent at the endpoint.

Smell test — “the payment timed out — did Stripe charge or not?” · “we need exactly-once delivery to Kafka” · “the webhook says ‘sent’ but the partner’s DB says ‘not received.’”

Confusables — FLP (FLP is about agreement within a quorum; Two Generals is about agreement between two parties over a flaky channel) · idempotency (idempotency is the workaround, not the law).

End-to-End argument

Saltzer, Reed & Clark 1984

Statement — a function (reliability, integrity, exactly-once effect, encryption against a specific adversary) can be completely and correctly implemented only with the knowledge and help of the application standing at the endpoints of the system. Lower-layer implementations of the same function (TCP checksums, LB retries, middlebox validators) may be useful as performance optimisations, but they are never sufficient as the proof of correctness — the authoritative check belongs at the endpoint.

Smell test — “TCP gave us a checksum — why do we still need a SHA-256 on the upload?” · “the load balancer retried but the user got billed twice” · “our middlebox dropped the trailing CRLF.”

Confusables — defence-in-depth (defence-in-depth says “check in many places”; end-to-end says “the only check that proves correctness lives at the endpoints — the others are optimisations”).

The recognition reflex. When a symptom hits your desk, first answer “is this partition-time, steady-state, or end-of-protocol?” That single question maps to CAP, PACELC, or Two Generals respectively. If the answer is “the agreement protocol itself isn’t terminating,” it’s FLP. If it’s “we’re relying on a middle layer to prove correctness,” it’s End-to-End.

Deep-dive: CAP theorem

The other four laws will follow the same structure. This first one is fleshed out fully so the section template can be reviewed before it’s repeated.

§1Pattern signature

  • Pattern name — CAP theorem; in design-review speech, “the partition-time consistency-or-availability choice.”
  • Smell test — three things you actually hear:
    • “What does the service return when us-east can’t reach us-west?” (the design-review form)
    • “The orders page went 503 for 90 seconds during the WAN flap.” (the postmortem form — CP system saying “rather error than lie”)
    • “Why are there two open shopping carts for the same user?” (the bug-report form — AP system that accepted writes on both sides of a partition)
  • Confusables (with the distinguishing question) —
    • PACELC. Looks like CAP but covers the no-partition case too. Ask: “is the bug only during a netsplit, or also in the happy path?” If the latter, it’s PACELC’s ELC half.
    • FLP. Looks like CAP because both are “the distributed system can’t do X.” Ask: “is the symptom ‘reads return stale / writes get rejected’ (CAP) or ‘the quorum never agrees’ (FLP)?”

§2The problem

  • Scenario — a 3-replica Spanner-style KV holds the key user-42:balance, replicated to us-east1, us-central1, and europe-west1. The transatlantic link between europe-west1 and the two US regions drops for 12 minutes. Two clients write at the same time: one in Frankfurt deducts €50, one in Iowa deducts $50. The store has no choice that keeps both writes succeeding and both clients reading a linearizable balance. It must either reject one (CP — sacrifice availability on the minority side) or accept both and reconcile later (AP — sacrifice linearizability).
  • What it looks like before someone names it —
    • Code review: “Let’s just write to both regions and read from either — should be fine.”
    • Bug report: “Bought one ticket; got charged for one; itinerary shows zero seats reserved.”
    • Dashboard shape: error-rate flat at 0 %, then a vertical cliff to 50 % the instant the inter-region link drops — exactly the keys whose primary is on the unreachable side.
  • What people did before — assumed the network was reliable (one of Deutsch’s “fallacies of distributed computing”). The classic break: a master – slave Postgres pair with synchronous replication and a single VIP, where a partition between master and slave hangs every write until ops manually flips.
  • The simplest version — N = 2 replicas R₁, R₂, 1 key x, 1 client. Link between R₁ and R₂ drops. Client writes x = 1 at R₁, then reads x at R₂. R₂ can either reject (CP) or return the old value (AP). No third option exists in this state space.
  • Formal name — CAP theorem; conjectured by Brewer (2000), proved by Gilbert & Lynch (2002). The formal “C” is linearizability — not “consistency” in the loose ACID sense.

§3Theory grounding

CAP is the partition-time corner of a larger landscape. Linearizability sits at the strong end of the consistency-model spectrum; availability here means “every non-failing node answers every request” (a much stricter notion than “the service is up”). CAP says: during a partition, the intersection of those two is empty. PACELC (next deep-dive) extends this to steady state by adding the L/C trade you pay on every write even when no partition exists; FLP tells you why even outside a partition, consensus needs timeouts to make progress — which is why every real CP system (Raft, Paxos, Zab) is technically a partially-synchronous system, not an asynchronous one.

Serializable vs linearizable vs strict serializable — a worked example. Two transactions on disjoint keys, where the only thing that varies between models is whether real-time ordering and transactional atomicity are both enforced.

T1 (commits at 10:00): write(x=1); write(y=1)
T2 (starts at 10:01): read(x); read(y)
  • Serializable only — T2 can be ordered before T1 → reads x=0, y=0. Legal, even though wall clock says T1 finished first.
  • Linearizable only — doesn’t apply — these are transactions, not single ops. (If we treated each read/write individually, T2’s reads would see T1’s writes, but T2 could see x=1, y=0 if interleaved — violating transactional atomicity.)
  • Strict serializable — T2 must be ordered after T1 → reads x=1, y=1. Atomic and respects real-time.

The full consistency-level spectrum

TL;DR. Two orthogonal axes generate the spectrum: real-time ordering (does an op completing at t₁ have to be visible to ops starting at t₂ > t₁?) and transactional atomicity (do groups of ops appear all-or-nothing?). Strict serializability is the only level that demands both at full strength; each weaker level drops part of one or the other and recovers latency, availability, or both in exchange.

Level (strongest → weakest) Guarantee Use when Coordination cost (engineering + perf) Real systems
Strict serializable
= serializable + linearizable		 Multi-op txns equivalent to a serial order that respects real-time. The strongest possible model. Money transfers, inventory decrement, cross-shard balance moves — anywhere “A finished before B started” must hold globally. Needs a single source of truth across the cluster. Achieved by global consensus (Paxos/Raft) + synchronized clocks (Spanner’s TrueTime, ~7 ms commit-wait) or a single global leader. Every write pays a cross-region round-trip (~70 ms us-east↔EU). Minority side unavailable under partition. Schema changes are global and slow. Spanner, CockroachDB, FoundationDB, FaunaDB
Serializable
txns, no real-time		 Multi-op txns equivalent to some serial order — not necessarily wall-clock. A txn that started later may legally be ordered before one that committed earlier. Single-region OLTP business logic (order processing, ledger updates) where no external observer compares results across regions out-of-band. See note below on why “intra-region” is the boundary. No global clock or leader required. SSI (Snapshot Serializable Isolation) does MVCC + write-conflict detection on commit and aborts losers. 2PL holds row locks until commit. Cost is mostly in abort-and-retry under contention, not in coordination round-trips. PostgreSQL SERIALIZABLE, MySQL InnoDB SERIALIZABLE, YugabyteDB
Linearizable
single op, real-time		 Each individual read/write is atomic and totally ordered by real-time. No multi-op atomicity — per key only. Distributed locks, leader election, config registers, atomic counters, fencing tokens — anywhere one key is the coordination point. Leader + quorum write per op: 1× intra-region round-trip (~2 ms), 1× cross-region (~70 ms). Multi-key atomicity needs a separate 2PC layer on top — pure linearizability won’t give it to you. Zookeeper, etcd, Chubby, Raft, Spanner single-key reads
Sequential
total order, no real-time		 All ops appear in some total order consistent with each client’s program order. Wall-clock skew is invisible. Rarely a target in modern distributed DBs — the classic home is shared-memory multiprocessor memory models. Some replicated logs expose it. Same machinery as linearizable minus the real-time bound — reads may be served from a slightly stale snapshot if the per-client order is preserved. In practice the saving over linearizable is small enough that few systems target this level explicitly. x86 memory model (intra-CPU), some legacy replicated KV
Snapshot isolation
MVCC default		 Each txn reads from a consistent snapshot at start; commits succeed if no concurrent txn wrote the same keys. Allows write-skew anomalies that serializable forbids. OLTP default for most CRUD apps — reports, dashboards, user profile edits where write-skew is rare or tolerable. MVCC stores per-row version chains; commit time validates only write-set conflicts. No global ordering needed. Cheap reads (snapshot is local), cheap writes until contention. PostgreSQL REPEATABLE READ, Oracle, SQL Server, CockroachDB default
Causal
happens-before respected		 Causally related ops are seen in the same order by all replicas; concurrent ops may be observed in different orders by different observers. Social feeds (reply must follow post), comment threads, collaborative editing, chat — anywhere user-perceived causality matters but global order doesn’t. Each write carries a vector clock or dependency vector; reads block until dependencies are visible locally. No consensus, no global clock. Metadata grows with active writers; merge logic resolves concurrent siblings. COPS, Riak with vector clocks, Bayou, Cosmos DB “consistent prefix”
Session (RYW)
read-your-writes, monotonic reads		 A single session sees its own writes immediately and never observes the data going backward. Other sessions get no such guarantee. User-facing apps where “I just edited my profile — where is it?” matters but cross-user ordering doesn’t. Cheapest enforcement: pin the session to a single replica (sticky routing). Alternative: client tracks the last-write LSN and the server waits for that LSN before serving the next read. No cluster-wide coordination. MongoDB causal-consistent sessions, Cassandra with sticky load-balancing, most RDBMS with session affinity
Eventual
no ordering		 If writes stop, all replicas converge to the same value eventually. No guarantee about when or about the order observed in between. Like-counters, view counts, DNS, geo-replicated session stores, recommendation caches — tolerant of staleness, intolerant of latency. The cheapest model. Async replication + gossip + read-repair; no synchronous round-trips on the write path. Application must absorb stale reads and (sometimes) merge conflicts. DynamoDB default, Cassandra CL=ONE, DNS, Bigtable cross-cluster
Why “intra-region correctness, but not cross-region real-time” for serializable. Serializable lets the system pick any legal serial order — including one that disagrees with wall-clock. That freedom is invisible inside a single region: a single primary serialises txns in arrival order, and arrival order tracks wall-clock closely enough that no observer can demonstrate a real-time violation. The freedom only becomes a bug when an external observer compares results across regions out-of-band:
  • EU client commits T1 (deduct €50) at 10:00, then Slacks a US client “I just paid.”
  • US client reads at 10:01, but serializable is free to place the US read before T1 in the chosen serial order — the read returns the pre-T1 balance.
  • The user has caught the system “going backwards” relative to real time. Strict-serializable forbids this; plain serializable allows it.
So: pick serializable when all observers route through one region (or never compare notes across regions out-of-band); pick strict-serializable when they do. The cross-region Slack-message is the canonical leak path — if your domain has any out-of-band channel between observers, you need real-time ordering.
The cost of moving up the spectrum. The jumps aren’t linear — most of the cost concentrates at exactly two boundaries:
  • Crossing into linearizable / strict-serializable. Real-time ordering forces a single source of truth across the cluster — a leader, a quorum protocol, or synchronized clocks (Spanner’s TrueTime needs GPS + atomic clocks in every DC). Every write pays a cross-machine round-trip; under a partition, the minority side becomes unavailable (CAP). Strict-serializable additionally demands global multi-key atomicity, layered on top via 2PC over the consensus log.
  • Dropping below linearizable. Plain serializable can be implemented with snapshot-based concurrency control (SSI) on a single MVCC engine — no global clock, no leader, no cross-region commit-wait. Causal consistency drops further to vector clocks; eventual drops further to async gossip. Each step down trades a class of anomalies (write-skew, stale reads, lost updates) for a class of cost savings (no quorum, no commit-wait, no synchronous replication).
  • Concrete contrast. Strict-serializable Spanner: ~70 ms p50 cross-region write, GPS/atomic clock infrastructure, minority-region 503s on partition. Serializable Postgres-SSI: ~2 ms commit on a single primary, abort-and-retry under contention, no clock infrastructure, but no cross-region linearizability and no partition tolerance beyond that single primary.

Picking the level. The honest question isn’t “how strong can we make this?” (the answer is always “strict-serializable”) but “what is the weakest level that still rules out the bug class we cannot ship?” Money-double-spend → strict-serializable. Stock-listing showing “10 left” for a beat after sellout → bounded-staleness or eventual. Post showing before its parent comment → causal. The CAP, PACELC, FLP, Two-Generals, and End-to-End laws above are the constraints that price each row in this table.

§4The mechanism — a concrete tiny example

  • Setup — 3 tasks of a balance-store Borg job: balance-store-task-0 in us-east1-b, balance-store-task-1 in us-central1-a, balance-store-task-2 in europe-west1-d. They hold 5 keys, user-1…5:balance. Spanner-style quorum write (W = 2 of 3); leader is task-0.
  • Happy path — a deduction of $10 from user-3: client → task-0 (leader) → Paxos prepare to task-1 and task-2 → quorum ack → commit, reply.
  • Partition path — the transatlantic link drops. task-2 is now on the minority side (cut off from task-0 and task-1). On the majority side, task-0 + task-1 still form a quorum — writes continue. On the minority side, task-2 can’t form a quorum — any write attempted via task-2 blocks or fails. A linearizable read on the minority side likewise fails.
  • Invariants maintained —
    • No two clients ever see disagreeing committed values for the same key at the same logical time (linearizability holds on the majority side).
    • A committed write survives any minority-side loss (durability holds).
  • What it explicitly does NOT guarantee —
    • That the minority side returns anything but errors. (CAP’s entire content.)
    • That latency stays bounded outside a partition — that’s PACELC’s ELC half.
    • That two operations are independent — conflicting concurrent writes are still serialised by the leader, not magically commutative.
  • Complexity — CAP isn’t an algorithm; it’s an envelope. The cost lives in the algorithm you pick to live inside the envelope (Paxos: 2 round-trips per write; Raft: 1 round-trip after leader election; etc.).

§5Key insight + consistency model provided

  • The trick, in one sentence — during a partition, the minority side cannot tell whether the majority side is alive (and disagreeing) or dead, so it must either refuse to answer or risk answering wrong. There is no information available to it that would let it pick correctly.
  • Why naive doesn’t work — the naive “just read from your local replica and write to all replicas” design appears to give both C and A. It doesn’t: it gives eventual consistency, not linearizability, and the moment two clients hit different sides during a partition they observe disagreeing histories — the canonical “double-spend” bug.
  • Improvement margin — the naive design has unbounded staleness during a partition; a CP system bounds staleness to zero on the majority side (at the cost of 100 % unavailability on the minority side), and an AP system bounds staleness to the partition duration (at the cost of conflicts to merge).
  • What the CP algorithm is actually made of — a CP store like a Spanner directory or a Raft cluster is the composition of six moving parts. They interlock; each one closes a hole the others can’t.
    1. (C1) Leader — the single writer for a given key range that serialises the order of ops by sequence number. Source of the “one history” that linearizability requires.
    2. (C2) Quorum write protocol (Paxos / Raft) — every commit needs W > N/2 acks from replicas before reply. Ensures a committed write survives any minority loss.
    3. (C3) Persistent log — the leader writes the ordered ops to durable storage (Colossus, local SSD) on quorum before replying. Survives a leader crash; the basis of recovery.
    4. (C4) Failure detector + leader lease — a bounded-time mechanism (phi-accrual, Chubby lease, Raft election timeout) that decides “old leader is gone, elect a new one.” Turns the system from async (FLP-stuck) into partially-synchronous (live).
    5. (C5) Linearizable read protocol — the rule that says a read sees the latest commit. Three implementations: route to leader, leader-lease + read-index (Raft), or safe-time snapshot read (Spanner). Without one of these, reads from followers may be stale.
    6. (C6) Leader epoch / fencing token — a monotonically increasing generation number stamped on every write; storage rejects any write carrying a lower epoch than it has already accepted. Closes the “stale-but-alive leader after a GC pause” split-brain hole that (C4) alone cannot.
  • What breaks if you remove any one of them —
    • Remove (C1) the leader ⇒ two clients can independently propose conflicting orderings for the same key; you need a different agreement scheme (chain replication, leaderless EPaxos, CRDTs) and the C/A choice re-appears at that layer.
    • Remove (C2) the quorum requirement ⇒ a minority partition could also accept “commits”; both sides advance their logs independently ⇒ split-brain. You’ve traded CP for AP without naming the trade.
    • Remove (C3) the persistent log ⇒ a leader crash after acking but before disk-flush silently loses committed writes; durability collapses even though linearizability looked fine in steady state.
    • Remove (C4) the failure detector / lease ⇒ the cluster can’t tell “slow” from “partitioned,” so it either hangs forever (FLP) or elects a second leader while the first is just slow (split-brain).
    • Remove (C5) the linearizable read protocol ⇒ reads from followers may return values older than a write the same client just made — you get a CP write path sitting in front of an AP read path, which is the classic “our system is strongly consistent except when it isn’t” bug.
    • Remove (C6) the leader epoch / fencing token ⇒ the post-GC-pause stale leader keeps writing after a new leader was elected; both leaders’ writes land at the storage tier — the Kleppmann scenario, the canonical reason naive Redis-SETNX locks are unsafe.
  • Consistency model provided —
    • CP system: linearizability on the majority side; unavailability on the minority side. Promises real-time-ordered single-key reads/writes. Does not promise multi-key transactions unless a separate concurrency-control layer (Spanner’s TrueTime + 2PC, Calvin, etc.) sits on top.
      • “Majority side” / “minority side” defined — a partition splits the N replicas into two (or more) groups. The group that contains more than N/2 replicas is the majority side: it can still form a quorum and keep making progress. Any smaller group is a minority side: it cannot form a quorum and therefore cannot safely accept writes or confirm linearizable reads. For the balance-store with N = 3 and the EU link dropping: majority = {task-0, task-1} (2 of 3 can ack writes); minority = {task-2} (1 of 3 cannot ack anything).
      • What “linearizability on the majority side” means to a user — a client connecting to task-0 or task-1 sees a single, real-time-ordered history. If a write W returned 200 OK at wall-clock time t₁ and a read R is issued at t₂ > t₁, then R is guaranteed to see W’s value (or something newer). Writes commit, reads see the latest committed value, and the partition is invisible to these clients — they can’t tell from the API that anything is wrong.
      • What “unavailability on the minority side” means to a user — a client routed to task-2 sees its strict reads and all writes return errors (typically 503 quorum_unreachable or lease-expired) until the partition heals — the replica refuses to answer rather than risk returning a stale or unconfirmed value. “Unavailability” here is the precise CAP sense (“every non-failing node answers every request” is violated), not “the whole service is down”: the majority-side clients are still fine. Bounded-staleness reads (Spanner’s read_only with a max-staleness flag) can still succeed locally on task-2 because they explicitly opt out of linearizability — they’re an escape hatch CAP doesn’t forbid.
    • AP system: eventual consistency; with vector clocks or CRDTs, causal consistency. Promises convergence after the partition heals. Does not promise read-your-writes unless sessions pin to a specific replica.

§6Physical deployment

Same partition, two topologies, two behaviours. CP keeps correctness, sheds availability on the cut-off side; AP keeps availability everywhere, accepts conflicts to merge after the partition heals.

  • Client-side or server-side? — server-side. The CAP choice is encoded in the storage tier: Spanner (CP) vs. Cassandra (AP). Clients only see the consequence (503 vs. stale).
  • Dedicated job, or library inside an existing job? — always a dedicated job. CAP-relevant state is the database itself — a Spanner cell, a Bigtable instance, a Chubby cell — never a library bolted into app tasks. The reason: linearizability needs an agreement domain, and an agreement domain needs membership, which needs its own home.
  • Per-task vs sharded state — sharded with replication. Each Spanner directory (the unit of placement) is on exactly one Paxos group of typically 5 tasks across 5 failure domains; a Cassandra token range is on RF tasks (commonly 3). No single task holds the full state.
  • RAM, disk, separate store? — on disk (Colossus for Spanner, local SSD for Cassandra), in front of which sits a per-task block cache. The tier is dictated by durability, not by CAP.
  • How do tasks sync, staleness window? — CP: synchronous Paxos — followers are at most one round-trip behind on commit. AP: gossip + hinted handoff + read-repair — staleness is bounded by the hint TTL (Cassandra default 3 hours) plus repair cadence (default weekly).
  • Source of truth, rebuild from scratch — CP: the Paxos log on Colossus; rebuild = snapshot transfer + tail-of-log replay. AP: every replica is partial source of truth; rebuild = bootstrap from any peer + repair.
  • Named Google service —
    • CP default: Spanner directory pinned to a region triplet (e.g. us-east1 + us-central1 + europe-west1), Paxos replicas one per region zone, witness in a third region for tie-breaking. Or Chubby cell (5 tasks, one DC) for small, slow-changing data.
    • AP default: Bigtable replication for wide-column eventual; CRDT-style apps lean on application-layer Lamport/vector-clock merges sitting on top of a Bigtable.
  • Per-task cost —
    • CP (Spanner): ~32 GiB RAM + 1 TiB SSD per task; ~8 vCPU; one cross-region Paxos round-trip per write (~70 ms us-east ↔ europe-west1); ops complexity high (TrueTime, schema changes, leader leases).
    • AP (Cassandra): ~16 GiB RAM + 1 TiB SSD per task; ~4 vCPU; no cross-region round-trip on local-quorum writes; ops complexity medium (repair, compaction, gossip).

§7Request flow

  • 1client → task-2: write request lands at the EU replica.
  • 2task-2 forwards leader: Paxos prepare to task-0 & task-1.
  • 3quorum ack: 2 of 3 ack; leader commits to log.
  • 4200 OK: client sees the new balance.
  • 5partition, CP read: client retries read at EU.
  • 6503: CP system refuses rather than lie.
  • 7partition, AP read: Cassandra CL=LOCAL_ONE.
  • 8200 stale: AP system returns last-seen value.
  • Happy path — arrows 1–4 above. One round-trip from EU to the US leader; one Paxos round between US replicas. End-to-end ~80 ms (EU→us-east1) + ~30 ms (us-east1↔us-central1) ~= ~110 ms p50.
  • Hit / miss / error during the partition —
    • CP majority side: hit. Indistinguishable from happy path; latency may rise slightly while the failure detector confirms the EU replica is gone.
    • CP minority side: error. Every write and every linearizable read returns 503 quorum_unreachable; stale reads (Spanner read_only with a bounded staleness) can still succeed on the EU replica because they don’t need the leader.
    • AP either side: hit, but stale on the minority side until the partition heals.
  • Setup / warmup path — on cold start of a CP store, the new task fetches the latest snapshot from Colossus, replays the Paxos log from the snapshot LSN, and only joins the quorum once it’s caught up. During this window it doesn’t count toward the quorum — a fact that matters when sizing replication factor for tolerable simultaneous failures.

§8Integration with other primitives

  • What it sits next to —
    • Below CAP: consensus (Paxos / Raft / Zab) — the algorithm that implements the CP choice; quorum replication (Dynamo-style) — the algorithm that implements the AP choice; failure detectors — what tells the system “partition” in the first place.
    • Beside CAP: consistency-model spectrum — CAP picks the strong end (linearizable) vs. the weak end (eventual); intermediate models (causal, sequential) sit between and may relax the CP cost.
    • Above CAP: caching — an AP cache in front of a CP store re-introduces staleness deliberately for latency, recreating the CAP trade at the cache layer.
  • Pattern chains it participates in —
    • CP chain: Chubby lease → fencing token → Spanner write. The lease establishes who’s the unique writer (CAP-CP at the metadata tier); the fencing token survives a stale-but-not-dead leader (closes the gap that pure CP can’t close because the leader doesn’t know it’s lost the lease yet); the Spanner write commits the work linearizably.
    • AP chain: Dynamo quorum → hinted handoff → read repair → Merkle-tree anti-entropy. Quorum gives bounded consistency on the write path; hinted handoff buffers writes intended for unreachable replicas; read repair fixes drift on the read path; Merkle-tree repair closes the residual long-tail drift on a slow background loop.
    • Bridge chain: per-operation CAP. One store, two tiers of consistency — Cassandra CL=QUORUM for billing rows, CL=LOCAL_ONE for feed cards; Spanner strong vs. bounded-staleness reads on the same database.
  • What it depends on — a failure detector good enough to call partitions, and a quorum protocol that doesn’t accept a write without majority acks. Failure-detector tuning is where most production CAP bugs live.
  • What it makes harder downstream —
    • For CP: any multi-key transaction becomes a cross-shard 2PC over the consensus log — high latency, blocking on coordinator failure unless mitigated (Spanner uses TrueTime + paxos-per-shard).
    • For AP: every read at the application layer must be prepared to see siblings; UI must show “merging…” or pick a deterministic resolution; counters must be CRDT-typed (G-Counter) to avoid lost updates.
  • What commonly goes wrong at the seams — a CP store fronted by an AP cache: the cache accepts and serves stale data during the partition that the underlying store refused. The user sees “available” data that the source of truth never confirmed. Fix: tie cache lease to leader epoch.

§9Failure modes

  • CAP itself doesn’t “fail” — it’s a constraint. The failure mode is the consequence of your CAP choice when a partition actually happens.
  • CP failure mode —
    • Fail-closed: minority side returns errors. Blast radius: the fraction of clients homed to the minority partition (often 1 of 3 regions, ~33 % of traffic).
    • Recovery: automatic the moment the partition heals; minority replicas catch up via Paxos log replay; no merge work because nothing was accepted on the wrong side.
    • Latent failure: a stale-but-not-dead leader still believing it holds the lease. Mitigated by short leader leases (Chubby: 12 s) and fencing tokens on the write path.
  • AP failure mode —
    • Fail-open: both sides accept writes; clients see no errors. Blast radius: 0 client-visible errors, 100 % of writes during the partition will need merging.
    • Recovery: hinted handoff replays buffered writes; read repair and Merkle-tree repair close the rest; CRDT or LWW resolves conflicts. Recovery time = partition duration plus hint-replay backlog.
    • Latent failure: lost updates if the conflict-resolution rule is wrong (LWW with skewed clocks: the later-clock write wins even if its application-time was earlier).
  • The dangerous middle — systems that think they’re CP but lose linearizability under partition because of an unfenced cache, a stale read replica that’s eligible to serve, or a leader-election protocol with no quorum requirement. This is the “illusion of CP” bug class; it usually surfaces in a postmortem with the line “we didn’t realise X was a different consistency tier.”

§10Observability

  • Logs / metrics / traces to emit —
    • CP store: paxos_quorum_loss_count, leader_election_seconds, leader_epoch (in every write log entry), minority_partition_reject_count, commit_wait_ms (Spanner-specific, TrueTime cost). Log every leader transition with old and new epoch numbers.
    • AP store: hinted_handoff_backlog_bytes, read_repair_count, merkle_repair_seconds, divergent_replica_count, conflict_resolution_count (CRDT / LWW dispositions).
    • Both: cross-region link health from the failure detector (peer_phi_accrual), per-region client-error rate.
  • The single graph that tells you the primitive is healthy —
    • CP: quorum_reachable_replicas over time per Paxos group, with an SLO line at “≥ majority.” If this dips below majority for any group, the group is unavailable; if it dips often, you have a chronic networking problem masquerading as a database problem.
    • AP: hinted_handoff_backlog_bytes over time. Steady state should be near zero; growth signals an ongoing partition; sustained growth past hint TTL signals data loss.
  • Alerting — leading vs lagging —
    • Leading (about to break): peer_phi_accrual > 3 for 30 s (a replica is becoming unreachable but hasn’t failed yet); commit_wait_ms p99 > 50 for a Spanner directory (TrueTime uncertainty is widening — a GPS antenna problem).
    • Lagging (already broke): quorum_unreachable_count > 0 for any group (CP, you’re unavailable for some keys right now); hint_replay_lag_seconds > hint_ttl_seconds (AP, you’re past the point where the system can self-heal — data loss imminent).

§11Scale and capacity

  • When the naive version stops working — a single Paxos group caps somewhere around 10k–30k writes/s before the leader saturates on log-replication CPU and serialisation. The naive “one Paxos group for the whole database” design dies the moment you cross that ceiling.
  • Next-step solution — shard the database into many independent Paxos groups (Spanner’s directory model: each directory is one group; the database is millions of directories). Cross-directory transactions become 2PC over Paxos groups — an order of magnitude more expensive than single-directory writes, which is why the splitting heuristic prefers keeping related data co-located.
  • Rough numbers (for the worked example — balance-store) —
    • CP topology: 5 tasks per Paxos group; 16 GiB RAM, 1 TiB SSD, 8 vCPU per task. Single-group ceiling ~20k QPS write, ~200k QPS local read. Cross-region write latency ~70 ms.
    • AP topology: RF=3 per region, 9 nodes globally; same per-node footprint. Single-node ceiling ~50k QPS write (no consensus), ~200k QPS read. Local write latency ~5 ms.
  • At 10× current load —
    • CP: split each directory into 10 smaller directories; routing layer (Spanner’s zonemaster) directs reads/writes by key range. Cost: more cross-directory transactions, more metadata churn during splits.
    • AP: add 30 more nodes (add capacity to the ring); rebalance via consistent hashing. Cost: a Merkle-tree repair window during which both ring shapes are valid.
  • At 100× current load —
    • CP: directories now in the millions; cross-region link bandwidth becomes the cost driver, not CPU. You start splitting placement policies (some directories CP within one region only, replicating async to others) — you’re moving toward per-directory CAP choices.
    • AP: hot partitions dominate; you start salting and secondary-partitioning hot keys; ring-membership gossip overhead at ~1000 nodes becomes a workload itself.

§12Alternatives and when to pick each

AlternativeWhen it winsDisqualifying constraint
CP — Spanner / Chubby / F1 Correctness must hold across the partition: balances, locks, leader election, schema metadata, scheduler state. Reads & writes are infrequent enough that ~100 ms cross-region latency is tolerable. You can’t afford the minority-side unavailability window (e.g. ad-impression counters where a 30-s outage costs millions).
AP — Bigtable replication / Cassandra-style Latency & availability dominate correctness: sessions, carts, view counts, feeds, analytics events. Conflicts are merge-able (LWW or CRDT) or rare enough to handle at the app layer. Hard-correctness invariants you can’t weaken (no “double-spend allowed”), or counters that need exact arithmetic without CRDT discipline.
Per-operation CAP — Spanner stale-read / Cassandra tunable CL / Cosmos DB One store covers both kinds of workload: write strong, read mostly stale; or use CL=ALL for billing rows and CL=ONE for browsing. Cuts the “two databases” tax. Team can’t reliably remember which call sites use which level; reviewing every PR for CL discipline becomes load-bearing. Default-to-strong tends to drift to default-to-weak under cost pressure.
Single-region only (CA — not real CAP) You really have one DC, no replication, partitions are a property of inter-DC links you don’t have. (Famously: not a real engineering option above “single-AZ” scale.) Any availability requirement that survives the loss of that DC.
Default for the common case. For a typical web backend — most pages tolerant of staleness, a handful of pages (payment, lock, leader) not — the right move is per-operation CAP on a CP store: Spanner with bounded-staleness read flags for the read-heavy pages and strong reads/writes for the correctness-critical paths. The opposite layering (CP store as the source of truth, AP cache in front) is the other common-and-correct configuration, used at every internet-scale company; the key discipline is invalidating the cache on leader epoch change so the cache never serves data the leader has rolled back.

§13Classic mistakes & traps

  • “We chose CA.” — CA is not a configuration; partitions are a property of any distributed deployment. The seductive-but-broken pattern: a master-backup Postgres with synchronous replication and no quorum — looks like CA on day one, blocks all writes the first time the WAN flaps and is unprovably split-brain after the failover.
  • “Eventual consistency is good enough.” — until it isn’t. The pattern: an AP store backing a counter (likes, balances). The seduction: low write latency, no quorum cost. The break: lost increments under partition because the merge rule is LWW and concurrent writes silently overwrite. The fix is type-level (G-Counter CRDT), not a configuration knob.
  • “Spanner is CA, not CP.” — a famous Google internal claim. The argument is that Spanner’s SLO for availability is so high that operationally it behaves as if it were CA. The trap for engineers: confusing the SLO claim with the protocol property. Spanner is CP; under a real partition, the minority side genuinely refuses writes. Build your fallback assuming partitions exist.
  • Synchronous distributed lock with Redis SETNX — looks atomic, looks like a CP primitive, isn’t. The seductive line “Redis is single-threaded so it’s consistent” ignores Redis replication’s asynchronous default — a partition lets two clients each hold the lock from two different replicas. Fencing tokens (see fencing-tokens primitive) close the gap.
  • Treating CAP as system-wide rather than per-operation — the choice is made at the call site, not at the database-installation phase. The trap: a team picks “we’re a CP shop” and pays Spanner-style write latencies on the recommendations endpoint where they didn’t need to. The fix: per-endpoint consistency requirements driven by the bug-class you can’t tolerate, not a default.

§15Production examples

  • Spanner (CP, global) — the canonical CP-at-Google example. Each directory is one Paxos group across a region triplet; reads from any replica via TrueTime-bounded snapshot timestamps. Deviation from textbook CP: TrueTime makes cross-region read latency cheap, only paying the CP cost on writes (commit-wait of TrueTime uncertainty + Paxos round-trips). Reference: Spanner OSDI 2012, F1 paper.
  • Chubby (CP, per-cell) — a 5-task Paxos group per DC, holding small slow-changing data (locks, configs, leader-election ephemerals). Deviation: hard cap on use cases — explicitly “no high-throughput data here”; the < 1 MB-per-file limit and lease semantics enforce the boundary. Reference: Burrows 2006.
  • Bigtable (per-row strong, cross-cluster AP) — within a cluster, single-row writes are strongly consistent; across clusters (replication), eventual. Deviation: most users never see the CAP choice because they use one cluster; the moment they enable replication, the AP semantics are on by default — a common bug source.
  • Apache Cassandra (AP, tunable per-op) — the open-source counterpoint to Bigtable replication. Per-operation consistency level (ONE / QUORUM / ALL / LOCAL_QUORUM / EACH_QUORUM) makes the CAP choice visible at the call site. Deviation: EACH_QUORUM writes are a CP-ish synchronous-cross-region pattern that most ops accidentally configure and then can’t scale.
  • DynamoDB (AP → per-op CP) — originally pure AP (Dynamo paper 2007); now supports strongly-consistent reads as an option. Deviation: even “strong” reads are only linearizable per-key, not across keys — cross-key transactions require the separate Transactions API.

Comprehension probes — CAP

Integration-layer questions, not algorithm-recall ones.

  1. Integration probe. You front a Spanner directory with a Memcacheg cache. A 90-second partition isolates one region; the Spanner side correctly refuses minority-side writes, but users in that region keep reading from the local Memcacheg. What bug class are you about to ship to production, and which single design change closes it?
  2. Per-op CAP probe. Your team chose Cassandra with CL=QUORUM as the default for everything. You measure write latency at p99 = 280 ms — way above the 50 ms budget. What’s the likely cause, and what’s the per-endpoint refactor that gets you back under budget without losing the correctness on the endpoints that need it?
  3. Confusion probe. The on-call says “the Raft cluster is unavailable.” You look at the dashboard: heartbeats are flowing, leader is stable, but writes are rejected with quorum_unreachable. Is this a CAP-CP behaviour, an FLP behaviour, or a Chubby lease-expiry behaviour — and which one log line nails it?
  4. Reverse-drill (1 of 4). Two short scenarios. (a) Your collaborative document editor stops accepting edits when the user’s laptop goes offline. (b) Your billing service returns 503 when one of three regions is unreachable. Which is fundamentally the same pattern as CAP, which isn’t, and why? Hint: where is the partition, and what does “available” mean for each user?
